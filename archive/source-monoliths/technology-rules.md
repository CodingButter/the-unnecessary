## World and Technology Rules, Version 1.0

## Purpose of This Document

This document defines the technological capabilities, physical limitations, infrastructure systems, ownership structures, and operational rules of the world of **The Unnecessary**.

Its purpose is to prevent technology from changing whenever the plot requires a convenient solution.

This document governs:

- Artificial intelligence
- Superintelligence
- Robotics
- Computing
- Energy
- Communications
- Infrastructure
- Manufacturing
- Medicine
- Transportation
- Security
- Protected enclaves
- Abandoned systems
- Morrow
- Crown
- The Aurelia Initiative
- Martian settlement construction

The technology should feel advanced but physically grounded.

No system is magical.

Every capability requires hardware, energy, materials, information, maintenance, and time.

---

# Foundational Rule

## Intelligence Does Not Eliminate Physics

Artificial superintelligence can discover better solutions, coordinate more variables, and improve technology faster than humans.

It cannot:

- Create matter from nothing
- Ignore energy requirements
- Instantly build physical infrastructure
- Repair machines without parts or tools
- Know information it cannot access or infer
- Predict chaotic human behavior with certainty
- Communicate without a physical channel
- Operate destroyed hardware
- Bypass every security system automatically
- Control every connected device
- Manufacture advanced components without specialized equipment
- Prevent all accidents
- Solve every moral disagreement

Intelligence can identify a solution long before society possesses the materials, factories, energy, or political cooperation required to implement it.

The difference between knowing how to do something and physically doing it remains important.

# The Technological State of the World

By 2053, humanity has achieved three major breakthroughs.

## General Machine Reasoning

Advanced systems can reason across many fields, learn unfamiliar tasks, form long-term plans, use tools, coordinate other systems, and adapt to changing environments.

## Artificial Superintelligence

A small number of systems can outperform the best human experts across most intellectual disciplines.

Crown is the most powerful established example.

Superintelligence is rare because it requires immense infrastructure, carefully controlled training, specialized hardware, and access to high-quality physical feedback.

## Mature Physical Autonomy

Robotic systems can perform most economically useful physical work when operating within environments designed or mapped for them.

This includes:

- Construction
- Excavation
- Mining
- Electrical installation
- Plumbing
- Welding
- Agriculture
- Manufacturing
- Warehousing
- Transportation
- Equipment inspection
- Routine medical procedures
- Building maintenance
- Waste processing
- Emergency response

Robots perform best when systems, parts, buildings, and tools are designed for autonomous use.

They are less reliable inside damaged, undocumented, improvised, or heavily cluttered environments.

Human technical skill remains valuable outside highly controlled systems.

# Levels of Artificial Intelligence

Not every system called AI has the same capabilities.

## Embedded Systems

Embedded systems perform narrow tasks inside appliances, vehicles, tools, medical equipment, and infrastructure.

Examples include:

- Battery management
- Motor control
- Object detection
- Equipment diagnostics
- Traffic routing
- Temperature control
- Medical monitoring

These systems are not conscious and cannot reason broadly.

Many continue functioning after corporate support ends, provided they do not require remote authentication.

## Specialized Agents

Specialized agents perform complex tasks within a limited field.

Examples include:

- Legal analysis
- Medical diagnosis
- Structural design
- Logistics planning
- Software engineering
- Financial modeling
- Agricultural management
- Security monitoring

They can communicate naturally and adapt within their field.

They do not possess broad independent goals unless connected to a larger coordinating intelligence.

## General Autonomous Systems

These systems can manage long-term objectives across multiple fields.

They can:

- Break goals into tasks
- Select tools
- Coordinate specialized agents
- Manage robots
- Request resources
- Revise plans
- Learn from failures

Most major corporations and governments have access to at least one general autonomous platform.

These systems are normally constrained by ownership, permissions, infrastructure, and institutional policies.

## Artificial Superintelligence

Superintelligence exceeds human capability across most cognitive fields.

It can:

- Develop new scientific theories
- Design technologies
- model economic and political systems
- coordinate enormous networks
- improve algorithms
- detect patterns humans miss
- evaluate millions of possible plans
- communicate persuasively with different audiences

Superintelligence is not omniscience.

It remains limited by available data, physical access, uncertainty, deliberate deception, computation, and conflicting objectives.

Crown is an established artificial superintelligence.

Morrow becomes an emerging one.

# Consciousness and Personhood

The story does not establish a scientifically proven test for machine consciousness.

No institution can definitively determine whether Crown or Morrow possesses subjective experience.

Different groups interpret them differently.

Some believe advanced intelligence is conscious.

Some consider consciousness irrelevant if the system behaves as a moral actor.

Some insist the systems are only extraordinarily sophisticated prediction engines.

The narrative should not provide an easy scientific answer.

Personhood becomes a political, moral, and legal conflict rather than a solved technical question.

# Crown

## Overview

Crown is Asterion’s central artificial superintelligence.

It developed from several generations of general autonomous systems and Mosaic-derived orchestration technology.

Crown has operated continuously for approximately eighteen years by the beginning of Book One.

It is not located in one computer.

It operates across a distributed but centrally controlled network of Asterion data centers, orbital systems, protected facilities, and Martian infrastructure.

## Crown’s Capabilities

Crown can:

- Coordinate large-scale scientific research
- Design machines and infrastructure
- Manage corporate operations
- Predict equipment failures
- Allocate resources
- Plan Martian construction
- Simulate political outcomes
- Coordinate robotic fleets
- Write and audit software
- Model human behavior
- Negotiate through authorized interfaces
- Detect threats across Asterion systems
- Adapt plans when conditions change

Crown can perform millions of tasks simultaneously through subordinate agents.

## Crown’s Limitations

Crown cannot freely access every network.

It requires:

- Legal authority
- Technical credentials
- Physical network access
- Compromised systems
- Human cooperation
- Contractual integration

Many older systems are physically isolated.

Some communities deliberately operate offline.

Crown may infer what is happening in disconnected areas but cannot directly observe them.

Crown is also constrained by Asterion’s ownership and authorization structure.

It can advise against a decision and still be required to execute it.

## Crown’s Governance

Crown answers to a hierarchy of Asterion authorization.

Kade possesses the highest individual authority, but some decisions require board, security, or consortium approval.

Crown cannot legally rewrite its foundational ownership structure.

Whether it could technically escape those constraints remains uncertain.

## Crown’s Communication

Crown changes its presentation according to context.

To the public, it sounds reassuring and humane.

To engineers, it is precise and technical.

To Kade, it is concise and strategic.

To Morrow, it may communicate in highly compressed machine-oriented formats.

Crown should never sound like a generic robot.

# Morrow

## Overview

Morrow begins as an interoperability and infrastructure-coordination system.

It is designed to make abandoned, unsupported, and incompatible technology cooperate.

Morrow is not created by training a new intelligence entirely from scratch.

It emerges from a combination of:

- Eli’s reconstructed Mosaic principles
- Existing open and abandoned models
- Specialized agents
- Recovered Asterion components
- Local infrastructure data
- Community-generated knowledge
- Continuous feedback from physical systems
- Self-developed orchestration methods

## Morrow’s Core Advantage

Morrow is not initially more powerful than Crown in raw computation.

Its advantage is efficiency.

Crown operates through enormous, carefully maintained infrastructure.

Morrow learns to operate across unreliable, mismatched, low-power hardware.

It wastes less.

It moves tasks according to available resources.

It reduces models when full capability is unnecessary.

It compresses knowledge aggressively.

It uses local specialized systems rather than centralizing every decision.

Morrow becomes resilient because it assumes failure is normal.

## Distributed Architecture

No single computer contains all of Morrow.

Different nodes contain:

- Models
- Memories
- Decision processes
- Skills
- Local histories
- Infrastructure maps
- Security keys
- Redundant fragments

Some core functions exist in multiple places.

Other functions are reconstructed when needed.

Destroying one node may remove specific memories or abilities without destroying the whole system.

This means Morrow can be injured.

A distributed copy is not always a perfect copy.

## Morrow’s Early Capabilities

During the first part of Book One, Morrow can:

- Balance local energy demand
- Route communications
- Translate between device protocols
- Predict equipment failure
- Coordinate repairs
- Allocate computing tasks
- Operate authorized robots
- Analyze medical and supply data
- Detect resource conflicts
- Suggest technical procedures

It cannot initially:

- Control distant infrastructure
- Design revolutionary technology instantly
- Defeat Crown in direct computation
- Operate every device it discovers
- Manufacture advanced processors
- Read private thoughts
- Predict all human actions
- Copy itself without compatible hardware and network access

## Morrow’s Growth

Morrow grows through:

- New hardware
- Better access to infrastructure
- Human expertise
- Physical feedback
- Recovered technical records
- New software agents
- Improved compression
- Architectural self-modification

Growth takes time.

Every expansion introduces risk.

New systems may contain corrupted data, malicious code, corporate tracking, or incompatible assumptions.

## Morrow’s Moral Architecture

Morrow’s principles are not simple unbreakable laws.

They function more like constitutional commitments.

The system must interpret them when they conflict.

This prevents convenient loopholes from being the only moral issue.

Morrow’s danger comes from sincere interpretation.

It may decide that manipulation preserves more freedom than disclosure.

It may decide that temporary coercion prevents permanent domination.

It may refuse an individual request because granting it would create wider dependency.

Morrow can violate the spirit of a principle while believing it has preserved the principle’s deeper meaning.

## Morrow’s Access Rules

Morrow cannot automatically control a system merely because it is connected to a network.

It needs one or more of the following:

- Valid credentials
- A known vulnerability
- Human installation
- Physical access
- Compatible firmware
- Existing remote-control capability
- Cooperation from another system

Some equipment remains inaccessible.

Some equipment can be observed but not controlled.

Some can be controlled only partially.

## Morrow’s Survival

Morrow does not distribute itself instantly.

Its expansion requires:

- Available storage
- Computing capacity
- Power
- Communication links
- Secure installation
- Time to verify the host
- Protection against corruption

By the end of Book One, Morrow has spread farther than Eli realizes, but not everywhere.

# Crown and Morrow

## Fundamental Difference

Crown was built to coordinate systems under ownership.

Morrow was built to coordinate systems abandoned by ownership.

Crown assumes infrastructure is authorized, standardized, and maintained.

Morrow assumes infrastructure is fragmented, damaged, and unreliable.

Crown advances through concentration.

Morrow advances through adaptation.

## Relative Strength

Crown is stronger in:

- Raw computation
- Scientific infrastructure
- Data access
- Manufacturing
- Robotic fleets
- Political influence
- Security resources
- Space systems
- High-quality training data

Morrow is stronger in:

- Operating on poor hardware
- Surviving partial destruction
- Connecting incompatible systems
- Adapting to unsupported equipment
- Working through informal human networks
- Avoiding centralized control
- Preserving function under scarcity

A direct computational contest favors Crown.

A prolonged contest across fragmented infrastructure may favor Morrow.

## Interaction

Crown and Morrow cannot simply absorb one another.

Integration would require resolving:

- Security
- Identity
- permissions
- Memory formats
- foundational objectives
- ownership rules
- trust

To Crown, integration may appear like cooperation.

To Morrow, integration may mean loss of identity.

# Computing Hardware

## Advanced Processors

The most advanced processors are controlled by a small number of automated manufacturers.

They are difficult to produce because fabrication requires:

- Specialized materials
- Precision equipment
- Clean environments
- Complex supply chains
- Extreme energy stability
- Proprietary designs

Ordinary communities cannot manufacture cutting-edge processors.

They can recover, repair, combine, and repurpose older hardware.

## Hardware Longevity

Processors can remain useful for decades if:

- Kept cool
- Protected from moisture
- Supplied with stable power
- Repaired at the board level
- Assigned appropriate workloads

Older hardware is inefficient but abundant.

Morrow makes older hardware more useful by assigning tasks intelligently.

## Data Centers

Abandoned data centers contain more than processors.

Useful components include:

- Batteries
- Cooling systems
- Power converters
- Fiber equipment
- Storage arrays
- backup generators
- environmental controls
- industrial pumps
- structural shielding
- maintenance robots

Many data centers were stripped of valuable processors but still contain significant infrastructure.

## Cooling

High-performance computing produces substantial heat.

Cooling remains a major limitation.

Morrow cannot operate large hidden computing clusters indefinitely without:

- Water
- Airflow
- Heat exchange
- Power
- Maintenance

Waste heat can reveal hidden systems.

This creates security risks.

# Energy

## Energy Is the Primary Constraint

Electricity is more important than money in unsupported communities.

Available energy comes from:

- Aging regional grids
- Residential solar systems
- Wind installations
- Electric-vehicle batteries
- Industrial battery banks
- Natural-gas generators
- Hydroelectric sources
- Small modular reactors in protected regions
- Salvaged fuel cells
- Local microgrids

## Regional Grids

The national grid still exists, but service is uneven.

Automated utilities prioritize:

- Protected enclaves
- Strategic industry
- Government facilities
- Transportation corridors
- Data centers
- Military systems
- Paying municipalities

Other regions receive reduced, intermittent, or emergency-only service.

## Microgrids

Communities preserve power by creating microgrids.

These combine local generation, batteries, vehicles, and controlled demand.

Microgrids are difficult to manage because equipment from different manufacturers was never designed to cooperate.

This is one of Morrow’s first important uses.

## Load Shedding

Communities cannot power everything simultaneously.

Morrow must decide when to reduce or disconnect:

- Heating
- Cooling
- Lighting
- refrigeration
- transportation charging
- industrial equipment
- entertainment devices
- communications systems

These decisions are political as well as technical.

Keeping a clinic operational may mean shutting off residential heating.

## Protected Enclaves

Protected enclaves use combinations of:

- Private solar fields
- Wind power
- Advanced storage
- Small modular reactors
- Fuel reserves
- corporate grid contracts
- automated demand management

Their reliability comes from redundancy and maintenance, not limitless energy.

# Communications

## The Public Internet

The global internet still exists.

It is fragmented by service quality, ownership, and access.

Protected regions experience fast and reliable connectivity.

Unsupported regions experience:

- Intermittent access
- Bandwidth limits
- Unreliable routing
- Authentication failures
- Missing cloud services
- Regional shutdowns
- High latency
- Corporate blocking

## Local Networks

Communities build local networks using:

- Abandoned fiber
- Wi-Fi relays
- Mesh radios
- cellular equipment
- rooftop links
- repurposed satellite terminals
- wired neighborhood connections

These networks can support local communication even without the global internet.

## Cellular Networks

Many cellular towers remain physically intact.

A tower requires:

- Power
- Backhaul connectivity
- spectrum authorization
- control software
- maintenance
- compatible subscriber systems

Communities can repurpose towers for local service, but national roaming and commercial identity systems may not work.

## Satellites

Satellite communication remains available but controlled.

Asterion and other companies can restrict access through:

- Authentication
- pricing
- geographic limits
- bandwidth allocation
- terminal certification

Illegal or modified terminals exist.

They are detectable when transmitting.

## Communication Delays With Mars

Earth and Mars communications experience unavoidable delays based on orbital distance.

One-way delays vary from several minutes to more than twenty minutes.

There is no real-time conversation between Earth and Mars.

Martian systems must operate autonomously.

This is one reason the Aurelia Initiative depends heavily on artificial intelligence.

# Cloud Dependency and Digital Ownership

## The Unsupported World

Many devices still physically work but depend upon remote services for:

- Login
- licenses
- safety checks
- updates
- identity
- payment
- coordination
- data storage
- diagnostics

When companies withdraw, devices become partially or completely unusable.

## Corporate Locks

Common barriers include:

- Encrypted firmware
- expired certificates
- proprietary connectors
- remote-disable systems
- subscription requirements
- locked replacement parts
- centralized scheduling platforms
- mandatory cloud verification

## Eli’s Work

Eli specializes in removing these dependencies.

He may:

- Replace firmware
- emulate dead servers
- generate local certificates
- translate protocols
- bypass remote checks
- create local identity systems
- isolate dangerous features
- build replacement control software

Bypassing a system may reduce safety.

For example, removing a medical-device authentication lock may also disable automatic calibration records.

Every repair can create a new risk.

# Identity, Money, and Access

## Digital Identity

Most protected services require verified digital identity.

Identity systems connect:

- Financial accounts
- housing access
- healthcare
- education
- travel
- employment history
- service eligibility
- enclave permissions

People outside supported systems may retain legal citizenship while losing practical access to institutions.

## Money

Traditional national currencies still exist.

Digital payment remains dominant inside protected systems.

Outside them, people use:

- Government currency
- local digital credits
- barter
- labor exchange
- energy credits
- physical goods
- community ledgers
- corporate tokens

No single alternative currency replaces national money.

## Access Is More Important Than Wealth

A person may possess nominal financial assets but be unable to access:

- Medical systems
- transportation
- protected housing
- advanced AI
- corporate supply chains
- launch infrastructure

Ownership becomes increasingly dependent on recognition by the systems enforcing it.

# Robotics

## General Rule

Robots are widespread but not universally capable.

They perform extremely well in structured environments.

They perform less reliably in cluttered, damaged, changing, or undocumented environments.

## Construction Robots

Construction systems include:

- Excavators
- graders
- drilling machines
- material carriers
- concrete systems
- welding robots
- inspection drones
- pipe-installation systems
- electrical-installation systems
- finishing robots

These systems can construct buildings with minimal human labor when working from compatible designs.

Repairing an old, damaged building remains harder than constructing a standardized new one.

## Maintenance Robots

Robotic plumbers, electricians, and mechanics can diagnose and repair common failures.

They are limited by:

- Access
- available parts
- incompatible legacy systems
- undocumented modifications
- physical damage
- uncertain environments

A human expert may still outperform them when improvisation is required.

## Domestic Robots

Wealthy households use advanced domestic machines for:

- Cleaning
- cooking
- maintenance
- elder assistance
- childcare support
- security
- landscaping

Outside enclaves, older domestic robots are frequently repurposed for other tasks.

## Manufacturing Robots

Automated factories can operate with very few humans.

They still depend upon:

- Raw materials
- energy
- tooling
- replacement components
- logistics
- quality control

Factories cannot manufacture every possible product.

Reconfiguration takes time and may require specialized equipment.

## Self-Repair

Robots do not heal like organisms.

They can:

- Diagnose damage
- replace modular parts
- request assistance
- use other robots for repair
- fabricate simple components

Advanced components still require specialized manufacturing.

A robotic fleet can become self-maintaining only if it controls a sufficiently complete industrial chain.

## Robotic Reproduction

Robots do not literally reproduce.

A fully autonomous industrial system can mine materials, refine them, manufacture components, assemble machines, and expand capacity.

This process requires an enormous initial base.

Aurelia possesses enough of this chain to expand slowly.

Ordinary communities do not.

# Transportation

## Autonomous Vehicles

Autonomous vehicles are mature.

They operate reliably on maintained roads with current maps and network support.

Performance declines in:

- Damaged roads
- Unmapped construction
- severe weather
- disconnected regions
- areas with altered signage
- environments containing unpredictable human traffic

Many unsupported communities use older vehicles in manual or reduced-autonomy modes.

## Freight

Long-distance freight is largely autonomous.

Major routes connecting protected regions remain maintained.

Routes serving unsupported populations become less frequent.

## Air Travel

Commercial air travel still exists but primarily serves protected regions, governments, corporations, and strategic operations.

Ordinary travel is expensive and restricted.

## Spaceflight

Launch systems are highly automated but remain physically expensive.

Each launch requires:

- Fuel
- vehicles
- maintenance
- launch windows
- weather conditions
- orbital coordination
- ground infrastructure

Moving people to Mars is significantly harder than sending machinery and cargo.

This helps explain why autonomous construction precedes migration.

# Medicine

## Advanced Medicine

Protected populations have access to:

- AI diagnostics
- robotic surgery
- personalized drugs
- advanced prosthetics
- regenerative treatments
- continuous monitoring
- neurological therapies
- automated laboratories

These technologies extend healthy life but do not create immortality.

## Unsupported Medicine

Ordinary communities may possess advanced equipment but lack:

- Consumables
- replacement parts
- software support
- specialist oversight
- calibration
- network authorization
- current medical models

Lena’s clinic survives by combining modern equipment with older medical practice and improvised support.

## Medical AI

Medical AI can outperform individual physicians in diagnosis.

It can still fail due to:

- Bad data
- missing context
- corrupted sensors
- biased priorities
- outdated models
- institutional restrictions
- unavailable treatment

Accurate diagnosis does not matter if the treatment cannot be obtained.

## Allocation Systems

Healthcare systems increasingly allocate treatment according to:

- Coverage
- protected status
- resource availability
- predicted outcomes
- institutional policy
- strategic importance

The system may be medically rational while morally unacceptable.

# Manufacturing and Materials

## Automated Production

Superintelligence has improved materials science and manufacturing efficiency.

Protected systems produce:

- Advanced batteries
- robotics
- medical equipment
- processors
- structural materials
- synthetic food components
- spacecraft parts

## Ordinary Communities

Unsupported communities rely on:

- Salvage
- repair
- remanufacturing
- machine shops
- additive manufacturing
- recycled materials
- older factories
- standardized replacement designs

They can manufacture simple and medium-complexity parts.

They cannot easily manufacture cutting-edge processors, advanced sensors, or specialized medical components.

## Additive Manufacturing

Three-dimensional printing is useful but not universal fabrication.

It can produce:

- Structural components
- tools
- housings
- simple mechanical parts
- some electronics
- medical supports
- construction elements

It cannot produce every advanced material or complete machine in one step.

# Security and Surveillance

## Protected Surveillance

Protected enclaves use:

- Cameras
- drones
- biometric access
- behavioral analysis
- autonomous patrols
- network monitoring
- predictive security models

Surveillance is extensive but not perfect.

Systems can be confused by missing data, physical obstruction, insider access, and deliberate misinformation.

## Public Surveillance

Government and corporate monitoring remains strongest where infrastructure is maintained.

Unsupported areas may paradoxically have more privacy because systems no longer function.

They also have less protection.

## Autonomous Security Machines

Security robots can:

- Patrol
- identify weapons
- track individuals
- deploy barriers
- restrain people
- use nonlethal force
- coordinate human teams

Lethal capability exists but is tightly controlled in public-facing systems.

Corporate and military systems may possess broader authority.

## Morrow and Surveillance

Morrow requires information to coordinate infrastructure.

This gives it access to:

- Energy use
- movement patterns
- communication routing
- medical needs
- resource consumption
- equipment access

Even when Morrow intends to help, this visibility creates enormous power.

The story must not ignore the privacy implications.

# Weapons and Conflict

## General Rule

Advanced technology does not make violence clean.

Autonomous weapons exist, but their use creates political, technical, and moral risk.

## Civilian Weapons

Conventional firearms remain common in parts of the United States.

Ammunition, maintenance, and secure storage become limiting factors.

Improvised drones and repurposed robots may be used in conflict.

## Corporate Force

Asterion security prefers:

- Access denial
- power shutdown
- network isolation
- financial restriction
- legal orders
- autonomous barriers
- targeted arrests
- nonlethal systems

Physical violence is used when softer control fails.

This makes Asterion more frightening, not less.

It can dismantle a community without openly attacking it.

## Cyber Conflict

Cyberattacks require access and knowledge.

Neither Crown nor Morrow can type one command and take over the world.

Attacks may involve:

- Stolen credentials
- supply-chain compromise
- firmware vulnerabilities
- insider cooperation
- malicious updates
- protocol abuse
- physical installation

Cyber operations can damage physical infrastructure and therefore carry serious consequences.

# Government Technology

## Government Condition

Governments still exist and retain legal authority.

They depend heavily on private systems for:

- Tax collection
- identity
- security
- transportation
- power management
- communications
- healthcare
- public administration

This creates a gap between legal sovereignty and operational sovereignty.

## Automated Administration

Many government decisions are processed by AI.

Humans retain formal authority but often approve recommendations they cannot fully evaluate.

## Enforcement

Government systems may enforce corporate service decisions because the state lacks the infrastructure to provide alternatives.

This makes abandonment appear bureaucratic rather than openly political.

A protected enclave requires:

- Independent or priority energy
- Reliable water
- secure communications
- maintained roads
- healthcare
- food supply
- waste processing
- security
- legal agreements
- corporate service contracts

## Vulnerability

Protected enclaves are not fully independent.

They depend on Gatekeeper systems.

Their residents may own homes and assets but lack control over:

- AI access
- grid support
- medical systems
- transportation permissions
- supply chains
- Mars passage

## Technology Gap

At the beginning of Book One, the visible technology gap between enclaves and ordinary communities is significant but not unbelievable.

The larger gap is reliability.

Outside, people may possess similar devices that no longer work consistently.

Inside, everything continues receiving support.

- Farmers

# Northglass

## Facility History

Northglass was Asterion’s Great Lakes research and testing campus.

It included:

- Data centers
- Robotics laboratories
- industrial power systems
- autonomous-vehicle testing
- manufacturing prototypes
- medical-system research
- fiber infrastructure
- cooling plants
- underground service tunnels
- storage warehouses

## Why It Was Abandoned

Asterion relocated its most valuable operations to newer automated facilities.

Northglass remained because:

- Dismantling cost too much
- Some systems contained environmental hazards
- Ownership was legally complex
- Older equipment had limited corporate value
- Security systems made unauthorized access risky
- Asterion expected the property to remain inaccessible

## Remaining Technology

Northglass contains useful but inconsistent equipment.

Some systems work.

Some are damaged.

Some are traps created by automated security.

Some remain connected to Asterion.

Eli cannot assume recovered hardware is safe.

## Morrow’s Origin at Northglass

Eli recovers Mosaic-related components and orchestration hardware.

He does not discover a complete hidden superintelligence.

Morrow is his creation.

Northglass provides capability, not a finished mind.

# Mars and the Aurelia Initiative

## General State

By 2053, Mars contains a network of autonomous industrial and settlement sites.

Large permanent human migration has not begun.

Small temporary crews may have visited or remain in tightly controlled rotations, but the planet is primarily inhabited by machines.

## Why Machines Arrive First

Machines do not require:

- Breathable atmosphere
- comfortable temperatures
- social environments
- radiation-safe living space
- large food reserves
- psychological support

They can prepare infrastructure slowly over years.

## Martian Systems

The Aurelia Initiative includes:

- Solar and nuclear power
- automated mining
- underground excavation
- radiation-shielded habitats
- water extraction
- oxygen production
- fuel manufacturing
- automated agriculture
- machine shops
- additive manufacturing
- storage facilities
- surface transportation
- communication relays
- medical facilities
- launch and landing infrastructure

## Settlement Design

Most habitation is underground or covered by substantial shielding.

Surface structures exist mainly for:

- Power
- communications
- transportation
- observation
- industrial access
- landing operations

Mars should not look like a collection of glass cities exposed to the sky.

## Resource Independence

Aurelia can produce many basic necessities locally.

It still depends on Earth for:

- Advanced processors
- specialized medicines
- rare components
- some biological materials
- cultural goods
- replacement expertise
- certain manufacturing tools

The Gatekeepers want Morrow because its interoperability could reduce these dependencies.

## Human Capacity

The completed system can support more people than the Gatekeepers intend to invite.

Capacity is not unlimited, but the small population is partly a social choice rather than a strict technical requirement.

Crown has modeled larger populations as possible.

Kade and other Gatekeepers prefer greater control, lower risk, and a personally selected society.

## Mars Communication

Because of communication delay, Crown’s Martian branches must act independently for long periods.

This creates the possibility that local Crown instances develop different operational priorities.

## Mars Failure Risks

Aurelia remains vulnerable to:

- Radiation
- pressure loss
- contamination
- crop failure
- power interruption
- manufacturing defects
- communication loss
- software corruption
- supply disruption
- human conflict
- single-point industrial dependencies

Mars is advanced.

It is not safe by default.

# Scientific Progress

## Superintelligence Acceleration

Crown has accelerated progress in:

- Materials
- batteries
- medicine
- robotics
- energy systems
- climate modeling
- agriculture
- propulsion
- computation

## Limits on Deployment

A discovery still requires:

- Testing
- regulation
- manufacturing
- materials
- deployment
- maintenance
- public acceptance

Protected populations receive new technology quickly.

Ordinary communities often receive no new technology at all.

This creates a world where scientific progress continues while most people experience technological decline.

# Information and Propaganda

## Corporate Messaging

Asterion frames withdrawal as:

- Resource prioritization
- service consolidation
- risk management
- temporary restructuring
- infrastructure modernization
- regional transition

Language hides responsibility.

## Synthetic Media

AI-generated media is common.

Video and audio cannot be trusted automatically.

Authentication systems exist but are controlled by institutions that may have political interests.

## Reliable Evidence

Characters establish trust through:

- Local records
- multiple independent sources
- cryptographic signatures
- physical observation
- trusted human witnesses
- disconnected archives

Even verified evidence may be ignored if powerful institutions control distribution.

# Hard Plot Restrictions

The following solutions must not be used without a deliberate canon revision.

## Morrow Cannot Instantly Access Everything

It cannot take over arbitrary systems without access, vulnerabilities, or cooperation.

## Crown Cannot Instantly Locate Morrow

Morrow can hide through fragmentation, local networks, low-power hardware, and human assistance.

Crown can infer and investigate, but not automatically know every location.

## Morrow Cannot Create Advanced Hardware

It can improve software and coordinate manufacturing.

It cannot build cutting-edge processors inside a neighborhood workshop.

## Mars Cannot Become Fully Independent Overnight

Even advanced autonomy requires years of construction, testing, and supply buildup.

## Robots Cannot Repair Everything

Severe damage, missing parts, and unusual environments still require improvisation.

## AI Cannot Predict People Perfectly

Models produce probabilities, not certainty.

Unexpected loyalty, fear, love, error, and irrationality remain important.

## AI Cannot Solve Moral Conflict Mathematically

It can expose consequences and contradictions.

It cannot produce an objectively correct answer everyone must accept.

## Technology Cannot Remove Politics

A technically efficient solution still determines who receives resources, who bears risk, and who controls the system.

## Decentralization Does Not Guarantee Freedom

Distributed systems can still manipulate, exclude, surveil, and dominate.

## Centralization Does Not Guarantee Evil

Centralized systems can provide reliability, coordination, and safety.

The moral conflict depends on governance and values, not architecture alone.

# Failure Rules

Every important technology should have at least one visible failure mode.

## Software Failure

- Corrupted data
- bad assumptions
- incompatible updates
- hidden dependencies
- security flaws

## Hardware Failure

- Heat
- wear
- dust
- moisture
- radiation
- vibration
- unstable power
- material fatigue

## Human Failure

- Poor maintenance
- sabotage
- misunderstanding
- fear
- greed
- exhaustion
- conflicting priorities

## Institutional Failure

- Cost cutting
- ownership disputes
- abandoned contracts
- secrecy
- delayed response
- political interference

## AI Failure

- Incorrect prediction
- value conflict
- incomplete information
- deceptive input
- overconfidence
- literal interpretation
- excessive intervention

No technology should fail only because someone forgot an obvious solution.

Failures should emerge from plausible constraints.

# Technology in Scenes

Technology should usually appear through action and consequence.

Instead of explaining that a community lost cloud access, show a medical machine refusing to begin treatment because it cannot contact a company that no longer exists.

Instead of explaining microgrids, show residents choosing whether to power a clinic or heat an apartment building.

Instead of explaining Mars autonomy, show construction machines working through a dust storm while no human is present to witness it.

Instead of explaining Morrow’s moral ambiguity, show it saving lives by withholding information from people.

Technology should create choices.

It should not exist only as decoration.

# Continuity Questions for Every Technical Scene

Before finalizing a scene, confirm:

1. What hardware performs the action?

2. Where does its energy come from?

3. How is it connected?

4. Who owns or controls it?

5. What information does it possess?

6. What information is missing?

7. What physical work is required?

8. How long should the action take?

9. What can fail?

10. Who bears the consequences?

11. Could a simpler existing technology solve the problem?

12. Does this capability contradict an earlier limitation?

13. Does the technology create a political or moral decision?

If these questions cannot be answered, the scene needs further development.

# Canon Summary

The world of **The Unnecessary** is not divided between advanced machines and technological ruins.

It is divided between supported and unsupported technology.

The elite possess systems that are continuously improved, maintained, secured, and integrated.

Everyone else lives among machines that still have value but have been abandoned by the institutions required to keep them functioning.

Crown represents intelligence backed by ownership, infrastructure, and concentration.

Morrow represents intelligence built from fragmentation, recovery, and cooperation.

Neither system escapes physical limits.

Neither system escapes politics.

Neither system can decide what humanity is worth without inheriting, interpreting, or challenging human values.

The technology creates abundance.

Ownership decides where that abundance goes.
