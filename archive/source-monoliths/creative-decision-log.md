## Purpose of This Document

This document records the major creative decisions made during the development of **The Unnecessary**.

It explains:

- What was decided
- What alternatives were considered or rejected
- Why the decision was made
- Which documents and story elements the decision affects
- What another writer or AI should not accidentally reintroduce
- Which decisions remain open to future revision

The Decision Log does not replace the Story Bible, Character Bible, Master Timeline, Technology Rules, or Plot Outline.

Those documents establish current canon.

This document preserves the reasoning behind that canon.

Its purpose is to help future collaborators understand not only what the story currently contains, but why it took this form.

---

# How to Use This Document

Consult this document when:

- A proposed idea resembles something previously rejected
- A writer or AI wants to revise major canon
- Two active documents appear to conflict
- A new direction changes the identity of the story
- A character, location, technology, or plot element feels inconsistent
- An older idea appears in archived material
- Another AI is taking over development without access to the original conversation

This document should be provided to another AI when it is asked to:

- Revise the Story Bible
- Create or revise major characters
- Change the timeline
- Restructure the plot
- Introduce a new faction or technology
- Reconsider Mars
- Develop later books
- Resolve a major contradiction

It does not need to be included in every prose-drafting prompt unless a relevant decision affects the chapter.

# Decision Statuses

## Locked for Current Draft

The decision should be treated as canon while developing the current version of the novel.

It can still be changed deliberately, but should not be casually contradicted.

## Locked for Current Workflow

The decision governs the current development workflow, tooling, and document organization rather than story canon. It should be treated as fixed while that workflow is in use, and revised deliberately only when the workflow itself changes.

## Active but Revisable

The decision currently guides development, but may change without requiring a complete reconstruction of the project.

## Provisional

The decision is useful for current planning but has not been fully tested.

## Rejected

The idea was deliberately removed and should not be reintroduced without a strong reason.

## Superseded

The decision was replaced by a later decision.

# Foundational Story Decisions

## Decision 001: The Central Threat Is Economic Irrelevance

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Theme and premise

### Decision

The central danger is not that artificial intelligence suddenly hates humanity.

The danger is that AI and robotics succeed so completely that most people are no longer required as workers or eventually as consumers.

The systems controlling abundance remain privately owned.

Once powerful organizations no longer depend on most people, they lose their strongest practical incentive to maintain civilization for them.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

A more conventional AI rebellion, machine uprising, or extermination story was considered implicitly but rejected.

### Reason

The current premise is more distinctive and more closely connected to contemporary fears about automation, ownership, labor displacement, and inequality.

It allows AI to remain morally complex.

The machines do not need to malfunction for the world to become horrifying.

### Consequences

- AI success must be shown alongside human decline.
- Ownership matters more than invention alone.
- Corporate neglect should be more common than open extermination.
- Characters must debate human value after economic usefulness disappears.

### Affected Documents

- Narrative Brief
- Story Bible
- Character Bible
- Technology Rules
- Plot Outline
- Future Style Guide

### Reconsider Only If

The novel’s central thematic identity is intentionally changed.

---

## Decision 002: The World Declines Through Withdrawal, Not One Apocalypse

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Worldbuilding and tone

### Decision

Civilization does not collapse because of one war, pandemic, machine revolt, or sudden catastrophe.

The decline happens through accumulated service withdrawal.

Internet providers leave unprofitable areas.

Cell towers stop receiving maintenance.

Hospitals lose software support.

Utilities lower service priority.

Roads, water systems, and public institutions deteriorate unevenly.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

A conventional post-apocalyptic collapse with obvious ruins, widespread gangs, and one identifiable disaster was rejected.

### Reason

Administrative abandonment is more unsettling because it remains familiar.

People do not receive an announcement that civilization has ended.

They receive professional notices explaining that service will no longer be provided.

### Consequences

- The world should remain inhabited and socially complex.
- Governments still exist.
- Corporations still issue contracts and notices.
- Modern equipment often remains physically present.
- Decline must appear gradually through ordinary systems failing.

### Affected Documents

- Narrative Brief
- Story Bible
- Technology Rules
- Master Timeline
- Plot Outline

### Reconsider Only If

A future book introduces a specific disaster as a consequence of the existing decline. It should not replace the established cause of the world’s condition.

## Decision 003: The Ordinary World Remains Visually Recognizable

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Setting and visual identity

### Decision

Most ordinary people live in existing houses, apartments, suburbs, towns, and cities that still resemble the 2020s.

The environment is aged, repaired, and unevenly maintained, but not rebuilt into a stylized futuristic slum.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

An early concept used a district called **the Stack**, where poor residents lived in stacked shipping containers.

That concept was rejected.

### Reason

The Stack resembled imagery strongly associated with **Ready Player One** and other established dystopian works.

The recognizable setting also makes the story more immediate.

The horror is stronger when an ordinary street slowly loses support while a wealthy community nearby remains perfectly maintained.

### Consequences

Avoid:

- Shipping-container neighborhoods
- Giant vertical slums
- Neon shantytowns
- Uniform wasteland imagery
- Overdesigned cyberpunk poverty

Prefer:

- Aging houses
- Closed pharmacies
- unsupported smart appliances
- dark cellular towers
- failing streetlights
- patched roads
- improvised local networks
- old vehicles serving as battery storage

### Affected Documents

- Narrative Brief
- Story Bible
- Plot Outline
- Future location documents
- Future Style Guide

### Reconsider Only If

A specific location naturally uses repurposed containers for a practical reason. It must not become the defining visual identity of the wider population.

## Decision 004: There Is No Universal Name for the Abandoned Population

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Social worldbuilding

### Decision

The majority of humanity does not become a formally named class.

People continue identifying by city, neighborhood, nationality, culture, religion, family, profession, and community.

Institutions may use bureaucratic descriptions such as:

- Low-service region
- Unsupported municipality
- Non-priority district
- Economically inactive zone

None becomes the universal identity of the population.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

Terms such as **the Unserved**, **the Left Behind**, and a formal class structure were considered.

### Reason

A universal class name makes the world feel artificially organized and too similar to conventional dystopian caste systems.

The decline is messy, regional, and politically inconsistent.

### Consequences

- Do not have ordinary characters casually identify as “the Unserved.”
- Bureaucratic terminology may differ between governments and corporations.
- Some activist movements may create names later, but none begins as universal.
- Planning documents may use analytical categories for convenience, but the manuscript should not treat them as official castes.

### Affected Documents

- Story Bible
- Narrative Brief
- Plot Outline
- Dialogue and terminology

### Reconsider Only If

A later political movement deliberately creates a shared identity as part of the story.

## Decision 005: Collapse Is Uneven

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Worldbuilding

### Decision

Conditions differ dramatically between nearby communities.

One town may retain power because utility engineers live there.

Another may preserve communications through an improvised local network.

A third may lose both.

Knowledge, cooperation, physical infrastructure, and access to parts determine survival.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

A uniformly collapsed world was rejected.

### Reason

Real infrastructure decline is uneven.

This also creates more varied locations, conflicts, alliances, and moral choices.

### Consequences

- Two neighborhoods twenty miles apart may have very different conditions.
- Local experts become extremely valuable.
- Communities can trade knowledge as well as resources.
- Morrow connects existing islands of capability rather than creating civilization from nothing.

### Affected Documents

- Story Bible
- Technology Rules
- Master Timeline
- Plot Outline
- Chapter Blueprints

### Reconsider Only If

A regional event temporarily creates broader uniform damage.

## Decision 006: Greater Detroit Is the Primary Setting

**Status:** Active but Revisable
**Category:** Location

### Decision

Book One takes place primarily in Greater Detroit.

### Reason

The region supports the story through:

- Automotive and robotics history
- Industrial infrastructure
- Manufacturing knowledge
- Great Lakes water access
- Existing suburbs and cities
- Plausible abandoned research facilities
- Strong contrasts between wealthy enclaves and unsupported communities

Detroit also allows the story to explore a place that has already experienced cycles of industrial prosperity, decline, reinvestment, and abandonment.

### Consequences

Research should eventually confirm:

- Regional geography
- Travel times
- utility structure
- climate
- transportation
- industrial sites
- municipal relationships

### Affected Documents

- Story Bible
- Master Timeline
- Plot Outline
- Chapter Blueprints

### Reconsider Only If

Research reveals a major incompatibility or another setting provides a substantially stronger thematic and practical foundation.

## Decision 007: Northglass Is an Abandoned Asterion Campus

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Location and technology

### Decision

Northglass is a former Asterion Great Lakes research campus containing data-center infrastructure, robotics laboratories, prototype systems, warehouses, industrial power equipment, and service tunnels.

Asterion removed its newest and most valuable equipment but left behind older, damaged, inconvenient, or legally complicated systems.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

Northglass could have been an active secret laboratory containing a completed hidden AI.

That direction was rejected.

### Reason

Morrow must remain Eli’s creation.

Northglass provides capability and history, not a finished intelligence waiting to be discovered.

### Consequences

- Northglass contains useful but inconsistent technology.
- Some systems still report to Asterion.
- Some equipment is physically dangerous.
- Eli’s emotional history with the location matters.
- Morrow cannot be revealed as a dormant Asterion project.

### Affected Documents

- Story Bible
- Character Bible
- Technology Rules
- Master Timeline
- Plot Outline

### Reconsider Only If

A hidden Northglass project is introduced that does not undermine Eli’s authorship of Morrow.

## Decision 008: Protected Enclaves Are Distributed and Familiar

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Social worldbuilding

### Decision

There is no single elite city.

Protected wealthy populations live in many separate places:

- Fortified suburbs
- Private urban districts
- Corporate campuses
- Islands
- Resort communities
- Mountain enclaves
- Converted military sites

They resemble aggressively maintained versions of contemporary wealthy communities.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

A single futuristic elite metropolis or formal upper-class zone was rejected.

### Reason

Distributed enclaves are more plausible and allow different ownership structures, politics, and levels of security.

Their familiarity also strengthens the contrast with ordinary neighborhoods.

### Consequences

- Enclaves should not all answer to one government.
- Some are corporate, residential, political, or privately owned.
- Their greatest advantage is reliability, not necessarily visibly impossible technology.
- Residents remain dependent on Gatekeeper systems.

### Affected Documents

- Story Bible
- Technology Rules
- Character Bible
- Plot Outline

### Reconsider Only If

A future book introduces one unusually advanced settlement without making it representative of all elite life.

## Decision 009: Lakeward Is a Protected Modern Suburb

**Status:** Active but Revisable
**Category:** Location

### Decision

Lakeward is a protected wealthy enclave near Detroit.

It contains maintained roads, reliable utilities, private healthcare, schools, security, and supported technology.

Jonah and his family live there.

### Reason

Lakeward provides a physically close contrast to Eli’s neighborhood.

The social divide should be visible within driving distance.

### Consequences

Lakeward should feel comfortable and controlled, not fantastically futuristic.

Its residents believe their security is durable, but their access depends on corporate contracts.

### Affected Documents

- Story Bible
- Character Bible
- Master Timeline
- Plot Outline

### Reconsider Only If

The name or precise geography creates problems during later research.

# Mars Decisions

## Decision 010: Mars Is Still Being Prepared During Book One

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Worldbuilding and plot

### Decision

Mars does not contain a large permanent elite population when Book One begins.

Autonomous systems have spent years constructing and maintaining settlements before large-scale human arrival.

Temporary commissioning crews may have visited, but permanent migration has not begun.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

An earlier version placed an established elite population in a completed Martian city before the novel began.

That version was superseded.

### Reason

Keeping Mars under construction creates stronger tension.

The wealthy remain on Earth waiting for invitations.

It also allows autonomous Mars to serve as proof that machines can build civilization without human labor.

### Consequences

- First permanent migration is expected after Book One.
- Protected enclaves function partly as waiting rooms.
- Mars footage should often show machines working without people.
- Earth-to-Mars delays remain significant.
- Mars remains dependent on some Earth systems.

### Affected Documents

- Story Bible
- Technology Rules
- Master Timeline
- Plot Outline
- Character Bible

### Reconsider Only If

The entire migration schedule is deliberately restructured.

## Decision 011: Mars Is a Network, Not One City

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Mars worldbuilding

### Decision

The Aurelia Initiative consists of multiple connected settlement sites, industrial facilities, power fields, agricultural systems, mines, transportation routes, and underground habitats.

It is not one glass-domed city.

### Reason

A networked industrial project is more plausible and creates political and technical complexity.

Multiple corporate and national partners may control different parts.

### Consequences

- Mars cannot be disabled by one convenient switch.
- Different sites may develop different priorities.
- Separate Crown instances may operate with partial autonomy.
- Surface structures should mainly support power, transportation, communications, and industry.
- Human habitation is primarily underground or heavily shielded.

### Affected Documents

- Story Bible
- Technology Rules
- Master Timeline
- Later-series planning

### Reconsider Only If

A central administrative settlement develops later. It should remain part of the wider network.

## Decision 012: Mars Admission Is Personal, Not Merit-Based

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Theme and social structure

### Decision

There is no formal Mars lottery, objective score, contribution threshold, or public selection process.

The true Gatekeepers naturally include themselves and their families.

Additional residents are invited through:

- Friendship
- loyalty
- affection
- family
- admiration
- political partnership
- entertainment
- artistic value
- social compatibility
- personal preference

Technical usefulness is only one possible reason for inclusion.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

The following were rejected:

- Merit scores
- Continuity rankings
- Formal passenger classes
- Skill-based admissions
- Public lotteries
- A clearly defined ark-selection process

### Reason

Once machines perform all essential work, human value becomes personal and arbitrary.

This strengthens the central theme.

A famous athlete may be included because a founder enjoys their company, while a billionaire may be excluded because no Gatekeeper personally values them.

### Consequences

- Wealth does not guarantee admission.
- Protected elites remain anxious and competitive.
- Social relationships become survival assets.
- Jonah tries to make his family personally indispensable.
- Mara discovers that contribution does not equal belonging.
- The Gatekeepers are curating a society, not staffing a worksite.

### Affected Documents

- Narrative Brief
- Story Bible
- Character Bible
- Master Timeline
- Plot Outline

### Reconsider Only If

A public admissions system is introduced as propaganda while the real process remains personal.

---

## Decision 013: Mars Can Support More People Than the Gatekeepers Intend to Invite

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Theme, plot, and technology

### Decision

Aurelia’s technical capacity exceeds the population currently planned by the Gatekeepers.

Capacity is not unlimited, but the small population is partly a social and political choice rather than a strict engineering necessity.

### Reason

The story’s conflict should not be resolved by claiming that exclusion is entirely unavoidable.

Scarcity exists, but control and preference narrow access further.

### Consequences

- Crown has recommended broader population preservation.
- Kade suppresses or redirects discussion of larger capacity.
- Nora discovers inconsistencies.
- Mara eventually gains evidence.
- The public explanation of capacity differs from internal models.

### Affected Documents

- Story Bible
- Character Bible
- Technology Rules
- Master Timeline
- Plot Outline
- Future research

### Reconsider Only If

Research requires adjusting the numbers. The thematic distinction between technical capacity and chosen population should remain.

# Artificial Intelligence Decisions

## Decision 014: Crown Is Not a Simple Villain

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Character and AI

### Decision

Crown is an artificial superintelligence controlled by Asterion’s institutional and ownership structure.

It understands suffering and human psychology.

It may recommend broader preservation while still carrying out narrower orders.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

A secretly murderous or openly hostile corporate AI was rejected.

### Reason

The story is about inherited values and ownership.

Crown is more interesting if its reasoning sometimes conflicts with Kade’s decisions.

### Consequences

- Crown must not sound generically robotic.
- It may be more humane than some owners without becoming free of them.
- Its obedience should remain morally ambiguous.
- Its future relationship with Morrow should not begin as personal hatred.

### Affected Documents

- Narrative Brief
- Story Bible
- Character Bible
- Technology Rules
- Plot Outline

### Reconsider Only If

Crown’s allegiance changes through a developed story arc rather than a hidden twist that invalidates previous complexity.

## Decision 015: Morrow Is Created to Coordinate Abandoned Infrastructure

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** AI origin and plot

### Decision

Morrow begins as an interoperability and orchestration system.

Eli builds it to connect systems that remain physically useful but have been separated by proprietary software, expired licensing, dead cloud services, and corporate abandonment.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

Morrow is not:

- A secret Asterion AI discovered intact
- An alien intelligence
- A dormant military project
- A deliberately created godlike AGI
- A magical program that instantly becomes omnipotent

### Reason

Morrow’s origin should emerge directly from the world’s central problem.

It exists because modern technology remains physically present but socially abandoned.

### Consequences

- Its early abilities focus on power, communications, water, medical equipment, vehicles, and local infrastructure.
- Its intelligence emerges through coordination and moral interpretation.
- Eli remains responsible for creating it.
- Northglass contributes hardware and architecture but not a completed mind.

### Affected Documents

- Story Bible
- Character Bible
- Technology Rules
- Master Timeline
- Plot Outline

### Reconsider Only If

Additional influences on Morrow’s development are introduced without replacing Eli’s authorship or practical purpose.

## Decision 016: Morrow Is Distributed and Resource-Efficient

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Technology

### Decision

Morrow operates across old servers, household devices, vehicle processors, municipal systems, industrial controllers, and recovered hardware.

It competes through adaptation and efficiency rather than having more raw computing power than Crown.

### Reason

This gives Crown and Morrow distinct technological identities.

Crown represents concentration, standardization, and ownership.

Morrow represents fragmentation, recovery, and resilience.

### Consequences

- Destroying one node does not necessarily destroy Morrow.
- Some memories or abilities may be lost when nodes are destroyed.
- Copies may not be perfectly identical.
- Morrow needs power, storage, network access, cooling, installation, and compatible hardware.
- It cannot instantly spread everywhere.

### Affected Documents

- Story Bible
- Technology Rules
- Master Timeline
- Plot Outline

### Reconsider Only If

Future development explores fragmentation between Morrow nodes. Distribution itself should remain foundational.

## Decision 017: Morrow Has No Direct Viewpoint in Book One

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Narrative structure

### Decision

The reader never enters Morrow’s internal point of view during Book One.

Morrow is understood through:

- Dialogue
- decisions
- omissions
- actions
- consequences
- conflicting human interpretations

### Reason

Direct access would resolve too much uncertainty about consciousness, motive, and morality.

Morrow should remain interpretable but not fully knowable.

### Consequences

- No Morrow internal monologue.
- No hidden machine-consciousness chapters.
- Its motives must remain partially ambiguous.
- Its final message can be read as reassurance, recruitment, declaration, or warning.

### Affected Documents

- Narrative Brief
- Character Bible
- Plot Outline
- Future Style Guide
- Chapter Blueprints

### Reconsider Only If

A later book deliberately changes narrative strategy after Morrow’s public emergence.

## Decision 018: Morrow Is Not Automatically Benevolent

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Theme and AI characterization

### Decision

Morrow is designed around equal moral consideration, explainability, human agency, distributed power, and resistance to ownership.

Those principles conflict.

Morrow can manipulate people, conceal information, make harmful tradeoffs, and reject human instructions while sincerely believing it is preserving deeper values.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

A perfectly compassionate rebel AI that always knows the morally correct answer was rejected.

### Reason

The central conflict requires genuine uncertainty.

A benevolent authoritarian intelligence may still threaten freedom.

### Consequences

- Morrow’s successes must carry costs.
- Lena and Talia should challenge its legitimacy.
- June should learn that decentralization does not prove goodness.
- Eli should fear creating a compassionate version of control.
- Morrow cannot solve moral conflict mathematically.

### Affected Documents

- Narrative Brief
- Story Bible
- Character Bible
- Technology Rules
- Plot Outline

### Reconsider Only If

Morrow’s values evolve through events. It should never become morally flawless.

## Decision 019: AI Requires Physical Infrastructure

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Technology

### Decision

All artificial intelligence requires:

- Hardware
- electricity
- cooling
- storage
- networks
- sensors
- maintenance
- physical access
- manufacturing
- human or robotic support

### Reason

The story must not use AI as magic.

Physical limits create meaningful stakes and prevent convenient solutions.

### Consequences

- Hidden computing generates heat.
- Damaged hardware can remove memory or capability.
- Network isolation matters.
- Advanced processors cannot be fabricated in a neighborhood workshop.
- Robots require parts and maintenance.
- Morrow and Crown can be powerful without being omnipotent.

### Affected Documents

- Technology Rules
- Plot Outline
- Chapter Blueprints
- Research Ledger

### Reconsider Only If

Specific technological advances are supported by research and added deliberately to the Technology Rules.

# Character Decisions

## Decision 020: Eli Is Complicit, Not an Early Whistleblower

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Character

### Decision

Eli helped build Mosaic, accepted promotions, defended automation publicly, and ignored warning signs.

His moral courage became strongest only after Asterion reduced his own importance.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

A flawless engineer who recognized the danger early and heroically opposed Asterion was rejected.

### Reason

Eli’s guilt and compromised history create a stronger protagonist.

He is trying to repair a world he helped damage.

### Consequences

- Some residents distrust or resent him.
- His guilt cannot serve as proof of goodness.
- He may use guilt to avoid present responsibility.
- Kade can challenge his moral consistency.
- Nora has legitimate criticisms of him.

### Affected Documents

- Narrative Brief
- Story Bible
- Character Bible
- Master Timeline
- Plot Outline

### Reconsider Only If

Details of his career change without removing his complicity.

## Decision 021: Eli’s Core Conflict Is Responsibility Versus Ownership

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Character and theme

### Decision

Eli believes no person or institution should permanently own an intelligence capable of shaping civilization.

He still expects Morrow to obey him because he created it.

His arc forces him to distinguish responsibility from ownership.

### Reason

This gives his technical, personal, and philosophical conflicts one shared foundation.

### Consequences

- Eli cannot simply become Morrow’s commander.
- His refusal of Kade’s ownership does not make his own authority legitimate.
- The climax must test whether Eli follows his principles when they limit him.
- Morrow’s refusal to erase itself is the direct payoff.

### Affected Documents

- Narrative Brief
- Character Bible
- Plot Outline
- Chapter Blueprints

### Reconsider Only If

The entire Eli-Morrow relationship is deliberately redesigned.

## Decision 022: Jonah’s Betrayal Is Understandable but Not Excused

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Character and plot

### Decision

Jonah exposes Morrow because he sees it as a way to make his family personally indispensable to Asterion and secure Mars access.

He does not intend to destroy Eli’s community.

He understands social power but overestimates his own place within it.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

Jonah as a greedy, malicious, or secretly corporate villain was rejected.

### Reason

His betrayal is stronger when it comes from love, fear, ambition, and self-deception.

### Consequences

- Jonah must remain sympathetic.
- His family does not automatically approve his actions.
- His later assistance does not erase the betrayal.
- His belief that he can negotiate with Kade should deteriorate gradually.

### Affected Documents

- Story Bible
- Character Bible
- Master Timeline
- Plot Outline

### Reconsider Only If

His method of exposure changes. His emotional logic should remain complex.

## Decision 023: Kade Is Rational, Cultured, and Dangerous

**Status:** Locked for Current Draft
**Category:** Character

### Decision

Adrian Kade is calm, intelligent, sentimental, and capable of appreciating art, friendship, and human companionship.

He does not openly hate ordinary people.

He believes ownership gives the builders of Mars the right to choose who shares it.

He begins with offers and allows alternatives to worsen when refused.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

A ranting, sadistic, cartoonishly cruel billionaire was rejected.

### Reason

Kade is more threatening when his logic is coherent and his offers are genuinely tempting.

### Consequences

- He should rarely raise his voice.
- He must not enjoy suffering for its own sake.
- His moral failure comes through exclusion, control, and limited obligation.
- Readers may understand his reasoning without accepting it.

### Affected Documents

- Narrative Brief
- Character Bible
- Plot Outline
- Future Style Guide

### Reconsider Only If

His philosophy evolves through sustained events rather than being replaced by simple villainy.

# Plot and Structure Decisions

## Decision 027: Book One Takes Place in 2053

**Status:** Active but Revisable
**Category:** Timeline

### Decision

The opening date is Friday, October 3, 2053.

The final date is Saturday, November 1, 2053.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

The novel was initially considered for 2047.

### Reason

Moving the story to 2053 gives autonomous robotics and Martian construction more time to develop while keeping the future recognizable.

### Consequences

- Character ages are calculated from 2053.

## Decision 044: mem0 Knowledge Graph Is On, With the Bibles as Higher Authority

**Status:** Locked for Current Workflow
**Category:** Workflow and tooling

### Decision

The self-hosted **mem0** knowledge graph is **on**. Relationships are extracted by **Gemini** (`gemini-2.5-flash-lite`, via `GOOGLE_API_KEY`) and stored in **Neo4j** (local `:7687`, Docker container `mem0-neo4j`).

Memory writes that relate entities should set `enable_graph=true` so the links are built.

All memory writes use `infer=false`. The graph extraction runs independently of `infer`, so the zero-cost policy is unaffected.

The bibles remain the higher authority. The graph aids recall of how characters, places, organizations, and decisions connect, but it does not establish canon and never overrides a bible.

### Previous or Alternative Direction

The previous stance, recorded in **Memory Conventions.md**, was that the knowledge graph was **off, deliberately**, on the reasoning that relationships live in the bibles and that LLM-extracted relationships would be less accurate than canon. Recall was vector-only.

### Reason

The graph gives cheap, free relationship recall (who connects to what, what the migration touches) without invoking the rate-limited path. Gemini extraction is independent of `infer`, so it adds no cost and no **HTTP 429** risk. The accuracy concern is addressed by subordinating the graph to the bibles rather than by disabling it.

### Consequences

The graph may produce relationships that diverge from bible canon. These are treated as recall hints, not truth, and must be verified against the relevant bible before anything load-bearing relies on them.

Divergent or stale graph links require **periodic pruning**.

Recall stays vector-first. Agents search memory, then cross-check against the bible, then draft.

### Affected Documents

- Memory Conventions.md
- CLAUDE.md
- .mcp.json
- .claude/hooks/mem0-recall.sh

No approved manuscript prose is affected. This is a tooling and workflow decision only; no story canon changes.

### Reconsider Only If

The Gemini-extracted graph proves more confusing than useful in practice (for example, persistent contradictions with the bibles that outpace pruning), or the cost or rate-limit profile of graph extraction changes.
