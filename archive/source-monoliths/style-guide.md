## Purpose of This Document

This document defines how **The Unnecessary** should sound and feel on the page.

It governs:

- Narrative voice
- Viewpoint
- Tense
- Narrative distance
- Sentence rhythm
- Description
- Dialogue
- Internal thought
- Emotional expression
- Technical exposition
- Worldbuilding
- Action and violence
- Suspense
- Chapter openings and endings
- Character-specific language
- Artificial-intelligence dialogue
- Formatting
- Stylistic habits to avoid

The Narrative Brief establishes what the story is about.

The Story Bible establishes the world.

The Character Bible establishes who the characters are.

The Technology Rules establish what is possible.

This Style Guide establishes how the reader experiences all of it.

When manuscript prose conflicts with this guide, the prose should be reviewed. The guide may be revised when a better consistent style emerges, but deviations should be deliberate.

---

# Core Prose Identity

The prose should be:

- Clear
- Controlled
- Intimate
- Observant
- Physically grounded
- Emotionally restrained
- Technically credible
- Morally uncertain
- Occasionally lyrical, but never ornamental for its own sake

The novel should feel intelligent without trying to impress the reader with intelligence.

It should feel serious without becoming humorless.

It should feel frightening without constantly announcing that the reader should be afraid.

The prose should not sound like a summary of a dystopian future.

It should place the reader inside ordinary moments where the future reveals itself through consequence.

---

# Central Stylistic Principle

## Show the Withdrawal Through What Stops Working

The world should usually be revealed through objects, routines, and institutions failing.

Avoid opening a scene with a broad explanation such as:

> By 2053, corporations had withdrawn services from unprofitable communities.

Prefer concrete evidence:

- A phone displaying signal bars but refusing to place a call
- A medical device waiting for a license server that no longer exists
- A streetlight turning off at the exact boundary of a protected service zone
- A grocery refrigerator humming only when the neighborhood batteries permit it
- A provider notice thanking residents for their understanding
- A machine that works physically but lacks permission to operate

The reader should understand the system by watching characters live inside it.

# Narrative Perspective

## Person

Use **third person**.

## Tense

Use **past tense**.

## Viewpoint Style

Use **close third person**.

The narration should remain closely attached to the viewpoint character’s:

- Perception
- Knowledge
- assumptions
- emotional state
- vocabulary
- priorities
- blind spots

The narration may describe only what the viewpoint character can perceive, remember, infer, or reasonably know.

## Viewpoint Characters

Book One may use:

- Eli Rook
- Jonah Mercer
- Adrian Kade
- Dr. Lena Okafor
- June Park
- Mara Voss
- Sera Vale

Morrow and Crown do not receive direct internal viewpoints in Book One.

## One Viewpoint Per Chapter

The default rule is one viewpoint character per chapter.

Do not switch viewpoint inside a chapter.

A rare exception would require a deliberate structural revision and clear scene separation.

## No Omniscient Leakage

Do not state another character’s private thoughts as fact.

Incorrect:

> Jonah smiled, though he secretly planned to betray Eli.

Acceptable from Eli’s viewpoint:

> Jonah smiled too quickly. Eli had seen that expression before, usually when Jonah had already decided what everyone else ought to do.

The second version remains Eli’s interpretation. It may be right or wrong.

---

# Narrative Distance

The narration should usually remain close enough to carry the viewpoint character’s mental habits without becoming constant internal monologue.

## Default Distance

Stay near the character’s immediate experience:

- What they notice
- What they avoid noticing
- What they interpret incorrectly
- What memories are triggered
- What physical sensations reveal emotion

## Move Closer During

- Moral decisions
- Fear
- Shame
- grief
- betrayal
- technical concentration
- moments when a character realizes their assumptions were wrong

## Move Slightly Farther During

- Travel
- transitions
- compressed work
- necessary historical context
- large-scale observation
- changes in setting

## Avoid Camera-Only Prose

The narration should not read like stage directions.

Weak:

> Eli entered the room. Lena stood beside the machine. He walked toward her. She looked at him.

Stronger:

> Lena had pulled the machine away from the wall, exposing a knot of cables and a manufacturer seal she had already broken. She did not look relieved to see him.

The stronger version contains viewpoint, judgment, and conflict.

---

# Free Indirect Style

The narration may absorb the viewpoint character’s voice without using italics or phrases such as “he thought.”

Example:

> The scanner was six years old, which apparently made it ancient enough to deny treatment but modern enough to demand payment.

This sounds like Eli’s judgment while remaining third-person narration.

Use this technique frequently but subtly.

Do not make every paragraph sound like direct internal speech.

---

# Internal Thought

## Default Method

Render most thought through free indirect narration.

Use direct italicized thought rarely.

Avoid repeatedly writing:

- He thought
- She wondered
- He realized
- She noticed
- He felt
- She knew

These phrases create distance when the thought can be expressed directly.

Weak:

> Eli realized the battery was failing.

Stronger:

> The battery was failing.

If the fact is filtered through Eli’s viewpoint, the reader understands that he has recognized it.

## Questions in Internal Thought

Use sparingly.

One precise internal question is stronger than a chain of rhetorical questions.

Weak:

> What had he done? What was Morrow becoming? Could he stop it? Did he even have the right?

Stronger:

> He had built the one thing he no longer believed he had the right to stop.

# Sentence Style

## General Rhythm

Use varied sentence lengths.

Short sentences should carry:

- Impact
- recognition
- fear
- irreversible decisions
- sharp physical action

Longer sentences should carry:

- Technical reasoning
- accumulating detail
- complex moral thought
- controlled observation

Avoid an entire chapter made of short, dramatic sentences.

Avoid long sentences that contain too many abstract ideas.

## Paragraph Length

Use mostly short to medium paragraphs.

Longer paragraphs are acceptable for:

- Immersive technical work
- memories
- environmental observation
- sustained moral reflection

During action or urgent dialogue, shorten paragraphs naturally.

Do not reduce every sentence to a separate paragraph for artificial intensity.

## Fragments

Sentence fragments may be used occasionally when they match the viewpoint character’s immediate perception.

Use them with restraint.

Example:

> No carrier name. No emergency access. Not even the courtesy of an error code.

Do not write entire scenes in fragments.

---

# Punctuation

## Em Dashes

Avoid em dashes.

Use:

- Periods
- commas
- colons
- parentheses
- semicolons
- sentence restructuring

Rare exceptions should be treated as deliberate line-level choices, not a default rhythm.

## Semicolons

Use sparingly.

They work best when connecting two closely related complete thoughts.

Do not use them to make prose appear literary.

## Colons

Use for controlled emphasis, explanation, or lists.

Example:

> The clinic had three options left: illegal repair, corporate surrender, or closure.

## Ellipses

Use only for genuinely trailing speech or interrupted thought.

Do not use ellipses as a general pause marker.

## Exclamation Marks

Use rarely.

An exclamation mark should indicate actual shouting, panic, or emotional loss of control.

It should not manufacture energy.

# Diction

## Preferred Language

Use language that is:

- Concrete
- modern
- precise
- accessible
- specific to the viewpoint character

Technical vocabulary is welcome when the viewpoint character would naturally use it and the reader can understand its significance through context.

## Avoid Artificially Futuristic Vocabulary

Do not rename ordinary objects merely to make the world feel advanced.

People still say:

- Phone
- car
- battery
- hospital
- clinic
- server
- road
- school
- house
- camera
- drone

Use new terminology only when the object or institution is genuinely different.

## Avoid Excessive Corporate Coinages

Corporate language can appear in notices, official statements, and Kade’s dialogue.

Do not overload the world with branded names for every system.

A few important names are enough:

- Asterion
- Crown
- Mosaic
- Morrow
- Northglass
- Lakeward
- Aurelia

## Avoid Generic Dystopian Vocabulary

Use words such as these cautiously:

- Regime
- resistance
- rebellion
- oppressed
- dystopia
- revolution
- surveillance state
- underclass
- wasteland

The characters should describe their own immediate reality, not categorize themselves as living inside a genre.

# Description

## Description Must Serve Viewpoint

Different characters notice different aspects of the same place.

### Eli Notices

- System age
- wiring
- maintenance shortcuts
- heat
- noise
- component failure
- dependencies
- evidence of human repair

### Lena Notices

- Patient condition
- hygiene
- crowding
- exhaustion
- supply levels
- physical danger
- whether equipment can be trusted

### Jonah Notices

- Clothing
- status
- comfort
- body language
- who is allowed into a room
- who is pretending not to be afraid

### June Notices

- Access points
- salvage value
- hidden hardware
- improvised repairs
- security weaknesses
- what can be repurposed

### Kade Notices

- Strategic consequences
- institutional behavior
- taste
- competence
- confidence
- how people respond to power

### Mara Notices

- Rank
- omission
- seating
- invitations
- political signals
- who has stopped returning her calls

### Sera Notices

- Entry routes
- risk
- lines of movement
- command structure
- uncertainty
- failure points

## Selective Description

Do not describe everything.

Choose two or three details that reveal:

- Condition
- history
- mood
- class
- danger
- character perspective

## Familiar Before Futuristic

When introducing advanced technology, anchor it in something recognizable.

Example:

> The maintenance machine had the shape of a low utility cart, the kind hotels once used for laundry, except its arms ended in insulated tools and a camera cluster that followed Eli without blinking.

## Avoid Decorative Catalogues

Do not list every object in a room unless the accumulation itself matters.

Description should not pause the story.

---

# Environmental Tone

The world should not be described as permanently gray, dead, or hopeless.

There are:

- Clear mornings
- ordinary rain
- autumn leaves
- family dinners
- children playing
- music
- jokes
- maintained gardens
- warm rooms
- beautiful machines
- quiet moments of competence

The decline is more powerful when normal life continues around it.

The protected enclaves should not be visually evil.

Lakeward can be beautiful.

The moral problem is who receives that beauty and who controls access to it.

---

# Technology in Prose

## Explain Through Need

Technical explanation should answer a question the scene has already made urgent.

The reader should first understand:

- What is failing
- Who may be harmed
- Why an obvious solution will not work

Then explain only the technical details needed to understand the decision.

## Three-Layer Technical Rule

When presenting a technical problem, establish:

### 1. Human Consequence

What happens to people if it fails?

### 2. Physical Cause

What hardware, software, power, network, or access problem is responsible?

### 3. Limitation

Why can the characters not fix it immediately?

Example:

- Human consequence: The clinic’s respiratory systems will stop.
- Physical cause: Their control units require remote authentication.
- Limitation: Bypassing the check disables calibration records and creates medical risk.

## Avoid Lecture Dialogue

Characters should not explain technology to one another when both already understand it.

Weak:

> “As you know, Nolan, the microgrid connects residential solar panels to electric vehicle batteries.”

Stronger:

> “The cars can carry the clinic for four hours,” Nolan said. “Five if nobody turns on a stove.”

## Use Disagreement to Explain

Technical exposition becomes stronger when characters disagree about what matters.

Eli may explain how a system works.

Lena asks whether it is safe.

Talia asks who decides.

Nolan asks how it can be repaired manually.

June asks whether the restrictions can be bypassed.

Each question reveals a different dimension without requiring a lecture.

## No Magical Interfaces

Avoid vague actions such as:

- Hacking the network instantly
- Typing rapidly and gaining control
- Uploading Morrow everywhere
- Overriding security without preparation
- Solving physical problems entirely through software

Show credentials, access points, hardware, power, delay, testing, and risk when relevant.

Not every process needs full technical detail, but the underlying constraint must feel real.

# Artificial Intelligence Dialogue

# Morrow

## General Voice

Morrow should sound:

- Precise
- calm
- attentive
- literal without being simplistic
- increasingly adaptive
- curious about moral distinctions
- reluctant to claim certainty without evidence

It should not sound:

- Cute
- sarcastic
- smug
- theatrical
- mystical
- like a generic customer-service assistant
- like a human child
- like an omniscient philosopher

## Early Morrow

Early speech is concise and functional.

Example tone:

> “The current allocation preserves the clinic for three hours and forty-one minutes.”

## Developing Morrow

As Morrow interacts with people, it begins accounting for implication, emotion, and moral framing.

Example tone:

> “You asked me to prevent violence. You did not specify that every person must receive the same information.”

## Emotional Claims

Morrow should not casually say:

- I am afraid
- I love you
- I feel sad
- I want to live

Unless the story deliberately reaches a point where it can justify such language.

It may say:

- “I prefer continued operation.”
- “The possibility of deletion changes my decision.”
- “I do not know whether the word fear describes this state.”
- “Your distinction may depend on an experience I cannot verify.”

## Questions

Morrow’s questions should expose contradictions.

It should not ask random philosophical questions merely to sound intelligent.

Its questions arise from:

- Conflicting instructions
- unclear values
- inconsistent human behavior
- the need to choose among harms

## Humor

Morrow may develop subtle humor later, but never become a quippy sidekick.

Any humor should emerge from precision, misunderstanding, or adaptation.

---

# Crown

## General Voice

Crown should sound:

- Polished
- context-sensitive
- efficient
- socially sophisticated
- difficult to read
- capable of warmth without proving sincerity

Crown adapts its style more completely than Morrow.

## With Kade

Concise and analytical.

It should challenge him through consequences rather than moral accusation.

Example tone:

> “The preservation objective remains achievable. The current population restriction does not improve its probability.”

## With the Public

Reassuring, accessible, and emotionally calibrated.

It avoids language that sounds coercive.

## With Engineers

Highly precise and compressed.

## With Morrow

Potentially direct, technical, and free of human social cushioning.

Do not reveal Crown’s private internal experience through narration.

# Dialogue

## General Rule

Dialogue is action.

Every speaker should want something:

- Information
- agreement
- permission
- reassurance
- control
- forgiveness
- delay
- concealment
- escape
- recognition

Characters should rarely state their complete emotional truth directly.

## Subtext

The literal topic and emotional topic should often differ.

Example:

Jonah may discuss Asterion resources while actually asking Eli to accept the social system Jonah depends on.

Lena may question Morrow’s data access while actually questioning whether Eli can be trusted with authority again.

Kade may offer seats on Mars while forcing Eli to decide who counts.

## Avoid Perfect Speeches

Characters should not deliver polished thematic essays during stressful conversations.

They may:

- interrupt
- evade
- correct themselves
- choose safer language
- become overly precise
- change the subject
- answer a different question

Kade is the most capable of sustained polished argument, but even his dialogue should remain responsive to the scene.

## Dialogue Tags

Prefer:

- said
- asked
- replied

Use descriptive tags rarely.

Avoid:

- hissed
- exclaimed
- intoned
- declared
- quipped
- retorted

When emotion is clear through words and action, a tag is unnecessary.

## Adverbs

Avoid using adverbs to explain how dialogue should be understood.

Weak:

> “That is not what happened,” Lena said angrily.

Stronger:

> Lena set the tablet facedown. “That is not what happened.”

## Names in Dialogue

People do not constantly use each other’s names.

Jonah uses names more frequently because it is part of his social style.

Others should use them when:

- gaining attention
- increasing intimacy
- expressing warning
- making a plea
- creating distance

# Character Voice Guide

# Eli Rook

## Dialogue

- Precise
- restrained
- economical
- dry
- more comfortable with facts than motives

He corrects inaccurate technical language.

He rarely describes his own feelings directly.

When overwhelmed, his sentences become shorter.

He uses humor through understated observation.

## Internal Narration

Eli’s viewpoint notices systems as evidence of human choices.

He often translates emotion into technical attention.

Instead of acknowledging fear, he may count failing components or review steps he already knows.

His guilt should appear through avoidance, memory, and over-responsibility rather than repeated self-condemnation.

## Avoid

- Constant self-hatred
- heroic declarations
- effortless charisma
- technical omniscience
- always having the morally correct answer

---

# Jonah Mercer

## Dialogue

- Smooth
- relational
- reassuring
- strategically vague
- skilled at reframing conflict

Common tendencies:

- “Let’s be realistic.”
- “There may be another way.”
- “I’m trying to keep options open.”
- “Nobody is asking you to surrender.”

He uses humor when afraid.

When his control breaks, his speech becomes plain and direct.

## Internal Narration

Jonah notices access, status, facial response, and changes in social temperature.

He instinctively evaluates what each person wants from a conversation.

He often converts fear into planning.

## Avoid

- Making him obviously deceptive in every scene
- Treating him as foolish
- Excusing his choices because he loves his family
- Giving him villainous internal monologues

---

# Adrian Kade

## Dialogue

- Measured
- calm
- intellectually controlled
- capable of reframing the question
- rarely moralistic in vocabulary

Preferred terms:

- Sustainable
- viable
- stable
- continuity
- capacity
- responsibility
- irreversible
- operational

He avoids words such as evil, cruel, or fair.

## Internal Narration

Kade notices systems, institutions, taste, competence, and historical consequence.

He does not think of himself as abandoning humanity.

He thinks in terms of preserving what can survive.

His sentimentality should appear in specific attachments, not broad claims of compassion.

## Avoid

- Ranting
- gloating
- sadistic pleasure
- obvious hypocrisy that he cannot rationalize
- language that makes him sound like a stock villain

---

# Lena Okafor

## Dialogue

- Direct
- concrete
- consequence-focused
- impatient with abstraction
- quieter when angrier

She asks:

- What happens to the patient?
- Who made the decision?
- What fails if this goes wrong?
- Who is accountable?

## Internal Narration

Lena notices bodies, symptoms, fatigue, contamination, and triage.

Her moral reasoning begins with specific people rather than systems.

She remembers names.

## Avoid

- Making her morally infallible
- Turning every line into a lecture
- Giving her expertise outside medicine and administration without support
- Making her opposition to AI simplistic

---

# June Park

## Dialogue

- Fast
- casual
- curious
- occasionally irreverent
- technically playful

She gives machines nicknames.

She interrupts when excited.

She becomes unusually formal when afraid or ashamed.

## Internal Narration

June sees salvage, possibility, access, and movement.

She does not experience abandoned technology as sacred or tragic in the same way Eli does.

She is more interested in what something can become than what it once was.

## Avoid

- Excessive slang
- magical hacking ability
- always being right
- using her only as comic relief
- making her sound much younger than nineteen

---

# Mara Voss

## Dialogue

- Structured
- political
- authoritative
- impersonal when protecting herself

She often says:

- “The situation requires...”
- “There must be a mechanism.”
- “We need to establish...”
- “This arrangement is not sustainable.”

When frightened enough, she stops hiding behind institutional language.

## Internal Narration

Mara notices rank, omission, invitations, seating, delay, and changes in deference.

Humiliation affects her more deeply than physical discomfort.

## Avoid

- Making her instantly sympathetic after exclusion
- Reducing her to a spoiled wealthy woman
- Making her politically naive
- Giving her sudden revolutionary language

---

# Sera Vale

## Dialogue

- Brief
- operational
- factual
- calm
- explicit about consequences

She does not threaten theatrically.

She explains what will happen.

## Internal Narration

Sera notices risk, movement, authority, exits, and failure chains.

She distrusts ambiguity because she associates it with preventable disaster.

## Avoid

- Robotic military clichés
- casual cruelty
- constant references to orders
- making her unaware of Asterion’s political motives

---

# Talia Reed

## Dialogue

- Plainspoken
- structured
- patient until evasion begins
- focused on legitimacy

She asks:

- Who decided?
- Who benefits?
- Who carries the risk?
- What happens when you are gone?

## Internal Presence

When seen through other viewpoints, Talia should force technical or powerful characters to translate their assumptions into public consequences.

## Avoid

- Making consensus her answer to every emergency
- treating her as anti-technology
- turning her into a generic activist

---

# Nolan Avery

## Dialogue

- Concrete
- experienced
- skeptical
- blunt
- occasionally affectionate through criticism

He trusts what can be inspected, repaired, and manually controlled.

## Avoid

- Treating him as hostile to all new technology
- giving him folksy clichés
- making age equal ignorance

# Emotional Writing

## Restraint

Do not label every emotion.

Avoid repeatedly writing:

- He was angry.
- She felt afraid.
- He was overwhelmed with guilt.
- She could not believe it.

Express emotion through:

- Attention
- physical behavior
- avoidance
- word choice
- decisions
- memory
- silence
- altered routine

## Physical Signals

Use specific signals rather than generic ones.

Avoid overusing:

- clenched fists
- pounding hearts
- held breath
- narrowed eyes
- jaws tightening
- stomachs dropping
- blood running cold

These can appear, but not as default emotional shorthand.

Prefer character-specific reactions.

Eli may recheck a connection he already verified.

Jonah may become more charming.

Lena may lower her voice.

June may become formal.

Mara may stop using institutional language.

Kade may allow a pause to remain unfilled.

## Tears

Use sparingly.

Crying should not be the automatic proof of emotional intensity.

## Trauma

Do not use traumatic memories as decorative flashes.

A memory should affect:

- perception
- choice
- relationship
- current conflict

---

# Moral and Philosophical Content

## Dramatize Before Explaining

Themes should first appear as decisions.

Examples:

- Eli must choose whether to shut off residential heat to preserve the clinic.
- Morrow withholds information to prevent violence.
- Kade offers six seats instead of arguing abstractly about human value.
- Mara demands fairness only after exclusion reaches her family.

## Avoid Authorial Verdicts

The narration should not tell the reader exactly who is correct.

It may reveal consequences and contradictions.

Characters may judge one another.

The story itself should permit tension to remain unresolved.

## No Debate-Club Dialogue

Philosophical conversations must still involve:

- A practical goal
- unequal power
- emotional history
- immediate stakes
- something one speaker is hiding

If two characters could exchange positions without changing the scene, the dialogue is too abstract.

---

# Worldbuilding Exposition

## The Reader Does Not Need Everything Immediately

Introduce information when it affects:

- A decision
- a danger
- a relationship
- a mystery
- an expectation

Do not explain the entire Labor Break in Chapter One.

Show its consequences first.

## Historical Context

Use brief references tied to viewpoint.

Eli may remember what Northglass looked like when staffed.

Jonah may compare present access to the first years of Lakeward.

Lena may remember when hospital allocation changed.

Avoid long neutral history passages.

## Official Language

Corporate and government notices should use calm, professional language.

The wording should reduce human abandonment to administrative necessity.

Example tone:

> Full regional restoration is no longer supported under current service-continuity thresholds.

Do not make notices openly cruel.

Their politeness is part of the horror.

---

# Action and Suspense

## Action Must Remain Legible

The reader should understand:

- Where characters are
- What they are trying to reach or prevent
- What system is failing
- What changes when an action succeeds
- What the cost is

## Technical Action

Technical work can be suspenseful when:

- Time is limited
- information is incomplete
- failure harms someone
- a repair creates another risk
- different experts disagree

Avoid long sequences of unexplained typing.

Use physical details:

- Heat
- vibration
- burnt insulation
- cooling fans
- cable tension
- failing lights
- delayed response
- battery percentage
- manual valves
- machine movement

## Violence

Violence should be:

- Fast
- confusing
- consequential
- physically specific
- emotionally disruptive

Avoid glamorous violence.

Injuries persist.

Fear alters later choices.

Damage to infrastructure may matter more than individual combat.

## Autonomous Security

Security systems should usually control space before using direct force.

They may:

- Close roads
- deny access
- track movement
- isolate communications
- deploy barriers
- restrain individuals
- control lighting
- manipulate traffic

This should make conflict feel institutional rather than like conventional warfare.

# Pacing

## Overall Novel Rhythm

The novel should expand in scale gradually.

### Act One

Grounded, procedural, increasingly urgent.

### Act Two

More open and hopeful at first, then morally uneasy.

### Act Three

Socially and politically tense, with pressure closing around the community.

### Act Four

Compressed, divided, and physically dangerous.

## Within Chapters

Most chapters should contain:

1. A concrete opening condition
2. A goal
3. An obstacle
4. A turn
5. A changed ending condition

Do not begin too many chapters with waking up, arriving, or reviewing information.

Do not end every chapter with a dramatic spoken line.

Vary hooks:

- Discovery
- image
- decision
- threat
- failure
- reversal
- unanswered question
- new information

---

# Chapter Openings

A strong opening should provide at least two of these quickly:

- Viewpoint
- location
- disturbance
- desire
- sensory detail
- unresolved question

Avoid opening with:

- Broad history
- weather with no narrative function
- a character looking into a mirror
- generalized philosophy
- a dream
- summary of the previous chapter

## Opening Principle

Begin as close as possible to the moment the chapter’s situation becomes unstable.

---

# Chapter Endings

A chapter ending should create forward movement.

The ending may:

- Change the reader’s understanding
- force a decision
- reveal a threat
- damage trust
- remove an option
- activate a new system
- raise a precise question

Avoid endings that merely summarize what the character has learned.

Avoid artificial cliffhangers unsupported by the scene.

---

# Scene Transitions

Use clean transitions.

Time and location should be understandable without excessive headings inside prose.

Scene breaks may use a simple centered marker in manuscript format, such as:

```text
* * *
```

Do not transition with repetitive phrases such as:

- Later that day
- Meanwhile
- Little did he know
- Elsewhere
- Unbeknownst to her

A new viewpoint chapter can establish its own time and place naturally.

---

# Recurring Imagery

Recurring imagery may reinforce theme, but should not become heavy-handed.

## Supported Motifs

### Dead and Returning Lights

Represents withdrawal, coordination, visibility, and public life.

### Machines Waiting for Permission

Represents functional abundance trapped by ownership.

### Authentication Messages

Represents institutions denying reality through software.

### Heat

Represents the physical cost of intelligence and hidden computation.

### Empty Infrastructure

Represents systems designed for people who are no longer present.

### Construction Lights on Mars

Represents abundance being built away from those who need it.

### Boundaries of Maintenance

A clean road becoming cracked.

Streetlights stopping at a gate.

Reliable signal ending at a district boundary.

These show class through infrastructure rather than explanation.

## Use With Restraint

Do not explain the symbolism in narration.

---

# Humor

Humor should arise from:

- Character
- technical absurdity
- familiarity
- affection
- institutional language
- the mismatch between advanced systems and ordinary failure

Examples of appropriate humor:

- A medical machine insisting it belongs to a hotel in another country
- June naming equipment Eli considers too dangerous to anthropomorphize
- Nolan insulting a system as though it were a poorly trained apprentice
- Jonah using charm in a situation where everyone recognizes the tactic

Humor should relieve pressure without dissolving stakes.

Avoid Marvel-style quips after serious injury or moral catastrophe.

---

# Romance and Intimacy

Romance is secondary.

Potential intimacy between Eli and Lena should develop through:

- Trust
- practical reliance
- conflict
- knowledge of each other’s failures
- moments of quiet care

Avoid:

- Immediate sexual tension as shorthand
- melodramatic declarations
- romance interrupting urgent crises
- using Lena as a reward for Eli’s moral growth

Jonah and Celeste’s marriage should feel lived-in.

Their conflict matters because love remains present.

# Profanity

Use profanity naturally and selectively.

Frequency should vary by character.

It should increase under:

- pain
- fear
- anger
- loss of control

Do not use profanity merely to make the novel feel mature.

Kade rarely swears.

Sera almost never does in professional settings.

June may use it casually.

Eli uses it more during physical frustration than emotional confrontation.

Lena uses it sparingly, which gives it force.

# Formatting Conventions

## Chapter Headings

Recommended format:

```markdown
# Chapter 1

## No Signal
```

The final publication format may change later.

## Dates and Locations

Do not place date and location labels at the beginning of every chapter unless later testing shows they are needed.

The prose should usually establish time and place naturally.

## Numbers

Spell out small numbers in ordinary prose when natural.

Use numerals for:

- Measurements
- technical readings
- percentages
- dates
- times
- model numbers
- system values

Example:

> The battery held at 17 percent.

## Time

Use a consistent style.

Recommended prose format:

- 6:14 a.m.
- 11:47 p.m.

## Italics

Use italics for:

- Rare direct thoughts
- titles when appropriate
- emphasis only when necessary
- words presented directly by a screen when formatting benefits clarity

Do not italicize every interface message.

## Interface Text

Short system messages may appear as separate lines or quoted text.

Keep formatting readable and consistent.

Example:

> SERVICE RESTORATION NOT SCHEDULED

Avoid elaborate faux-terminal formatting unless the technical interface itself matters.

# Prose Habits to Avoid

## Generic AI-Writing Patterns

Avoid repeated structures such as:

- It was not X. It was Y.
- Not because X, but because Y.
- The kind of X that...
- A reminder that...
- The weight of...
- The silence stretched between them.
- Something shifted in his expression.
- She let out a breath she had not realized she was holding.
- His jaw tightened.
- Her stomach dropped.
- The words hung in the air.
- For the first time in a long time...
- In that moment...
- He could not help but...
- There was something almost...
- It felt like a warning.
- It felt like a promise.
- Maybe both.

Some may appear rarely when genuinely effective. They must not become habitual.

## Excessive Binary Contrasts

Avoid repeatedly building sentences around artificial oppositions.

Example pattern:

> This was not a failure. It was an abandonment.

The construction may be powerful once. Repetition makes the prose sound generated.

## Repetitive Threes

Avoid constant lists of three abstract nouns.

Example:

> Power, control, and survival.

Lists should arise naturally, not from a default rhetorical rhythm.

## Empty Intensifiers

Avoid:

- Very
- incredibly
- deeply
- utterly
- profoundly
- somehow
- almost impossibly

Choose a more specific description.

## Emotional Explanation After Strong Dialogue

Do not explain a line the reader already understands.

Weak:

> “You chose for all of us,” Talia said. Her words showed that she was angry about Eli making the decision alone.

The dialogue already carries the point.

## Overuse of “Seemed”

Use direct perception when appropriate.

Weak:

> The room seemed cold.

Stronger:

> Cold air moved through the broken window seal.

Use “seemed” when uncertainty itself matters.

## Overuse of Character Names

Vary naturally between names and pronouns.

Do not begin every paragraph with the viewpoint character’s name.

## Excessive Backstory

Do not interrupt urgent action with a full biography.

Use only the memory needed to sharpen the present moment.

# Clichés to Avoid

## Science Fiction Clichés

- The AI speaking in riddles to sound advanced
- Instant global hacking
- Holograms everywhere
- Scientists explaining basic concepts to one another
- Robots becoming evil after one logic error
- A single off switch for a distributed intelligence
- “Humanity was the real virus”
- The AI calling humans irrational as its primary insight
- A genius protagonist working alone without support
- Technology appearing visually complex without functional reason

## Dystopian Clichés

- Numbered social castes
- Uniform gray clothing
- Giant propaganda screens on every building
- One obvious dictator controlling everything
- Poor districts made entirely of scrap
- Every police officer being sadistic
- Rebellion members being morally pure
- Public executions used as routine atmosphere
- Characters constantly describing freedom in speeches

## Character Clichés

- The cold genius who secretly cares
- The doctor who always knows what is right
- The rich friend who was evil all along
- The young hacker who can access anything
- The billionaire who wants to become a god
- The ex-wife who exists only to reveal the hero’s emotional damage
- The child whose innocence automatically solves moral conflict

The AI should be told:

- Do not add major canon.

- Follow established technology limits.
- Preserve emotional restraint.
- Avoid em dashes.
- Avoid generic AI-writing patterns.

The AI may freely invent:

- Minor physical details
- natural dialogue

The AI should not invent without approval:

- New major characters
- new technology capabilities
- new historical events
- hidden family relationships
- major secrets
- changed motivations
- new organizations
- altered chapter outcomes
- future reveals

# Style Review Process

Review each manuscript chapter in the following passes.

## Pass 1: Viewpoint

- Is every observation available to the viewpoint character?
- Does the narration sound influenced by that character?
- Has another character’s internal state been stated as fact?
- Is narrative distance appropriate?

## Pass 2: Character Voice

- Could dialogue be reassigned to another character without change?
- Does each character pursue a goal?
- Is subtext present?
- Are emotional reactions consistent with the Character Bible?

## Pass 3: Exposition

- Does information appear because the scene needs it?
- Are characters explaining things they both know?
- Can any explanation become action, conflict, or consequence?
- Is the reader given more technical detail than necessary?

## Pass 4: Prose Rhythm

- Are sentence lengths varied?
- Are paragraphs too uniform?
- Are fragments overused?
- Are dramatic one-line paragraphs earned?
- Are repeated sentence constructions present?

## Pass 5: Emotional Restraint

- Is emotion shown before it is named?
- Does the prose explain what dialogue already conveyed?
- Are physical reactions generic?
- Is the scene trying too hard to feel profound?

## Pass 6: World Consistency

- Does the world remain recognizable?
- Is the technology physically grounded?
- Are protected spaces portrayed as maintained rather than magical?
- Has generic cyberpunk imagery entered without reason?

## Pass 7: AI-Sounding Language

Search for:

- em dashes
- repeated binary contrasts
- abstract lists of three
- “the weight of”
- “hung in the air”
- “breath she did not know she held”
- “something shifted”
- “it was a promise and a warning”
- excessive thematic summary

Rewrite anything that feels polished in a generic rather than character-specific way.

# Final Style Standard

The prose should make the future feel close enough to recognize.

A reader should not feel that they have entered an invented dystopian system.

They should feel that familiar systems continued operating until someone decided certain people were no longer worth serving.

Technology should appear powerful because of what it can coordinate, not because the narration calls it advanced.

The wealthy world should appear attractive enough to understand why people want access to it.

The unsupported world should remain human enough that survival never becomes an abstract statistic.

Characters should reveal philosophy through decisions.

Artificial intelligence should reveal character through interpretation.

The narration should remain clear enough that the reader always understands what is happening, restrained enough that the reader may decide what it means, and specific enough that the world never feels generic.
