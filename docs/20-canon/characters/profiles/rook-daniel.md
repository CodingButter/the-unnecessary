**Current residence:** Until his death, the Rook family house in Flint, Michigan, where he died. The same house is now occupied by Ruth alone.
**Household:** Married to Ruth Rook for about thirty-eight years; raised one son, Eli, in Flint [open that he and Ruth raised Eli in Flint]. Eli is an only child. The household is now Ruth alone in the Flint house.

**Faction or class:** Everyone Else, per `../../world/social-structure.md`. [open] (The Rooks "were never wealthy" per `./rook-eli.md`; a Flint repair tradesman is plainly outside the protected systems.)
**Primary viewpoint:** No. He is never a point-of-view character.
**Story role:** Offstage backstory anchor and the source of Eli's worldview. He is the transistor-radio man named in Chapter 1, the man whose lesson about systems is Eli's entire frame, and the parent whose name Eli carries. He is the absent father whose relic and instruction Eli keeps and works inside of every day.

## Physical and Identifiers

![Portrait of Daniel Rook](../portraits/rook-daniel.png)

### Frame

Five feet eleven inches, an inch taller than Eli, with the same narrow build thickened only slightly through the forearms and the back from decades of physical work. Lean, never heavy, the leanness of a man who skipped meals to finish a job. Posture economical and slightly stooped at the neck from a lifetime bent over open panels and pump housings. He took up little room and was easy to lose in a doorway, the same quality the chapter notes in Eli.

### Coloring

Pale, weathered complexion, reddened across the back of the neck and the forearms from outdoor municipal work in all weather. Dark brown hair gone iron-and-ash gray and thinning at the crown, cut short and plain, often cut by Ruth at the kitchen table (this is the household root of Eli cutting his own hair, an established Eli trait). Gray-green eyes, the exact tired gray-green the chapter gives Eli, which is where Eli's came from.

### Face

A long, narrow face, the "narrow face" register the manuscript uses for working fathers, lined deeply at the brow and the mouth. Heavy, level eyebrows. A short gray beard kept close, trimmed without much attention. Expression at rest was attentive and unhurried, a face that gave very little away and watched the work rather than the room. The stillness people find unnerving in Eli is the father's stillness, learned by copying him.

### Hands and handedness

Right-handed. Large, square repairman's hands, knuckles thickened and permanently grease-darkened in the creases, nails short and split. Old burns and small healed cuts across the backs and the fingers, the same catalog of marks the chapter gives Eli's hands, because Eli's hands are a copy of these. [open that Eli's hands carry such marks] Reduced sensation in two fingertips on the right hand from a lifetime of live work and one bad arc off a municipal control cabinet (rhymes deliberately with Eli's canon reduced-sensation fingertip from an industrial power-controller accident). His hands read the whole man: cold outdoor work, voltage, water, and patience.

### Distinguishing marks

A pale rope of scar across the left forearm where a pump coupling let go under pressure years ago. A flat shiny burn on the right palm from grabbing a controller bus that was supposed to be dead and was not, the story he told Eli as a warning more than once (this is a behavioral root, see Section 10). A deviated, once-broken nose from a falling access panel. No tattoos; he thought them a young man's spending. A wedding band worn thin and never removed, even for work, scarred and scuffed from the inside of a hundred panels.

### Identity and body status (2053)

Legally registered, practically stranded, per `../../technology/infrastructure/identity-and-money.md`. His verified identity existed on paper to the end, but the institutions that used to recognize it, the city payroll, the cardiology service, the pharmacy chain, withdrew from Flint the same polite way everything withdrew, and a registered identity bought him almost nothing by the end. No augmentations and no implants, partly economy and partly a tradesman's flat distrust of any device that has to ask a server for permission to keep working. [behavior-only] (proposed) Chronic condition: a heart condition, manageable for decades in the old world with monitoring and routine medication, that lost its chain of care one withdrawal at a time as the local cardiology service folded and the medication supply thinned. He died at home of it, untreated at the end not because the treatment was hard but because the institution on the other end of it had quietly stopped answering, the same administrative death the manuscript gives the clinic's patients. [reveal: exact in-story timing is an open plot decision]

### Movement and voice

He moved deliberately, never quickly, the unhurry of a man who had learned that rushing a live system is how it kills you. A slight favoring of the lower back late in the day. His voice was low, dry, and flat, a Michigan working vowel with no music in it on purpose, the verbal equivalent of his face. He spoke seldom and never to fill a silence. When he did explain a thing, it was while his hands were already in it.

### Grooming and default dress

Plain and functional. A canvas work coat over layered flannel through the cold months, a cap with a faded municipal-utility logo, insulated boots resoled more than once. A cloth in the back pocket, a multi-tool and a small flashlight always on him, the flashlight the thing he handed a child to hold so the child would learn by watching (behavioral root). Hands washed with shop soap that never quite cleaned the creases. He smelled of cold metal, machine oil, pond-cool water, and pipe tobacco he eventually quit and missed.

## Personality

In public Daniel was quiet, dependable, and undemonstrative. He was the man a street called when something had stopped and no one official would come. He did not perform competence; he simply arrived, found the fault, and left the thing running, and he was uneasy with thanks. In private he was warmer than he showed, but his warmth came out as attention and repair rather than words. He told his son he loved him by teaching him to find the fault himself.

His humor was bone-dry and rare, delivered without a change of expression, almost always at the expense of a machine pretending to be smarter than it was. Eli's exact dry register, the joke offered flat on the exit, is the father's, inherited whole.

**Articulated goal:** Keep the things people depend on running, water first, and be the man the street can call.
**Deeper need:** To matter through being needed, quietly, without being thanked or watched. To be the unseen reason a thing still works.
**Governing fear:** That a system he was responsible for would fail and someone ordinary would pay for it while the people who owned the system were somewhere else and untouched.
**Core contradiction:** He spent his life proving that nothing runs without a person constantly preventing it from failing, and then, with his own failing body, he hid the person who needed preventing, himself, and let his own care lapse to keep paying for everyone else's. [behavior-only] (proposed)
**Moral boundary:** He would not walk away from a failing system that people drank from or relied on, and he would not let a family go without water to settle a bill.
**What could make them cross it:** Almost nothing on his own account; only being made to choose between his own household's survival and a stranger's could have moved him, and even then he would have resented the man who built the choice, not the stranger.
**Private reading of the collapse:** No one blew anything up. The owners simply stopped sending anyone to hold the systems up, finger by finger, and called it efficiency, and the systems kept their manners while they failed. Everything works only because someone is preventing it from failing, and the world decided that someone was a cost it would no longer pay. (This is the canon lesson, stated as his worldview.) [open]
**Personal definition of human value:** You are worth the failures you quietly prevent. A man is the difference between a thing running and a thing stopped.
**What they are preserving:** The unglamorous human labor that holds infrastructure up, and the proof, handed to his son, that you can own a thing outright when it never has to ask permission to work. (His entry in the Final Character Standard.)

## Daily Life and Habits

This section describes his working life in Flint before his death, grounded in the canon trade and `../../world/social-structure.md`. He rose before light and listened to the house first, the way a repairman listens, for the pump note and the furnace. He carried a transistor radio from room to room and out to the truck, a battery thing with a telescoping antenna that answered to no network and asked no server for a yes, and when a signal was thin he would hold it up to a window and turn slowly until it cleared. [open that he was a transistor-radio man] He worked the municipal water equipment on a city contract while the city still paid, then kept working pieces of it on barter and goodwill after the city stopped, because the water did not stop needing him when the payroll did. He took payment in money while there was money, then in food, parts, labor, and favors as the everyday economy turned to barter, per `../../technology/infrastructure/identity-and-money.md`. He ate what Ruth cooked, slept lightly, and could not pass a stopped thing without opening it.

## Hobbies and Interests

- The transistor radio and a shelf of older analog receivers he kept alive for the pleasure of catching a far station at night, a human voice arriving over the air that belonged to no company. [open that the radio is his]
- Tinkering for its own sake: small motors, clocks, and pumps brought back from the dead on the kitchen table, given away once fixed.
- Reading the technical manuals Ruth brought home from the library, slowly and completely, the way some men read scripture (ties the two parents together).

## Likes and Dislikes

Likes: the steady note of a pump that is running right, strong black coffee gone cold while he worked, a far AM station at night, the weight of a good tool, a child holding the flashlight steady, rain on a tin roof. Dislikes: any device that has to phone a company to do its job, thanks said too loudly, a job done fast instead of right, men who owned systems they could not have fixed, and being watched while he worked.

## Voice and Speech

Low, dry, flat, and spare. Short declarative sentences, often issued while his hands were busy, so the words came in fragments between turns of a wrench. He used almost no metaphor, and when he reached for one it was mechanical and exact. He let silences stand. His characteristic move was to answer a question by handing you the tool and the problem, so you arrived at the answer yourself. Under stress he got shorter and more literal, the same compression the manuscript names in Eli. His one repeated saying, the family scripture, was the lesson Eli carries: that things appear effortless only because someone is constantly preventing them from failing. [open]

## History and Background

Born in Flint around 1983, in the long industrial decline the manuscript names as the city Eli grew up in (Flint origin grounded in `./rook-eli.md`). He came up in the trades when there were still factories to keep running, and when the factories thinned he moved his skills to where they were still needed, onto the city's industrial controls and then onto the municipal water equipment, the unglamorous machinery a city quietly dies without. [open trade] He married Ruth around 2012. Their son Elias Daniel was born February 11, 2015, named for the father. [open name; canon DOB] Daniel taught the boy by the panel and the flashlight, gave him the lesson about prevented failure, and watched him win scholarships and leave for the systems Daniel had only ever repaired by hand.

As the withdrawal reached Flint, the city stopped paying for the water work and Daniel kept doing pieces of it anyway, on barter and stubbornness, because the water did not care about the payroll. His own heart condition lost its chain of care the same administrative way, and he died at home in the Flint house circa spring 2050, about 67, three years before Book One opens. [reveal: exact in-story timing is an open plot decision] Eli kept the transistor radio.

## Private History and Behavioral Roots

Causes and their visible effects. Most are author-facing constraints, never explained on the page.

- Grabbed a control bus that was supposed to be dead and got the burn for it -> never trusted a "powered down" indicator again, always tested live, and drilled the same caution into Eli, which is why Eli works abandoned systems with a respect that looks like slowness. [behavior-only] (proposed)
- Was blamed when systems failed and never thanked when they did not -> learned to be undemonstrative, to let the running machine be the only statement, and passed that flat affect to his son. [behavior-only] (proposed)
- Spent a career being the unseen reason water kept moving -> needed to be needed without being seen, and could not tolerate being the one cared for. [behavior-only] (proposed)
- Kept the radio and the analog receivers because they answered to no company -> taught Eli, by object lesson and never by speech, that the only thing you truly own is the thing that never has to ask permission to work. [behavior-only] (proposed)
- Hid how sick he was so the money kept going to the work and to Eli -> gave Eli a wound he cannot name and a reflex to over-invest in keeping other people's medical devices alive. [reveal: exact in-story timing is an open plot decision]

## Secrets

- He concealed the worsening of his heart and rationed his own care, so the household money kept going to parts, to the unpaid water work, and to closing the gap in Eli's scholarship, telling Ruth he was "fine, just slow lately." From: Ruth and Eli. Cost of exposure, while he lived: they would have stopped him and spent the money on him, which he could not allow. [reveal: exact in-story timing is an open plot decision]
- He kept doing municipal water repairs off the books after the city stopped paying and stopped authorizing it, quietly, because the alternative was a neighborhood with no clean water. From: the city, what was left of it. Cost: nominal legal risk no one was enforcing; really a private defiance he never named as one. [behavior-only] (proposed)

## Role and Series Potential

His function is to be the origin of Eli, present in the protagonist before the protagonist appears. In Chapter 1 he is already on the page, unnamed, in the gesture of the phone held to the window "a thing his father would have done with a transistor radio." [open] He is the human source of the book's central image: a machine that needs no server's yes, owned outright because it never has to ask. That image is, for the author's eyes only and not to be stated on the page, the thematic seed of the first principle of the thing Eli secretly built and buried, that an intelligence should have no permanent owner. [reveal: Book 1] (proposed) Daniel must never be sentimentalized into a folk saint; his lesson should sometimes be wrong, or insufficient, the way hand labor proves insufficient in the manuscript. Series potential: deceased, he stays a relic and a standard Eli measures himself against, surfacing in memory at the moments Eli is tempted to become an owner. His death is also a quiet rhyme with the clinic's administrative deaths, a private instance of the public pattern, available to the story whenever the exact reveal timing is chosen.
