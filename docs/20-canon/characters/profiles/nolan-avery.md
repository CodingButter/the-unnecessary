# Nolan Avery

## Basic Information

**Full name:** Nolan James Avery
**Age:** 64
**Birthplace:** Detroit, Michigan
**Current residence:** Eli’s neighborhood
**Former occupation:** Electrical-grid engineer
**Current occupation:** Community power supervisor
**Primary viewpoint:** No
**Story role:** Infrastructure elder, skeptic, and symbol of retained human knowledge

## Physical Appearance

Nolan is heavyset with a damaged right knee.

He walks with a metal brace he repairs himself.

He has a gray beard, a deep voice, and permanently stained work gloves.

## Background

Nolan spent thirty-five years maintaining electrical systems.

He understands the regional grid more intimately than anyone else in the community.

He distrusts AI because he watched companies replace experienced crews with automated management systems that failed in ways younger engineers did not understand.

## Personality

Nolan is stubborn, generous, proud, and suspicious of abstraction.

He values apprenticeships and practical knowledge.

He resents Eli’s corporate background but recognizes his skill.

## Relationship With Eli

Nolan initially treats Eli as someone who helped destroy the profession Nolan loved.

Over time, they develop mutual respect.

Nolan becomes one of the first people Eli trusts with Morrow’s physical infrastructure.

## External Goal

Keep the lights on long enough to train people who can replace him.

## Internal Need

Accept that preserving old knowledge may require integrating new intelligence rather than rejecting it.

## Greatest Fear

Nolan fears that the community has become dependent on a small number of aging specialists who cannot be replaced quickly enough.

## Moral Boundary

He will not allow critical grid control to become fully automated without a manual fallback.

## Secret

Nolan’s health is deteriorating more rapidly than he tells anyone.

He knows the community may lose him within a year.

## Story Function

Nolan’s mortality adds urgency to Morrow’s creation.

The community is not only losing corporate support.

It is losing the people who remember how the systems work.
