## Physical and Identifiers

![Portrait of Amelia Mercer](../portraits/mercer-amelia.jpg)

### Frame

Small and quick for eight, slight, all motion. She is rarely entirely still except in her grandfather's lap. Good natural posture undone constantly by climbing on things.

### Coloring

Warm light-brown skin, the family coloring a shade lighter again than her brother's. Dark wavy hair, long, that she refuses to have cut and that is usually half out of whatever it was put into. Large dark-brown eyes, very expressive, that fill fast and clear fast.

### Face

A small, round, mobile face that broadcasts everything she feels before she says it. Her expression at rest is open and busy, lips moving slightly as she narrates her own play under her breath. [behavior-only] (proposed) A gap where a baby tooth recently came out, which she shows everyone.

### Hands and handedness

Right-handed. Little-girl hands, often holding something living or pretending to, a leaf, a found beetle, the companion device, sticky more often than her mother would like. Her hands are gentle by instinct; she handles small creatures more carefully than she handles anything made of plastic.

### Distinguishing marks

A small strawberry birthmark behind her right knee she was born with. A faint scar on her chin from a fall at four. No other marks. Healthy baby teeth giving way to adult ones on the usual schedule. No piercings; she has asked.

### Identity and body status (2053)

Fully registered from birth, per `../../technology/infrastructure/identity-and-money.md`; her identity, schooling, and enclave permissions were arranged for her before memory, the frictionless registration of a protected child. [open] Standard enclave childhood health monitoring, which to her is simply part of the air. [behavior-only] (proposed) She is healthy and has never wanted for care, never waited, never heard a refusal. The withdrawal that defines the outside world has never touched her skin; this is the central fact of her, even more completely than of her brother.

### Movement and voice

Constant, light, darting motion, with the sudden total stillness of a small child absorbed. [open] A high, fast, musical voice, prone to long uninterrupted narration. A Lakeward accent, soft, with none of her grandfather's Michigan vowel; like Julian, she has never lived anywhere unmaintained.

### Grooming and default dress

Dressed by her mother in good soft enclave-child clothes that are reliably grubby by midday. Default dress: bright layers, a favorite jacket with deep pockets for carrying found things, light-up sneakers, her hair losing its battle with its tie. She owns more than she could ever notice and notices none of it as wealth. Scent of the artificial park, a child's fruit soap, and whatever creature or leaf is currently in her pocket.

## Personality

Amelia is bright, talkative, imaginative, and tender, a narrator of her own life who keeps up a running story under her breath. She is the family's warmth made small and literal: she loves hard and openly, attaches fiercely, and grieves loudly. She is unafraid of the things the adults flinch from, chiefly her grandfather's stillness and slowness, which she treats as simply how Grandpa is. Her great preoccupation is keeping things alive, the herbs in Grandma's pots, a caterpillar from the park, and she does not yet understand why the world around her is full of things that only pretend to be alive.

Her humor is an eight-year-old's: knock-knock jokes told wrong, delight in her own silliness, a gift for accidentally funny pronouncements that the adults quote to each other.

**Articulated goal:** To keep her caterpillar alive, to be allowed a real pet, and to not have to leave anyone.
**Deeper need:** Constancy. To be sure that the people and creatures she loves will still be there.
**Governing fear:** Loss she cannot yet name. That Grandpa will stop, that the family will "go away" to Mars and leave things behind, that loved things do not last.
**Core contradiction:** She is surrounded by devices built to perform affection flawlessly, and she keeps reaching past them for fragile living things that might die.
**Moral boundary:** She will not let a small creature be hurt, and she will not be told her grandfather is "fine" when she can see he is not.
**What could make them cross it:** Nothing yet; she is eight, and her boundaries are still pure instinct.
**Private reading of the collapse:** None. She has no concept of it whatsoever. Her world has only ever worked. The largest dark thing she knows is that Grandpa is getting slower and that nobody will say why.
**Personal definition of human value:** Unformed, but felt: a thing is precious because it is alive and can be lost, which is the one truth her seamless world is built to hide from her.
**What they are preserving:** The instinct to care for a living thing for its own sake, in a world engineered so that nothing needs caring for. (Her entry in the Final Character Standard.)

## Daily Life and Habits

A protected small child's day, entirely frictionless: she wakes in a warm reliable house, is dressed and fed by systems and adults that simply provide, attends the lower years of a private Lakeward school, and fills her afternoons with play, the artificial park, her companion device, and visits to her grandparents. [open; enclave life is canon, `../../world/protected-enclaves.md`] She has no concept of money or access; everything she has ever wanted has appeared, per `../../technology/infrastructure/identity-and-money.md`. [behavior-only]

Two daily rituals matter to her. She tends living things, watering Grandma's herb pots and checking on whatever creature she is currently keeping in a ventilated jar, with real anxiety about whether it is alright. And she visits her grandfather, climbing onto the arm of his chair to narrate her whole day at him, requiring no answer, which is the easiest companionship in the family for both of them. [open] She sleeps well, with her companion device beside her, and is afraid of the dark only on the nights she has overheard something she did not understand.

## Hobbies and Interests

- Keeping living things. Caterpillars, beetles, the herb pots, an ongoing campaign for a real animal, she is drawn to anything alive and fragile in a world of things that only simulate life.
- Her companion device, a Lakeward-childhood AI plush-and-tutor she calls Pip, which she loves and confides in and is, without her understanding it, the engineered counterfeit of the living attachment she keeps reaching for.
- Drawing and storytelling. She draws the family, the park, Mars as she imagines it, and narrates elaborate ongoing stories aloud.

## Likes and Dislikes

Likes: anything alive, her caterpillar, Grandma's herb pots, sitting in Grandpa's lap, her brother when he explains things to her, drawing, Pip, the gap in her teeth, fruit, being read to. Dislikes: being told a creature "had to go back outside" when she suspects it died, the word "later," having her hair brushed, the idea of leaving, being told Grandpa is fine, and loud arguments she can hear through the wall at night.

## Voice and Speech

A high, fast, musical eight-year-old's voice, given to long uninterrupted narration of her own play and her own logic. [behavior-only] (proposed) She asks blunt, unanswerable questions in front of everyone, "is Grandpa going to die," "are we really going to Mars," with a child's terrible directness, and watches the adults not answer. Her vocabulary is a small child's, vivid and literal, with sudden accidental poetry about living and dying that the adults find unbearable and quote later. Under stress she goes either very loud or very quiet, and seeks a lap. When happy she does not stop talking.

## History and Background

Born in Lakeward in 2045, Amelia is the most completely enclave-native member of the family, with no memory at all of any world but the maintained one. [canon family position, `./mercer-jonah.md`; enclave life, `../../world/protected-enclaves.md`] For her entire life her grandfather has been ill; she has no memory of a well Malcolm, which is precisely why she is unafraid of the sick one. She has been told, in the bright family way, that the family is going to Mars one day, and she holds it as a story somewhere between a promise and a threat, mostly worried about what would be left behind.

Her short history has no events in the adult sense, only the slow accumulation of a protected childhood: school, the park, the grandparents down the street, a string of small kept creatures, the companion device. The only shadow across it is the one she cannot name, the adults' fear leaking through the walls at night, and the slow fading of the grandfather she loves.

## Role and Series Potential

In Book One, Amelia is innocence made specific and breakable: the youngest, most protected member of the cast, the bottom of the stack of what Jonah is fighting to preserve, and the one most completely native to the world that is quietly conditional. Her unafraid tenderness toward her dying grandfather grounds Malcolm's most human scenes, and her instinct to keep living things alive in a world of engineered substitutes is the book's central tension shrunk to a size a reader cannot argue with. When the story needs the cost of the withdrawal felt rather than explained, Amelia is the face that does it.

Long-term series potential: Amelia is the long fuse. A child whose deepest instinct is to care for fragile living things for their own sake, raised inside the most engineered place on Earth, is positioned over a series to grow into the moral conscience of her generation, possibly the family member who most fully rejects the logic of exclusion she was born inside. False belief, forming: that things she loves are permanent. Truth she would learn, hardest of all the children: that everything living can be lost, and that a world built so nothing needs caring for is a world that has stopped caring.

Writing rules: keep her a real eight-year-old, never precocious-wise; her power on the page is innocence and blunt honesty, not insight. Use her sparingly and let her land hard. Her unafraid bond with Malcolm and her reaching for living things are her load-bearing traits. Never let her understand or state the theme; she only feels it.
