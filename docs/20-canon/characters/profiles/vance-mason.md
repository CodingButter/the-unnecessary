# Mason Vance

**Full name:** Mason Vance [canon surname Vance, from "Vance's boy" in Chapter 1]
**Common name:** Mason. In approved prose he is only "Vance's boy" and "the boy." [canon]
**Age at the start of Book One:** 17 [canon] ("seventeen," stated in Chapter 1)
**Birth date:** March 5, 2036 (not listed in `../../timeline/character-birth-dates.md`; invented under Section 6 to fit the canon age of seventeen on October 3, 2053, and tagged for the spine)
**Birthplace:** Greater Detroit, Michigan
**Current residence:** A rented house in Eli's neighborhood, Greater Detroit
**Household:** Lives with his single father, Marcus Vance. His mother is absent from the household.
**Occupation:** Student at the neighborhood program run out of the old elementary, and his father's working partner: he helps with hauling, salvage, and keeping the house running, in the hands-on local economy, per `../../world/social-structure.md`.
**Faction or class:** Everyone Else, per `../../world/social-structure.md`. [canon] (Plainly outside the protected systems: he brings a dead doorbell to a neighbor's bench rather than buy a new one, and pays nothing.)
**Primary viewpoint:** No. He is never a point-of-view character.
**Story role:** Minor walk-on with one on-page scene. The cleanest expression in Chapter 1 of the generation born inside the withdrawal: a seventeen-year-old who has never known reliable systems and whose want, stripped of every feature, reduces to the animal need a doorbell was invented to answer. Someone is here. Let me know.

## Physical and Identifiers

![Portrait of Mason Vance](../portraits/vance-mason.jpg)

### Frame

Five feet nine and still filling out, thin and a little gangly, the build of a working teenager who eats what the household has and works on his feet. Posture is loose and a touch hunched, hands often in his pockets, the unfinished carriage of seventeen.

### Coloring

Fair, sallow complexion like his father's, reddened at the ears and knuckles by the cold. Light brown hair, fine and straight, overlong and pushed out of his eyes, cut at home. Pale gray eyes set close, the same as Marcus's. A faint shadow of first stubble he does not yet shave with any regularity.

### Face

A narrow face. [canon] This is the load-bearing canon detail: Chapter 1 names him as having "his father's narrow face," so the narrowness, and its match to Marcus, is fixed for both. Long and lean-jawed, sharp-cheeked, a thin straight nose, the features pulled close and vertical. His resting expression is a guarded flatness, the same economy of expression his father wears, which on a seventeen-year-old reads as a kid who learned early not to show too much. [canon detail: a "flatness Eli knew and let pass"]

### Hands and handedness

Right-handed. Young working hands, already roughening at the palms from helping his father haul and salvage, the nails short and not always clean, a teenager's hands learning a laborer's wear. He held the doorbell across the bench and took it back fixed; his hands are competent with objects, used to carrying and lifting. They reveal a kid being shaped early into the same physical trade as his father.

### Distinguishing marks

A small healing scab or two on the knuckles, the ordinary damage of a boy who works and plays rough. A faint scar through one eyebrow from a fall as a younger child. No tattoos yet. Straight, uncapped teeth. He is too young to carry much history on his body, which is itself the point: the marks are still few.

### Identity and body status (2053)

Born inside the withdrawal and registered into a system that already meant less than it used to, per `../../technology/infrastructure/identity-and-money.md`. He has a verified identity on paper and almost no institutional access behind it, and he has never known it to be otherwise. [behavior-only] No augmentations, no implants, by economy and by a world where such things are for the protected enclaves he has never seen. Healthy, with the ordinary resilience of seventeen; any real care would come from Lena's community clinic under care-without-a-bill.

### Movement and voice

He moves with a teenager's loose, quick, slightly unfinished gait. A flat working Detroit accent like his father's. His voice is changing-into-settled, quiet and clipped, low-key; in Chapter 1 he speaks in short flat statements, the household register, and does not waste words or affect. [canon, his lines and their flatness]

### Grooming and default dress

Plain, worn, and mended, a near copy of his father's kit a size smaller. Default dress: hooded sweatshirts layered under a secondhand jacket, jeans, worn sneakers or work boots depending on the day, a knit cap. Clean enough, visibly hand-kept. Scent of cold air and whatever the house cooked. No accessories beyond a dead-or-dying phone he still carries. He is presentable on nothing, the way his father keeps him.

## Personality

In his one scene Mason is plain, contained, and matter-of-fact, a kid who brings a broken thing to be fixed and states what he wants with a flatness past his years that Eli recognizes and lets pass. He is not sullen exactly; he is undeceived. He has grown up watching clever machines refuse to do simple things and watching his father work without rest, and he has drawn the unspoken lesson that you ask for the thing itself and nothing more. Under the flatness is an ordinary seventeen-year-old: capable, a little tired, loyal to his father, and carrying a want so basic it is almost tender, that the house be able to know someone is in it.

His humor, when it surfaces, is dry and economical like the rest of him. He does not perform feeling. The closest he comes to longing in Chapter 1 is the simple admission, spoken for himself and his father both, that what they want is for the house to know there is somebody in it.

**Articulated goal:** Get the doorbell working again. Make the house able to tell them someone is there.
**Deeper need:** To know that he and his father are not invisible, that a presence in the house registers, that if someone came, the house would say so, and someone would answer.
**Governing fear:** That the silence is just the world now, permanently. That no chime, no signal, no answer is simply how it is, and that no one is ever coming, and there is nothing strange in that.
**Core contradiction:** He is fluent in the dead cleverness of the machines around him, grew up among features and apps and remote everything, and wants none of it; he distrusts every layer the cleverness wraps on and craves only the single dumb animal function, a bell that rings.
**Moral boundary:** He will not leave his father to carry a thing alone, and he will not take or ask for more than the household needs. He pays his way in work and asks for the thing itself.
**What could make them cross it:** Real hunger or a real threat to his father, and he could take or do something he knows is wrong to protect the two of them, with the same flatness he protects everything else with.
**Private reading of the collapse:** There is no collapse to him, only the world. He has no before. Machines have always waited on a permission that never comes; companies have always been a thing that switched your stuff off and moved on. He does not mourn it because he never had it.
**Personal definition of human value:** Someone who shows up when you ring. Value is presence, the plain fact of a person being there and being able to be known.
**What they are preserving:** The plain fact of presence. That there is somebody in the house, and that it can be known. The door that rings.

## Daily Life and Habits

His days split between the program at the old elementary, where the neighborhood teaches its own now that the district stopped paying for buses and teachers, and helping his father with whatever the work is. [program is canon] He hauls and carries, learns salvage by doing it, tends the battery and the stove, and runs the small errands of a careful poor household, like carrying a dead doorbell to Eli. He eats what the house has, plain and heavy, and trades small labor on the food board. He sleeps like a teenager when he can. His phone is half-dead and reaches almost nothing, and he has never expected it to reach much, so he uses it for the little it still does and does not grieve the rest.

When something in the house breaks past his father's hands, Mason is the one who takes it to the neighbor who can fix it for nothing, which is exactly what Chapter 1 shows: he comes first with the doorbell, hands it across the bench, and takes it back amputated down to its one true function, a bell that rings. [canon]

## Hobbies and Interests

- Pickup ball and rough games with the other kids who cut through the neighborhood on the way to the program, the ordinary physical fun of seventeen.
- Tinkering with salvaged parts alongside his father and, increasingly, with whatever Eli will let him watch at the bench, a quiet pull toward understanding the stranded machines.
- Music and old downloaded video on his half-dead phone, the fragments that still play without a signal, watched and replayed because they ask nothing of a network.

## Likes and Dislikes

Likes: a thing that just works, the program days, ball games in the cold, watching Eli talk a machine down off its own obedience, food in quantity, his father in a good mood after a paid day. Dislikes: a device that wants to ask a company for permission, the way grown-ups talk about a "before" he never had, being treated as younger than the man's work he already does, the cold getting into the house, and the particular silence of a doorbell that will not ring. [the doorbell's silence is canon-grounded; the rest accepted as canon (Decision 056)]

## Voice and Speech

Short, flat, plain statements, the household register he shares with his father. He does not soften or pad. In Chapter 1 his lines are *So can you fix it* and *That's what we want,* delivered with a flatness Eli knew and let pass, and that is the whole of his music: a question with no decoration, an answer with no want showing on top of it. [canon] His vocabulary is ordinary and concrete. He does not perform feeling, which makes the one tender thing under his words, that he wants the house to know there is somebody in it, land all the harder for not being said as a feeling at all. Under stress he would get shorter and quieter, like his father, and reach for something to do with his hands.

## History and Background

Born in Greater Detroit in 2036, into the withdrawal rather than before it: he is the generation that has never known reliable systems. [canon age; the generational reading is the spine's] His father was eighteen when he was born, so the two of them have always been a tight, narrow-faced unit, and his mother left the household at some point he was too young to fully remember. He grew up in the neighborhood program and at his father's side on whatever the work was, learning early to ask for the thing itself and to keep his feelings economical.

By Book One he is seventeen, a competent and contained kid who carries a dead doorbell to the neighbor who can fix it. In Eli's shop he gets the device back stripped of every clever feature it ever had, down to a bell that rings, and that is exactly what he wanted, because the cleverness was never the point. The point was that the house be able to say someone is here. [canon]

## Role and Series Potential

In Chapter 1 his function is exact and quietly devastating: he is the demand-side proof that the withdrawal raised a generation. His repair is the chapter's clearest image of the want under all the dead cleverness, a machine stripped back to the single animal need it was invented to answer. Through him the doorbell stops being a gadget and becomes the household's plea, someone is here, let me know. Book One arc, minor: from the contained kid at the bench toward, if the book follows him at all, a first pull toward learning the stranded machines himself. Long-term series potential: strong for a walk-on. The seventeen-year-old who wants only that the house know someone is in it is a natural apprentice figure for Eli and a clean point-of-view onto the generation that has no before, and could grow into one of the young hands who carry Morrow's work forward, or into a skeptic who has seen too many clever things fail to trust a new one. False belief, if promoted: that wanting more than the thing itself is a kind of weakness. Truth he would learn: that it is allowed to want, and to be answered.

Writing rules: do not make him precocious or a tragic poster child; his power is the flatness, not pathos worn on top. Keep the want under the words, never on them, exactly as Chapter 1 does. Do not let the mentorship with Eli bloom faster than earned. Never give him more affect than the household register allows; the one tender thing he says, he says flatly.
