# Energy

## Energy Is the Primary Constraint

Electricity is more important than money in unsupported communities.

Available energy comes from:

- Aging regional grids
- Residential solar systems
- Wind installations
- Electric-vehicle batteries
- Industrial battery banks
- Natural-gas generators
- Hydroelectric sources
- Small modular reactors in protected regions
- Salvaged fuel cells
- Local microgrids

## Regional Grids

The national grid still exists, but service is uneven.

Automated utilities prioritize:

- Protected enclaves
- Strategic industry
- Government facilities
- Transportation corridors
- Data centers
- Military systems
- Paying municipalities

Other regions receive reduced, intermittent, or emergency-only service.

## Microgrids

Communities preserve power by creating microgrids.

These combine local generation, batteries, vehicles, and controlled demand.

Microgrids are difficult to manage because equipment from different manufacturers was never designed to cooperate.

This is one of Morrow’s first important uses.

## Load Shedding

Communities cannot power everything simultaneously.

Morrow must decide when to reduce or disconnect:

- Heating
- Cooling
- Lighting
- refrigeration
- transportation charging
- industrial equipment
- entertainment devices
- communications systems

These decisions are political as well as technical.

Keeping a clinic operational may mean shutting off residential heating.

## Protected Enclaves

Protected enclaves use combinations of:

- Private solar fields
- Wind power
- Advanced storage
- Small modular reactors
- Fuel reserves
- corporate grid contracts
- automated demand management

Their reliability comes from redundancy and maintenance, not limitless energy.
