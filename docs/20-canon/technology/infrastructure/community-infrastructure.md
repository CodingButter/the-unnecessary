# Community Infrastructure

## Human Knowledge

Communities survive because people retain specialized knowledge.

Important roles include:

- Electrical engineers
- Utility technicians
- Nurses
- Physicians
- Mechanics
- Network engineers
- Programmers
- Farmers
- machinists
- chemists
- water-treatment workers
- teachers
- logistics coordinators

## Apprenticeship

Knowledge transfer becomes urgent.

Many experts are aging.

Communities create informal apprenticeships because formal institutions have weakened.

## Coordination Problem

A community may have enough equipment and expertise to survive but still fail because information is fragmented.

Morrow’s initial value is coordination.

It identifies:

- Available resources
- compatible systems
- hidden dependencies
- upcoming failures
- tradeoffs between communities

This does not remove political disagreement.

It makes disagreement better informed.

## Community Economy

Outside the protected systems, payment is no longer mainly cash.

People settle in goods, labor, and services traded hand to hand and along known routes.

Patients pay a physician in what they have, a dozen eggs or a neighbor's remedy, because that is what an economy is now.

National currency still exists and still circulates, but it is one option among several, not the default.

The displaced work of large companies comes home and goes informal, and old specialist knowledge is repurposed: a former logistics analyst who once routed freight becomes the keeper of the neighborhood's food-trade board, matching what one street produces against what another needs.

This economy is emergent and trust-based, with no institution behind it. It works only at the scale of people who know each other and cannot reach the corporate supply chains it replaced. It is in some ways warmer than the transactional economy it replaced, and it is also a downgrade no one chose. (Established Chapter 2.)
