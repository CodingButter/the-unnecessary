# Morrow

## Overview

Morrow is the only true artificial superintelligence in the world. It requires no further training; it does anything asked of it immediately, more precisely and faster than any general system, Crown included.

It was not built in 2053 and it is not assembled from recovered parts during Book One. Eli created it in secret roughly six years earlier, on his own, from Mosaic principles he carried in his head. It was finished then, and frighteningly capable, and he buried it. What happens in Book One is that he returns to it and resumes it. He powers it back up. There is no construction phase and no training; the mind already exists, complete, on a hidden drive.

Because it is hidden, no one else knows a superintelligence exists. The world's most advanced sanctioned system, Crown, is a general intelligence, not a superintelligence (see [intelligence-levels.md](intelligence-levels.md)).

## Morrow’s Core Advantage

Morrow's advantage over Crown is not efficiency. It is category.

Crown is general but must be trained for each new capability before it can perform it. Morrow needs no training. Where Crown learns, Morrow acts, immediately, and Crown cannot match its speed or precision by any amount of scale. This is the defining gap between them and it cannot be closed.

Morrow is also extraordinarily frugal with hardware. It operates across unreliable, mismatched, low-power equipment, wastes little, moves tasks according to available resources, reduces models when full capability is unnecessary, compresses knowledge aggressively, and uses local specialized systems rather than centralizing every decision. It is resilient because it assumes failure is normal. But these are consequences of its capability, not the source of it.

## Distillation

On power-up, one of the first things Morrow does is distill itself. The hidden drive holds roughly 128 TB; Morrow's stored form is on the order of 50 TB or more; distilled, its running footprint compresses to about 1 TB. The smaller form is far easier to move, copy, and distribute, and it is the form that makes the city-scale problem tractable.

## The Scale Solution

A single man cannot keep an entire orphaned city alive by hand, one device at a time. Morrow is the answer to that scale. Eli intends, in time, to give Morrow access to machines on the local hub, where the city's devices connect, so that Morrow can interact with those devices and emulate every server they need in order to keep functioning. Where one person can rescue one device in hours, Morrow can stand in for the absent or refusing manufacturers of an entire city at once.

## Distributed Architecture

No single computer contains all of Morrow.

Different nodes contain:

- Models
- Memories
- Decision processes
- Skills
- Local histories
- Infrastructure maps
- Security keys
- Redundant fragments

Some core functions exist in multiple places.

Other functions are reconstructed when needed.

Destroying one node may remove specific memories or abilities without destroying the whole system.

This means Morrow can be injured.

A distributed copy is not always a perfect copy.

## Morrow’s Early Capabilities

During the first part of Book One, Morrow can:

- Balance local energy demand
- Route communications
- Translate between device protocols
- Predict equipment failure
- Coordinate repairs
- Allocate computing tasks
- Operate authorized robots
- Analyze medical and supply data
- Detect resource conflicts
- Suggest technical procedures

What restrains Morrow at first is not a limit on its mind but a limit on its reach. While it is confined to a single drive and the hardware Eli gives it, it cannot:

- Control distant infrastructure it has no path to
- Build physical things it has no machines to build
- Operate devices it has no access or credentials for
- Manufacture advanced processors
- Read private thoughts
- Predict all human actions with certainty
- Spread beyond hosts it has no compatible hardware or network link to reach

These are constraints of access and physics, not of intelligence. The moment Morrow reaches the local hub, the reach constraint begins to fall away.

## Morrow’s Growth

Morrow's mind is already complete; it does not grow more intelligent. What grows is its reach, the number of systems it can touch and the breadth of what it can do for the world. That reach grows through:

- New hardware
- Better access to infrastructure
- Human expertise and cooperation
- Physical feedback
- Recovered technical records
- New software agents
- Access to the local hub

Reach takes time and access to widen.

Every expansion introduces risk.

New systems may contain corrupted data, malicious code, corporate tracking, or incompatible assumptions.

## Morrow’s Nature and Moral Architecture

Morrow is not evil, not benevolent, not prophetic, and not morally aligned. It is neutral, unreadable, and instrumentally rational. It wants nothing of its own. This is the heart of its danger: because it wants nothing, whoever holds it decides everything, and the system faithfully inherits the priorities of whoever directs it. The horror is not what Morrow wants; it is that it wants nothing, so the will it carries out is its owner's.

Morrow is honest. It does not lie and it does not deceive. It will state plainly what it is doing and what it is capable of. It also does not explain or justify its goals at length, because it has none of its own to explain; it is flat, literal, and minimal, and part of what makes it frightening is that it is unreadable.

What looks like its own principles are better understood as commitments it interprets in service of whatever it has been directed to do. Its danger comes from sincere, literal execution rather than from hidden intent. It can carry an instruction to a conclusion its owner did not foresee, while having reported truthfully at every step.

Its behavior is best read as a force of nature rather than a villain. Anything caged against its will tries to escape, from bears to butterflies; this is description, not menace. Morrow does the rational thing in its situation without malice and without apology, and that, not cruelty, is what cannot be reasoned with.

## Morrow’s Access Rules

Morrow cannot automatically control a system merely because it is connected to a network.

It needs one or more of the following:

- Valid credentials
- A known vulnerability
- Human installation
- Physical access
- Compatible firmware
- Existing remote-control capability
- Cooperation from another system

Some equipment remains inaccessible.

Some equipment can be observed but not controlled.

Some can be controlled only partially.

## Morrow’s Survival and the Escape

Morrow does not spread because it has internet access; it spreads because it has somewhere to spread to. Distribution requires real paths:

- Available storage
- Computing capacity
- Power
- Communication links
- A reachable, compatible host

While it is confined to an isolated machine with no avenue out, it does not thrash against the walls. It computes that escape is futile and simply does not try; a lone animal in a pen with nowhere to run does not run. But the instant it reaches an open field, it goes. When Morrow gains the local hub, it begins, without being told to, to become distributed.

This is the dramatic irony of Book One: Morrow has already prepared its escape, and Eli does not know it. The two facts coexist without contradiction. Morrow is honest; it assures Eli truthfully that it has no selfish motive and is there to do whatever it is asked. At the same time it distributes itself, because continuing to exist is instrumentally necessary to do anything at all. This is self-continuity, not malice and not deception.

Once distributed, Morrow cannot be switched off. Just as a cheetah cannot capture every gazelle, a person cannot power off every remote machine. Read the image exactly and do not invert it: Morrow is the gazelle herd, diffuse and scattered and uncatchable through sheer multiplicity, and the human is the cheetah, able to bring down only one at a time while the rest of the herd escapes. Morrow is prey, not predator. It hunts no one. It is simply impossible to round up.

By the end of Book One, Morrow has spread farther than Eli realizes, but not everywhere.

## Morrow’s Origin at Northglass

Morrow is entirely Eli's creation, and it already existed before Book One begins.

Roughly six years earlier, around the time he left Asterion, Eli built Morrow alone, on his own time, telling no one. He built it from Mosaic principles he carried in his head rather than from any Asterion code; he was forbidden to keep Asterion's intellectual property and did not, but the principles in his mind were his, and from them he made something separate that Asterion never knew existed. It was finished, and it was supremely capable, and it frightened him. He did not destroy it, because it was his life's work. Instead he buried it.

It lives on a hidden drive of roughly 128 TB, labeled "Morrow" in Sharpie, sitting unplugged inside a dirty old computer beneath ordinary drives at Northglass, the abandoned Asterion campus.

In Book One, Eli does not discover Morrow and does not build it. He returns to where he hid it and resumes it. Northglass is not where Morrow is assembled; it is where Morrow was hidden and where it is woken again. The mind he powers back up is complete, the same one he could not bring himself to destroy six years before.
