# Formatting Conventions

## Chapter Headings

Recommended format:

```markdown
# Chapter 1

## No Signal
```

The final publication format may change later.

## Dates and Locations

Do not place date and location labels at the beginning of every chapter unless later testing shows they are needed.

The prose should usually establish time and place naturally.

## Numbers

Spell out small numbers in ordinary prose when natural.

Use numerals for:

- Measurements
- technical readings
- percentages
- dates
- times
- model numbers
- system values

Example:

> The battery held at 17 percent.

## Time

Use a consistent style.

Recommended prose format:

- 6:14 a.m.
- 11:47 p.m.

## Italics

Use italics for:

- Rare direct thoughts
- titles when appropriate
- emphasis only when necessary
- words presented directly by a screen when formatting benefits clarity

Do not italicize every interface message.

## Interface Text

Short system messages may appear as separate lines or quoted text.

Keep formatting readable and consistent.

Example:

> SERVICE RESTORATION NOT SCHEDULED

Avoid elaborate faux-terminal formatting unless the technical interface itself matters.

# Names and Terminology

Use established names consistently.

## Artificial Intelligence

- Crown
- Morrow
- Mosaic

Do not constantly place “the” before Crown or Morrow unless grammar naturally requires it.

## Organizations and Locations

- Asterion
- Northglass
- Lakeward
- Aurelia Initiative

## Population Language

Avoid using a single universal term for unsupported humanity.

Use local or institutional language appropriate to context.

## Mars

Do not refer to Mars as humanity’s ark unless a character or promotional campaign uses the term.

The narration should not adopt Asterion’s marketing language uncritically.

# Profanity

Use profanity naturally and selectively.

Frequency should vary by character.

It should increase under:

- pain
- fear
- anger
- loss of control

Do not use profanity merely to make the novel feel mature.

Kade rarely swears.

Sera almost never does in professional settings.

June may use it casually.

Eli uses it more during physical frustration than emotional confrontation.

Lena uses it sparingly, which gives it force.

# Copy-Edit Consistency Ledger

> Owned and maintained by the **copy-editor**, kept here as part of this file so the
> project has one house style sheet rather than two. This ledger pins the mechanical
> micro-decisions so the same word is never re-litigated and never drifts chapter to
> chapter. It is a record, not a re-ruling: the conventions above (numbers, time,
> italics, names, and terminology) already rule those questions, and the ledger defers
> to them rather than repeating them. Where this guide is silent (a borderline spelling,
> a one-off compound, a recurring coined term), the copy-editor pins the most defensible
> default here under Decision 060, with the chapter that prompted it. If an entry ever
> conflicts with a Style Guide file or a canon bible, the more authoritative source wins
> and the conflict is surfaced, never averaged.

## How to read this ledger

- Each entry is a pinned decision: the term or rule, the house treatment, and a short
  why (or the convention above it follows). Entries grow from real chapters; when a
  draft forces a new mechanics decision, the copy-editor adds it here and dates it.
- The audio agents (voice-designer, audiobook-director, live-narration-director) read
  this ledger for consistent spelling, capitalization, and term treatment. Where a
  term's pronunciation is load-bearing, the entry says so in the pronunciation column.
