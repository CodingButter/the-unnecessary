# Emotional Writing

## Restraint

Do not label every emotion.

Avoid repeatedly writing:

- He was angry.
- She felt afraid.
- He was overwhelmed with guilt.
- She could not believe it.

Express emotion through:

- Attention
- physical behavior
- avoidance
- word choice
- decisions
- memory
- silence
- altered routine

## Physical Signals

Use specific signals rather than generic ones.

Avoid overusing:

- clenched fists
- pounding hearts
- held breath
- narrowed eyes
- jaws tightening
- stomachs dropping
- blood running cold

These can appear, but not as default emotional shorthand.

Prefer character-specific reactions.

Eli may recheck a connection he already verified.

Jonah may become more charming.

Lena may lower her voice.

June may become formal.

Mara may stop using institutional language.

Kade may allow a pause to remain unfilled.

## Tears

Use sparingly.

Crying should not be the automatic proof of emotional intensity.

## Trauma

Do not use traumatic memories as decorative flashes.

A memory should affect:

- perception
- choice
- relationship
- current conflict

# Moral and Philosophical Content

## Dramatize Before Explaining

Themes should first appear as decisions.

Examples:

- Eli must choose whether to shut off residential heat to preserve the clinic.
- Morrow withholds information to prevent violence.
- Kade offers six seats instead of arguing abstractly about human value.
- Mara demands fairness only after exclusion reaches her family.

## Avoid Authorial Verdicts

The narration should not tell the reader exactly who is correct.

It may reveal consequences and contradictions.

Characters may judge one another.

The story itself should permit tension to remain unresolved.

## No Debate-Club Dialogue

Philosophical conversations must still involve:

- A practical goal
- unequal power
- emotional history
- immediate stakes
- something one speaker is hiding

If two characters could exchange positions without changing the scene, the dialogue is too abstract.
