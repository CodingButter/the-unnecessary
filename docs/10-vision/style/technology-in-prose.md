# Technology in Prose

## Explain Through Need

Technical explanation should answer a question the scene has already made urgent.

The reader should first understand:

- What is failing
- Who may be harmed
- Why an obvious solution will not work

Then explain only the technical details needed to understand the decision.

## Three-Layer Technical Rule

When presenting a technical problem, establish:

### 1. Human Consequence

What happens to people if it fails?

### 2. Physical Cause

What hardware, software, power, network, or access problem is responsible?

### 3. Limitation

Why can the characters not fix it immediately?

Example:

- Human consequence: The clinic’s respiratory systems will stop.
- Physical cause: Their control units require remote authentication.
- Limitation: Bypassing the check disables calibration records and creates medical risk.

## Avoid Lecture Dialogue

Characters should not explain technology to one another when both already understand it.

Weak:

> “As you know, Nolan, the microgrid connects residential solar panels to electric vehicle batteries.”

Stronger:

> “The cars can carry the clinic for four hours,” Nolan said. “Five if nobody turns on a stove.”

## Use Disagreement to Explain

Technical exposition becomes stronger when characters disagree about what matters.

Eli may explain how a system works.

Lena asks whether it is safe.

Talia asks who decides.

Nolan asks how it can be repaired manually.

June asks whether the restrictions can be bypassed.

Each question reveals a different dimension without requiring a lecture.

## No Magical Interfaces

Avoid vague actions such as:

- Hacking the network instantly
- Typing rapidly and gaining control
- Uploading Morrow everywhere
- Overriding security without preparation
- Solving physical problems entirely through software

Show credentials, access points, hardware, power, delay, testing, and risk when relevant.

Not every process needs full technical detail, but the underlying constraint must feel real.

# Worldbuilding Exposition

## The Reader Does Not Need Everything Immediately

Introduce information when it affects:

- A decision
- a danger
- a relationship
- a mystery
- an expectation

Do not explain the entire Labor Break in Chapter One.

Show its consequences first.

## Historical Context

Use brief references tied to viewpoint.

Eli may remember what Northglass looked like when staffed.

Jonah may compare present access to the first years of Lakeward.

Lena may remember when hospital allocation changed.

Avoid long neutral history passages.

## Official Language

Corporate and government notices should use calm, professional language.

The wording should reduce human abandonment to administrative necessity.

Example tone:

> Full regional restoration is no longer supported under current service-continuity thresholds.

Do not make notices openly cruel.

Their politeness is part of the horror.
