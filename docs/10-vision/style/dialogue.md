# Dialogue

## General Rule

Dialogue is action.

Every speaker should want something:

- Information
- agreement
- permission
- reassurance
- control
- forgiveness
- delay
- concealment
- escape
- recognition

Characters should rarely state their complete emotional truth directly.

## Subtext

The literal topic and emotional topic should often differ.

Example:

Jonah may discuss Asterion resources while actually asking Eli to accept the social system Jonah depends on.

Lena may question Morrow’s data access while actually questioning whether Eli can be trusted with authority again.

Kade may offer seats on Mars while forcing Eli to decide who counts.

## Avoid Perfect Speeches

Characters should not deliver polished thematic essays during stressful conversations.

They may:

- interrupt
- evade
- correct themselves
- choose safer language
- become overly precise
- change the subject
- answer a different question

Kade is the most capable of sustained polished argument, but even his dialogue should remain responsive to the scene.

## Dialogue Tags

Prefer:

- said
- asked
- replied

Use descriptive tags rarely.

Avoid:

- hissed
- exclaimed
- intoned
- declared
- quipped
- retorted

When emotion is clear through words and action, a tag is unnecessary.

## Adverbs

Avoid using adverbs to explain how dialogue should be understood.

Weak:

> “That is not what happened,” Lena said angrily.

Stronger:

> Lena set the tablet facedown. “That is not what happened.”

## Names in Dialogue

People do not constantly use each other’s names.

Jonah uses names more frequently because it is part of his social style.

Others should use them when:

- gaining attention
- increasing intimacy
- expressing warning
- making a plea
- creating distance
