# Global Continuity: World State at Story Start

> No manuscript chapters have been approved. These files contain pre-draft starting conditions only.

This file records the state of the world at Book One time zero, derived strictly from approved world canon and the pre-book 2053 timeline. It captures conditions that are true at or before the opening of the story. It does not record the opening crisis beats of Act One, the construction of Morrow, or any later plot event. Those are not yet drafted and are not established continuity. Where the pre-book timeline contains forward-looking warnings about what may happen later, those warnings are recorded below only as states of knowledge or unresolved threads, never as events that have already occurred.

## Year and Era

The year is 2053. Book One opens in early October 2053 within the final months of that year, after the month-by-month conditions described in the pre-book 2053 timeline through September.

The date is distant enough that artificial superintelligence, mature robotics, and autonomous Martian construction already exist. It remains close enough that the physical world still resembles the present day. Most people alive remember the 2020s. Older people remember when the internet, electricity, transportation, medical systems, and consumer services were assumed to be permanent. Younger people have grown up watching those systems slowly disappear.

The world has not ended in an apocalypse. The old social order is ending because artificial intelligence succeeded, and the abundance it created belongs to too few people. Deterioration is administrative before it is physical. People do not wake up to discover that civilization has ended. They receive a polite notice explaining that civilization will no longer be servicing their area.

## Social Structure

There are no universally accepted names for the social groups, and the world has not reorganized itself into clearly labeled castes. For planning purposes the population can be understood through three broad conditions. These labels rarely appear in narration.

### The Gatekeepers

The highest level consists of the people who control the systems required to construct and operate the autonomous future: founders of major artificial intelligence companies, space transportation companies, robotics manufacturers, energy networks, automated mining systems, advanced manufacturing firms, and private infrastructure providers, plus a small number of political partners, major shareholders, military allies, and family members. Their importance comes from ownership and control, not income alone. They control launch systems, the machines constructing Mars, the intelligence coordinating those machines, and the private energy systems sustaining elite enclaves on Earth. They decide which contracts continue, which communities receive support, and which individuals are allowed into their future. They are not united by a formal government. They cooperate because their companies and ambitions depend on one another, and they also distrust each other.

### The Protected Wealthy

Beneath the Gatekeepers are wealthy families, executives, investors, political intermediaries, senior professionals, early adopters, celebrities, and former public officials. They possess enough wealth to avoid the immediate consequences of mass displacement and they live in protected enclaves. They have private power systems, dependable communications, maintained roads, advanced healthcare, food delivery, autonomous security, and access to premium artificial intelligence. They do not control the systems supporting them. Their security depends on contracts, relationships, ownership stakes, and the continued interest of the Gatekeepers. Most believe they will eventually be invited to Mars. Very few possess an enforceable guarantee. Their greatest fear is discovering that they belong outside the walls.

### Everyone Else

The majority of humanity receives no shared name and still identifies by nation, city, neighborhood, culture, family, religion, occupation, or community. Some journalists call them the left behind. Government agencies use phrases such as low-service regions, non-priority districts, unsupported municipalities, or economically inactive zones. None of these labels is universal. These people live inside an increasingly degraded version of the world they already knew. They still occupy normal houses, apartments, suburbs, and cities, drive aging cars, use old smartphones, watch television, and visit schools, stores, churches, clinics, and municipal buildings. The systems supporting those places become less reliable each year. Their civilization is not replaced by a visibly new one. It is frozen and slowly eroded.

## Government and Corporate Power

Governments still exist. Their authority is weakened by dependence on private infrastructure.

The most powerful corporations no longer require mass human employment, and after a short interval they no longer require mass consumption either. They exchange energy, materials, transportation, manufacturing, medicine, software, and infrastructure with one another, and their automated systems maintain the needs of their owners and protected associates.

Asterion is the dominant artificial intelligence corporation and the owner of the most powerful existing superintelligence. The capability state of that superintelligence is recorded in the technology-state continuity files rather than here.

## Infrastructure Decline

The collapse is uneven. Two neighborhoods separated by only a few miles may experience completely different levels of civilization. One community preserves electricity because several retired utility engineers live there. Another maintains a local internet network using abandoned fiber and salvaged routing equipment. A hospital continues operating because former technicians bypass the manufacturer licensing systems, while a nearby hospital closes because no one knows how to do the same. One town maintains water treatment, another loses it. One apartment complex organizes shared battery storage, another burns after its unsupported electrical-management software fails.

The problem is not always physical scarcity. The wider world still contains enormous amounts of technology. The companies responsible for maintaining, authenticating, updating, and connecting that technology have withdrawn. A machine may function perfectly but refuse to operate because its license server is offline. A cellular tower may remain intact but have no company willing to power it. A hospital scanner may be usable but unable to verify its software subscription. A fleet of delivery robots may sit untouched because its central coordination platform was discontinued. Modern civilization is filled with functional objects that no longer have permission to function.

Knowledge has become more valuable than traditional wealth. People who understand power grids, water systems, communications, medicine, electronics, agriculture, and mechanical repair are essential to their communities.

### Greater Detroit conditions through September 2053

The pre-book 2053 timeline establishes the following conditions as already true at story start. They are recorded here as standing conditions, not as scenes:

- In January 2053 the regional power provider introduced automated priority tiers. Eli's neighborhood is classified below protected, industrial, and government zones.
- By April 2053 part of an abandoned cellular system in the region had been restored using documentation, giving the local network limited cellular function that depends on power.
- In September 2053 the regional internet service provider announced that it would stop restoring full service to several unsupported neighborhoods. As of story start, the local internet provider in Eli's neighborhood operates only a few days each week.
- In September 2053 Asterion detected increasing unauthorized use of abandoned infrastructure around Northglass, and its monitoring of the region has begun.

The pre-book timeline also records warnings that point past time zero, for example that the local grid may become unmanageable and that several clinic systems will lose support before the end of the year. Those are states of awareness, not events that have occurred, and are tracked as unresolved threads rather than established continuity.

## Enclave Status

Protected enclaves are not futuristic utopias. They resemble polished versions of wealthy communities from the 2020s. The grass is maintained, the streetlights work, the roads are repaired, homes receive high-speed internet, private clinics possess current equipment, and autonomous security patrols watch entrances and boundaries. The difference is reliability. Outside the walls people spend increasing time maintaining the systems required to survive. Inside, the wealthy still expect those systems to remain invisible.

There is no single worldwide protected district. Hundreds exist. Some cooperate, some compete. Some are owned by corporations, others are financed by groups of wealthy residents. To many inhabitants they are temporary homes, because the residents believe their real future is being built on Mars. Their belief that this protection is permanent is not, in canon, true, but at story start the residents hold that belief.

## Location Baselines

### Greater Detroit (primary setting)

The story takes place primarily in Greater Detroit. During the automation boom the region saw major investment in robotics, manufacturing, energy storage, data centers, autonomous vehicles, and advanced logistics, and its old automotive infrastructure made it ideal for the new industrial economy. For several years it appeared to be experiencing a second golden age. Then most human workers became unnecessary. Factories remained productive while surrounding communities lost income. Companies consolidated. Facilities closed or became fully autonomous. Public infrastructure deteriorated. The region now contains ordinary neighborhoods, partially functioning municipalities, abandoned technology campuses, autonomous industrial facilities, and protected wealthy enclaves within driving distance of one another. The Great Lakes make the region strategically important for water, agriculture, transportation, and energy potential.

An informal neighborhood network exists. Several nearby communities have created informal agreements to share electricity, technical knowledge, medicine, food, communications, and labor. The network has no formal government. Each neighborhood retains its own leadership and priorities. The alliance is fragile. Some communities cooperate and others hoard resources. At story start this network exists but is not yet coordinated by any single system.

### Eli's neighborhood

An ordinary residential neighborhood in the Detroit metropolitan area. It has no special dystopian name and residents use the name it had before the collapse. Houses, apartments, schools, stores, garages, churches, clinics, and streets remain recognizable. The changes are subtle but constant. Several streetlights no longer work. Cellular coverage disappears during power shortages. The local internet provider operates only a few days each week. A closed pharmacy still contains empty automated dispensing systems. Electric vehicles are used as neighborhood battery storage. A former public library now hosts a local server and communications hub. People still attempt to live normal lives, but normality requires increasing amounts of technical skill and collective labor.

### Lakeward (protected enclave)

A wealthy district north of Detroit and one of many protected enclaves. It includes gated residential areas, private schools, medical facilities, artificial parks, autonomous security, and local energy generation. Its streets resemble the best-maintained communities of the 2020s, and residents do not experience the daily deterioration visible outside. It is not controlled by a single government. Its services are maintained through corporate contracts and resident ownership agreements. The people living there believe their protection is permanent, although in canon it is not. Jonah Mercer and his family live there.

### Northglass (abandoned campus)

Asterion's abandoned Great Lakes research campus. At story start it is abandoned. It still holds Asterion server facilities, robotics laboratories, prototype hardware, and other equipment that mostly cannot function because it depends on discontinued authentication services. As of September 2053 Asterion has detected increasing unauthorized use of abandoned infrastructure around Northglass and has begun monitoring the region. Northglass is not yet in use as a base for any new system at time zero.

### Mars sites

Mars is not yet populated by the elite when the first novel begins. For years autonomous systems have prepared it. The planet is inhabited primarily by machines. Construction lights move across an empty landscape, excavators work without operators, underground habitats expand, and agricultural chambers grow food for people who have not arrived. Mars appears in Book One through corporate footage, system telemetry, private meetings, and occasional viewpoint scenes connected to the Asterion leadership. The public-facing project is the Aurelia Initiative, described publicly as a mission to preserve humanity and establish a second civilization, and in canon it is private infrastructure controlled by the people whose companies built it. Admission to Mars has no formal public application, no objective scoring system, and no clearly defined threshold. It depends on ownership, relationships, affection, loyalty, and personal preference.

## What Is Functional and What Has Failed at Story Start

The following summarizes the baseline operational state of the ordinary world outside the enclaves. Detailed system-by-system technology states live in the technology-state continuity files.

### Functional, with conditions

- Physical infrastructure largely remains in place: roads, buildings, towers, clinics, homes, and the equipment inside them.
- Electricity in Eli's neighborhood exists but runs on an automated low-priority schedule, below protected, industrial, and government zones.
- Limited local cellular service exists, restored from an abandoned system earlier in 2053, and depends on available power.
- A local internet provider operates a few days each week.
- A former public library functions as a local server and communications hub.
- Electric vehicles function as neighborhood battery storage.
- The informal neighborhood network functions as a loose, fragile cooperative arrangement.
- Inside protected enclaves such as Lakeward, power, communications, roads, healthcare, food delivery, and autonomous security remain reliable.

### Degraded

- Cellular coverage in ordinary neighborhoods disappears during power shortages.
- Internet service in unsupported neighborhoods is intermittent and, per the September 2053 announcement, will no longer be fully restored.
- Many machines remain physically intact but cannot operate because their license, authentication, or coordination services are offline or discontinued.

### Failed or withdrawn

- Manufacturer maintenance, authentication, updates, and connectivity for much abandoned technology have been withdrawn.
- A neighborhood pharmacy is closed, leaving empty automated dispensing systems behind.
- Northglass operates as an abandoned campus whose equipment mostly cannot function.
- Full internet service to several unsupported neighborhoods is no longer being restored.

## Boundaries of This Baseline

The Act One opening events are not recorded here as established continuity. At story start, the service-termination notices that open the novel have not yet been delivered as scenes, the clinic authentication loss has not yet occurred, no character has yet entered Northglass to recover Asterion hardware, and Morrow does not yet exist. Northglass is abandoned. The neighborhood network is uncoordinated. These are the conditions from which the first drafted chapter will proceed, and they are tracked forward from this baseline rather than treated as already drafted.
