# Chapter 3: Borrowed Time

The door settled back toward its frame, slow and reluctant, swelled too tight to seat, and stopped where Lena had left it, a hand's width short, a slice of the work light falling out past its edge across the floor of the hall. Lena's feet went off down the corridor. The clinic's small sounds, a cough two rooms over, the tick of a baseboard giving up the last of its rationed heat, the low talk at the front counter, all of it drawing back to a thin murmur past the gap, and it was just the closet and the clock and him.

He stood a moment and let the room come to him before he touched anything, the way his father had stood in a stranger's basement before he laid a hand on the pump, listening first. The work light hung off its hook and threw his shadow long and bent across the window somebody had painted shut years back. The air was the stale cold of a year unopened, dust and old electronics and that faint sweet animal smell, a mouse gone somewhere in the wall. On the steel rack the clinic's own server sat squat under its single amber light, a decade older than anything else in the building, kept alive all this time for the records and nothing harder. The light burned steady. It was telling the truth, which was that the box was running, and that running was the whole of what it could promise.

He crouched in front of it the way he crouched in front of everything, and put the deadened fingertip of his left hand flat to the casing first, the old habit, never trusting his eyes about a thing that was cold or hot or live until the part of him that no longer felt much had confirmed it. Cool metal. They were his father's hands, scarred and burn-marked from the trade, doing the thing his father's hands had done, and the rack gave off its small electrical note, the one under hearing that he heard anyway. A light and a board and not much. He had said as much to Lena an hour ago and it had not gotten better for being looked at.

He had walked the three machines before he came back here, and he carried them in his head now the way she carried the beds. Three machines, three different ways of asking a dead company for permission. The scanner in the second room ran a license check at startup, reached out once to be told it was allowed to know what it knew, and sat down if the answer did not come. The drug cabinet checked an authorization before it would unlock a schedule it had not already loaded. The controller down the hall, the one that mattered, phoned home on a cycle, a small handshake every so many minutes, asking again and again across the night whether it was still permitted to keep a man breathing. At midnight the company would stop answering all three, and not one of the engineers who had built them was left anywhere to say what each would do when no answer came.

So you put up something local that answered in the company's place. That was the clean idea, and he had said it out loud to her, and it was true, and it was the easy half. Stand the records box up as the thing the orphaned machines reached for, point each device's call at the closet instead of the sky, catch what it said, learn the shape of the question, and hand it back the yes. He could do that. Capture the traffic, read the protocol off it, raise the endpoints that answered. The trouble was never the yes. The trouble was that a medical device worth its certification did not trust just any box that said yes. It trusted one signature, baked in at the factory, made with a key that had never left the company and never would, and a closet that owned only the public half of that key could check the signature until morning and never once produce it. Which threw him back onto the other front, the slow one, going into each device's firmware by hand to make it stop asking at all: dump the image, find the check, cut it out, write the thing back over a debug port without bricking the one machine a man's night hung on. Hours a device, done right. And under both of them, untouched by either, the thing he had told her plainest. He could forge the permission and never the calibration it had stood in front of, never the pressures, never the years of accumulated say-so that had made the yes mean the man was safe. Forge that, and the machine ran and reported itself fine and might be quietly, confidently wrong.

There had been a night, years back, when a floor of machines the size of a warehouse had thrown a fault that lit half its boards red, and he had sat down in front of it with his coffee going cold at his elbow and worked it slow and unhurried, while the manager stood at his shoulder breathing wrong. The man kept asking how bad it was. Eli kept not answering, because answering was time, and somewhere in the second hour the fault came apart under his hands and the boards went quiet and green. Easy was the wrong word and the only word. It had been easy. He had been very good and very calm and very well paid to be both, and nothing in that whole warehouse had been alive.

He came back to the closet on the same gesture, his hand already moving, and the amber light was still burning, and the man down the hall was still sixty-one and still could not hold a night by himself.

He made the order the way she had made hers. The records box first, toward the local server, while his hands were fresh and the grid was steady and the batteries still had something in them, because that work wanted the clear hours and the clear hours were the early ones. The controller's firmware was the thing he would not leave the building without finishing, whatever it cost the rest. The scanner he could lose; nobody died tonight for a scan that did not run. The cabinet he would reach if there was anything left of him to reach it with. Three machines, hours each, against the hours to midnight and one underpowered box and one set of hands, and the arithmetic did not close no matter which way he ran it. He had known it would not before he crouched down, and he had crouched down anyway, because that was the part of him there was no arguing with.

He opened the bag. The buckle was loud in the cold. He ran the first length of cable out across the floor and brought the end up to the rack and made the connection beside the amber light, and the clock started running in the corner of his mind where it would run now until the hour turned. Faster than he had ever worked. It still might not be enough. He set his hand to the box and started.

---

The resolver came up the way it always came up, in minutes, and lying. He pointed the scanner's name at the closet instead of the sky, so that when the machine in the second room reached out at startup to ask a dead company whether it was still allowed to know what it knew, the question came to him instead. That part was nothing. That part was hanging a sign over a door that read this way now and the traffic believing it. He carried a length of cable and a tap down the corridor and sat on the floor in front of the scanner while it booted, caught the call as it left, brought the capture back to the box, and for the length of one breath, the request scrolling up the panel in the cold cone of the work light, it looked like it might be enough.

Then he read it, and it was the shape he had known it would be, and the easy half closed behind him like a door.

He stood the endpoints up to answer it. He wrote the closet to say, in the company's place and the company's grammar, the small yes the scanner wanted to hear. He raised a certificate authority on the box and signed the certificates the conversation called for and handed them across, the deadened fingertip slow on the fine steps so he turned the finest of them to his right hand. The scanner looked at what he offered the way a man looks at a stranger holding his brother's name, and believed none of it.

The trust was not the closet's to give. The scanner carried the maker's own key burned into it at the factory, the one thing it had been told to believe before it was told anything else, and it would take a certificate signed by that key and by nothing else alive, and the closet did not have it and could not make it. He could stand the right answer in front of the machine until morning. It had been built well enough to know an answer from the maker's answer, and there was one way past a thing built that well, which was to go inside it and cut out the part that cared, and that threw him back across the room onto the firmware he had meant to keep a wall away. The two fronts ran together. The clean line he had drawn between them in the first hour was gone.

He went on anyway, because under the pinning there was a second wall and he wanted it under his hands before he spent the night on it. The machine did not only want a certificate. It wanted an answer to a question it made fresh, a number it sent out and a signature it expected back. The scanner asked it once, at boot. The controller down the hall would ask it again and again all night. And the signature could be written by one key in the world, the maker's private half, the half that had never left the company and never would. The closet held the public half. The public half could check that signature until the sun came up and never once produce it. He sat with it the way you sit with a load-bearing wall. You did not argue with a wall. You found another way to carry the roof or you did not carry it.

The other floor came up on him unasked, on a gesture, the way it had been coming all afternoon. Cool light and certain power and a fault running red across a third of it, and the manager at his shoulder, a good suit gone dark under the arms, pressing the number like a bruise. One point three million, the man kept saying. One point three, and that was only the morning, and if it ran past noon the investors would hear, and once the investors heard the confidence went, and confidence did not come back. How long. Just tell me how long. And Eli, years younger and well paid to be calm, had not looked up from the boards, because looking up was time, and had said it would be fine. It would be fine. And it had been. The fault came apart under his hands inside the hour like a chord resolving to where it had always meant to land, the boards going green in a wave, and the man behind him had made a sound close to a sob.

It would be fine. He had said it, and it had been true, and it had cost him nothing, and the saying had been the whole of what the man needed.

He could not say it now. That was what the memory had come to tell him and would not stop telling. Down the corridor was a chest that could not hold a night, and the sentence that had once been the easy center of him was the one sentence he did not have.

He made the scanner take the local yes in the end, because he had to know the floor of the thing. It cost him hours he could not spare, and then it sat down and ran on a word the closet gave it and reported itself well. For the scanner that was enough. A scan on a forged permission was still a scan, and if it lied a person looked twice and lost a minute. He kept the one small green light only long enough to understand it was worth nothing where it counted.

The yes had never been the hard part. He had said as much to her with his own mouth, and now the box said it back to him in the cold. The permission was a single bit, there or not there, and a single bit he could forge. What the permission had stood in front of did not cross the wire at all. The calibration. The pressures the machine was allowed to make and the ones it was not. The long unglamorous record of the company checking its own work, year on year, that had made the yes mean a body was safe and not only breathing. None of it was in the traffic. None of it was a thing a closet could be taught to say. He could hand a machine the company's word. He could not hand it the company's care, and on the drug cabinet it was the part that mattered, and on the controller down the hall it was the whole of the matter and the only part of it.

The corridor light widened at the door and Lena stood in it, one hand on the frame, not coming in. She did not look at the box. She looked at him.

"Is it working," she said. She had told him once, in this building, yes or the truth, not the comfort. She meant it now the way she meant most things.

He sat back on his heels and took the word that fit instead of the word that was kind. "The easy part's done," he said. "I can make the machines stop waiting on the company. I can make them run." He let it sit, because the rest was what she would carry back down the hall. "The part that keeps the man safe is the part I can't fake. It'll run, Lena. I can't make it true."

She held the frame a breath longer, and whatever she did with it she did behind the face she wore so a frightened person could read no verdict off it. "All right," she said, which was not all right, and went back to her patients, and the door stayed as she left it, open a hand's width on the corridor and the cold.

He let the half-built server stand there glowing, the scanner's useless green among the closet's amber, a thing that could keep a doorbell honest and could not vouch for a lung. He gave himself the one beat he never gave himself, on his heels with the dead fingertip flat to the warm casing of the box and the rack's note higher under the load, a fan somewhere inside it spun up that he had not wanted to hear. The room stayed cold while the machine warmed. Then he pulled his hands off the emulation and the hope of it for the machines that could kill a man, because if he could not build a thing the controller would trust he would go into the controller and try to make it trust nothing, need no one, ask no sky. He had burned the clear early hours learning in his hands what he had carried in his head before he crouched down. It was going on seven. Five hours to the hour that turned. He picked up the bag and went down the corridor toward the firmware and the machine that breathed for a man whose name he had not been given.

---

The room was dark except for the machine. He had thought he would have to ask Lena to clear it for him and he did not; she was off somewhere along her rounds and the door stood open and the bed held its long low shape under a blanket that rose and fell on a count that was not the body's own. He did not look at the shape. You did not look at the part of the job your hands could not reach. You looked at the part your hands were on, and his hands were on the controller, on its stand at the head of the bed, putting out the small even push and release into the dark. It was warm when he laid the dead fingertip to the casing, the particular living warmth of a thing doing work, a warmth that was the current's and the labor's and not the room's. Over the dead status light Lena had taped a card in her square hand. Cycle good. A last clean read and the time of it. The pressures, written out. A doctor's hand standing in for a machine's word, the whole of what he was trying to build tonight made small and human and stuck to a casing with tape, and it shamed him to read it. He found the service port under the lip of the housing, pulled the image off the flash without touching the breathing, and carried it back down the corridor to the cold.

He ran it open on the box. The image came apart into its parts, a loader and a kernel and the company's own code sitting on top, and he went into the code in the disassembler and walked it the way he walked a basement, slow, reading the pipe before he cut it. He found the check inside the first hour, the routine that reached for the sky and sat down if the sky did not answer, and for a breath it was the clean job he had named to himself at three o'clock: find the check, cut it out, write the image back, and the machine would stop asking anyone for permission to keep a man breathing. He cut it out. He built the patched image clean. And then he went looking for the door he would write it back through and found the third wall standing where he had not let himself look for it.

The boot ROM checked the image before it would run a line of it. Down under everything, burned in at the factory and not his to touch, a small unmovable verifier that read the whole of what it was about to run and matched it against a signature, and the signature could be made by the one key in the world he did not have and could not make, the same private half that had turned him back at the certificate and turned him back at the challenge, waiting for him here a third time at the bottom of the machine. A patched image was an unsigned image. An unsigned image did not boot. It did not fail loud. It did not fail safe. It simply did not come up, and the controller that did not come up was a controller a man was not breathing on. There was a way past a wall like that and he knew the shape of it, fault the verifier in the half-instant it read, starve the rail at the exact microsecond it decided, make the check stumble over its own yes. He had done it on things that did not matter. It was days of work to do it once on a part you had never met, days he did not have minutes of, and under it the second bind that did not care how clever he was: writing anything back meant a reboot, and a reboot meant the machine stopped breathing for the length of a boot, on a man whose own lungs would not cover the gap, and if the write was a hair wrong the boot never finished and the gap never closed. He could not practice on the only one there was.

The light shifted at the door. Lena stood in the hand's width of it with her rounds behind her, not coming in, and she did not ask the soft question this time. "The man," she said. "Tonight. Yes, or the truth."

He kept his hands where they were and gave her the truth, because she had told him once in this building that was the only thing she wanted from him. "I can make it run," he said. "Off the company. On its own." He made himself finish it. "I can't make it true. The one way I'd get there tonight, I'd be running him on numbers nothing's left to check. He'd breathe. The machine would tell you he was fine." He looked up then. "It'd be worse than no machine, Lena. I won't do that to him."

She held the frame a moment with that face she wore so a frightened person could read nothing off it. "All right," she said, the way she said it that was not all right, and took the plain shape of it back down the hall to the bed and the card, and that was the last word either of them spent.

He gave the cabinet twenty minutes he could not spare and a yes it would believe and never reached the bottom of it, and left it half-walked, because one machine half-done was not three machines saved and the arithmetic had never once closed.

The other floor came up on him a last time, on a gesture, the way it had come all night, and this time it was the top of it. The manager off the call, the handset down, the boards green behind them in their thousands. We're back up. We're fine. The hand coming down warm on Eli's shoulder, the man so light with relief he was almost laughing, and the thing he said, the thing Eli had carried out of that building and could not put down. This is why you're you, Eli. Everyone else is just another programmer. He had taken it the way he took praise, turned it toward the team and the system, and kept it anyway, every word, for the nights it would be needed. He came back to the closet on the same shoulder, and there was no hand on it now, and the controller was unsolved under his fingers, and nothing in that bright remembered floor had ever been alive.

The corridor light moved once more at the door and he felt rather than saw a shape stand in it, and he did not turn. He had not heard her, or he had, and there was nothing in him to turn with, and afterward he could not have said which. The shape did not speak. It did not come in. After a while the light was just the light again and he had not lifted his head from the work, his shadow thrown long and bent across the painted-shut window, his beard gone uneven under the day, his hands slower than they had been at three and slower than that now.

He came at last to the place the whole night had been walking him toward, the one move left that the clock would still allow, and stopped on the edge of it with his hands open over the keys. He could make the controller run. Not right. Run. He could put the company's yes into it in the company's grammar and strip away in the same stroke the calibration the yes had stood in front of, the pressures it was allowed to make and the ones it was not, the long unglamorous record of a body kept safe and not only breathing, none of which had ever crossed the wire, none of which a closet could be taught to say. The machine would push and release. The card would say cycle good. And somewhere under the count a man would be carried slowly and correctly toward a place the numbers were no longer watching. He took his hands off the keys. He had drawn the line to Lena hours ago with his own mouth and here it was under his fingers, and he held it, because holding it was the only thing left that he could do without lying, and lying was the one repair he had never made.

The clock finished the arithmetic for him the way it had always been going to. He was not going to bring the controller back the right way before the hour turned. He was not going to bring it back the wrong way at all. There were not enough hours and there was not enough of him, and there never had been, and he had known it crouching down at three and crouched down anyway. The man on the other floor had fixed it without breaking a sweat and been called a god for it. The man in the closet sat back on his heels in the cold with his father's spent hands open on his knees, the amber light steady beside him telling the only truth it had, that the box was running, and the small even push and release going on down the corridor on borrowed time, and there was nowhere to put any of it, and he did not try.

---

He had said the night was finished and his hands had not believed him. They went back to the controller's image on their own, the way they went back to everything, because stopping was a decision and his hands did not make decisions, they made repairs, and there was no repair here and they hunted one anyway. He gave the boot verifier the move he had no time for, the fault in the half-instant it read, starving the rail at the microsecond it decided, knowing it was days of work and giving it twenty minutes because twenty minutes was what the clock had left him. It did not come apart. It was not going to come apart. The phone lay face up on the rack beside the amber light and he watched the numbers climb toward the hour out of the corner of the eye that was not on the work. Forty-one. Fifty. The thing did not close. He had known at three o'clock that it would not close, and he had crouched down anyway, and here at the far end of the night was the closing of nothing, exactly the size he had measured it.

The hour turned on the phone with no sound at all. Eleven fifty-nine, and then the colon, and then the zeroes, the same small clean change the face made every minute of every day, made now once and meaning the end of a company's word for three machines in a building it had never heard of. He watched it the way you watch a thing you cannot put your hands on. He had braced, somewhere under the work, for all three to fall over at the stroke. Nothing fell. The rack gave its note. The work light held his bent shadow long across the painted-shut window. The room was exactly as cold as it had been a minute before, on the near side of the hour.

So he went and read them, because not knowing was the one thing he would not leave the building carrying. The scanner stood ready on its useless green. The cabinet down the hall held its gray locked face and answered when he asked it, the doses still behind the drawers, the schedule still loaded. And the controller was warm under the dead fingertip, the small even push and release going on into the dark, Lena's card taped over the dead light in her square hand. Cycle good. All three of them running on the far side of a permission that no longer existed anywhere in the world, and he stood in the corridor between the rooms and understood why, and it was worse than if they had stopped.

Nothing had restarted them. That was the whole of it. Each had made its last call before the hour and been told yes, and was holding that yes the way a held breath holds. The controller would go on sending its handshake into the dark every few minutes and go on breathing on the last yes it had, because a machine already up and running did not halt itself over a check that came back empty; only a cold start would force it to ask fresh and stop on the silence. He knew the scanner cold: bring it back up and the wheel would turn and turn for a server two thousand miles dead and never stop, and it would not boot. The cabinet would come up and keep its loaded schedules and refuse a new order, or lock the drawers and keep the keys, and no one alive knew which. The controller would come back asking a dead company whether it was still allowed to keep a man breathing, and the company would not answer, and the controller would not come back. The first restart killed all three. One outage where the generators lagged. One tripped cord. One reboot somebody gave it meaning to help. He had not saved a machine tonight. He had bought each of them a stay he could not control and handed the length of it to chance, and chance was the one repair his hands had never learned.

He waited for the other floor to come up on the gesture, the bright boards and the cool light and the hand coming down warm on his shoulder, the way it had come at him all night long, and it did not come. There was nowhere left for it to go. The man who had fixed the floor without breaking a sweat and been called more than a programmer for it had run out of road against this cold three feet of closet. Whatever the words had been worth on the bright floor, they were worth nothing here, where the work was real and the thing on it was alive, and the memory seemed to know it at last and went quiet, and left him the room.

The move was still there. It would be there until he walked out, the forged yes he had refused at the keys an hour ago and would refuse again. His hands knew the shape of it the way they knew everything. He did not make it. He had held the line then with the whole of the clock pushing on him, and he held it now with the clock run out, because holding it was the last thing left that he could do without lying, and lying was the one repair he had never made and was not going to make on a body.

He packed down to nothing he could finish. He coiled the cable off the floor and left the records box standing on its single amber light at the job that no longer meant what it had meant an hour before, a closet that could keep a doorbell honest and could not vouch for a lung. He left the controller cycling down the hall, warm, on the card and the borrowed minutes. Then he stood in the swelled door with the corridor cold coming up under it and could not find the thing to tell Lena that was both true and bearable, because there was not one. The machines were alive. The machines were doomed. Both at once, and the night between the two of them belonged to chance now and not to his hands, and he had nothing to put down over it. He went out the way he had come in, and left the amber light burning behind him.

---

The cold had quit being October somewhere in the last hour and gone down to the thing underneath it, and he walked home through it with his hands in his coat and the work still on them, solder-ghost and machine oil and the chill of metal that had been colder than his skin all night. The street gave nothing away. By day you could read the failures off the streetlights, the dead glass marking how far the maintenance had pulled back, block by block, the boundary drawn cold over his head. At one in the morning there was no boundary. The dead lights and the few live ones were all one dark, the whole street honest at last about how far the dark went, and there was no tide in any of it left to read.

A car sat at a curb with its orange cable run flat across the walk and into the side of a house, spending the last of itself into somebody's rooms. One window down the block held the low amber of a place that had budgeted a single lamp and meant to keep it lit till morning. The maples nobody owned let their leaves down into the dark unwatched. Three streets back, in a closet the size of a coffin, the records light was still burning and the small even push and release was still going on for a man whose name he had not been given, alive on borrowed uptime and on nothing his hands had managed, and his mind went back and picked at it the way a tongue goes back to a broken tooth, the firmware, the verifier, the rail, the half-instant it read. There was nothing there. There had been nothing there at three o'clock and there was nothing there now. He made himself stop reading the streetlights that had nothing to say, and let his feet carry him the rest of the way home.

His home was the shop's other half, the two of them one address, and he came in past the bench without putting on the light. The bench was where it would be easiest to go on. He could sit down at it and pull the night back open and work it again from the top, the clean way, with no clock on him, and prove to himself in the dark that it had been losable and not lost by him. It would change nothing in the closet three streets back. He went past it, not because he had made any peace with the night, but because his hands were done, and his hands were the part of him that decided a thing like that, and they did not reach.

The floor was cold through his socks the way it was cold every morning, only it was not morning. The rooms held the silence of a place running on a battery, the small outside noises of a thing still talking to the world all gone, the same silence he had woken into a thousand mornings and would wake into again. He did not eat. He did not wash the night off his hands. He sat down on the edge of the bed and then he was not sitting, he was down, the body folding along the seam it had been folding toward since midnight, the leaden weight of it coming down over the noise in his head and pressing it flat.

He set the phone where his hand could find it, the old habit, the phone that always woke before he did. The screen came up pale and he did not read it. Three streets off in the dark a machine he could not vouch for kept a man breathing on a permission that no longer existed anywhere in the world, and the keeping of it was chance's now and not his, and there was nothing to be done about it between here and the light. That was the whole of what he carried down with him. Sleep took him the way the cold had taken the street, evenly, from the edges in, and the neighborhood lay silent on its stored power all around him, and the night went on without his hands in it.

---

The phone woke before he did, the way it always had, and this once he would have given a great deal for it not to.

His thumb found the glass while the rest of him was still down under the weight of sleep, the habit of years running out ahead of the man. The screen came up pale in the gray of the early light, the way it came up every morning of his life. The floor was cold when he put his feet toward it and did not put them down. The room held the silence of a place running on stored power, the small outside noises a phone made when it was talking to the world all gone, the same silence he had fallen into a few hours back and the same he had woken into a thousand times. For the length of one breath it was only a morning, and he was only a tired man in it.

Then he read it.

*Eli. The power went out in the night. The generators were too slow.*

He read it twice, the way he had read the notice the morning before, the way a man reads a thing whose shape he knew before the words arrived. There was no softening anywhere in it. She had told him once, in that building, the truth and not the comfort, and she had not changed the habit for this.

He did not need the rest of it written. He had left it written himself, on the casing, in her tape and her square hand. Cycle good. He had left the controller warm and cycling on its borrowed minutes, and the man on it sixty-one and unable to hold a night alone, and somewhere out in the dark a line had sagged and the lights had gone, the way he had known they could go, the way no one was paid to hurry them back anymore. The outage had run long past the little backup the controller carried, the battery spending down to nothing in the dark while the generators that should have caught the load lagged. And the machine had done the one thing a stranded machine could do when the power came up under it again. It had restarted. It had come back asking a dead company whether it was still allowed to keep a man breathing, and the company was gone and did not answer, and so the machine had not come back, and the gap had opened, and a chest that could not cover a gap had not covered it.

He lay back down into the cold and did not move.

He had held the line. That was the place his mind went and could not get past. He had held the one line that was his to hold. He had not forged the yes. He had not stood the man on numbers nothing was left to check and called it saving him. He had refused the lie that would have carried him off slowly and correctly while the card said cycle good, and the world had taken him through the gap anyway, on an outage and a generator too slow, because a provider had drawn a line over the neighborhood at midday and stopped treating its dark as anything to answer for. Not his forging. Not his failing. The thing he had warned her of in the daylight with his own mouth, come true in the dark three streets off while he slept.

There was nothing to get up for. That was the whole of it and he lay still inside it. No fault to find, no pipe to read, no board gone red that his hands could walk back to green. His hands lay open on the blanket with nothing in them, the part of him that decided a thing like this, and they did not reach, because there was nowhere to reach. The man had a name he had never been given. He would not have it now. He had been a chest down a corridor and a count that was not a body's own, and then he was an absence the size of a person, and Eli lay under it in the gray light with the stored-power silence all around him and did not try to make it into anything. It was not anything yet. It was only the floor gone out from under the morning, and him in the dark of it, and the cold coming up through the house that had stopped talking to the world in the night.
