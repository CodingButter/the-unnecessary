# Gemini Editorial Critique

## High Severity

-   **category:** style
-   **location:** Multiple paragraphs, notably in Scene 3 (Rounds) and Scene 4 (It Is Down to Him).
-   **issue:** The prose repeatedly lapses into direct thematic summary, explaining the subtext to the reader. This violates the house style's core tenets of "subtext over explanation" and "dramatize before explaining." It pulls the reader out of Lena's immediate experience and into an authorial overview.
-   **suggestion:** Remove the explicit thematic analysis. Trust the concrete details and actions to carry the meaning. Specific examples:
    1.  Location: "...she could not have said, standing there, whether the room was a kindness or an indictment... and she held the two of them at once because they were both true and putting one down did not make the other less so." Suggestion: Cut this summary. End the beat on her hand on Dembélé's shoulder and the unresolved physical reality of the moment.
    2.  Location: The long paragraph in the final scene beginning "She let herself feel the shape of it now... it had become anger at the whole arrangement..." Suggestion: Rephrase this from a summary of the evening's evidence into Lena's direct, present-tense perception in the corridor. Instead of "It had grown all evening, bed by bed, the eggs...", try grounding it in the immediate sensory input: "The eggs on the counter. The woman's apology for a bill that no longer existed. Dembélé's careful columns. It was not the company's notice. It was this. The whole arrangement."
-   **severity:** high

-   **category:** style
-   **location:** Throughout the chapter.
-   **issue:** Frequent use of filtering language ("she could feel," "she watched," "she heard," "she saw," "she knew") that adds narrative distance and weakens the close-third person viewpoint. The style guide requires a close attachment to the character's perception.
-   **suggestion:** Eliminate filtering words and rewrite to state the perception or action directly. Specific examples:
    1.  Location: "...under her palm she could feel the cycle..." Suggestion: "...under her palm the machine cycled, a small even push and release."
    2.  Location: "...she watched the little wheel turn..." Suggestion: "...the little wheel turned..."
    3.  Location: "She heard herself say it and heard the shape of it..." Suggestion: "The words came out, and she registered their shape..."
    4.  Location: "She had known this. She had heard him reach for it..." Suggestion: "This was what he had reached for that afternoon..."
-   **severity:** high

-   **category:** prose
-   **location:** Scene 3, during the triage decision for Mr. Adeyemi.
-   **issue:** The narration tells the reader Lena's decision-making philosophy directly, rather than showing it through her actions. It feels like a summary of her character from the blueprint.
-   **suggestion:** Cut the abstract explanation and root the decision in the immediate, concrete options she is weighing. Instead of "She had learned to tell the difference between a choice with a safe option in it and a choice with only owned options...", show her thinking through the specific risks: "Send him? Into the dark, in a neighbor's car, toward a clinic that might not take him? Or keep him here. On this machine. With Eli in the back room and her in this chair. No safe choice. Only the one she could stand inside."
-   **severity:** high

## Medium Severity

-   **category:** prose
-   **location:** "The card told her the truth the light had stopped telling, which was that the machine was working, that it was working now, that no one anywhere was promising it would still be working in an hour."
-   **issue:** The sentence is slightly ponderous and the repetition of "working" is flat. The phrasing "the truth... which was that..." is a weak construction.
-   **suggestion:** Propose: "The card told her what the light no longer could: the machine was working now, with no promise of the next hour."
-   **severity:** medium

-   **category:** prose
-   **location:** "...the same clean type the cellular notice had used and the power notice had used..."
-   **issue:** The repetition of "had used" at the end of two consecutive clauses is rhythmically clumsy.
-   **suggestion:** Propose: "...the same clean type as the cellular and power notices..."
-   **severity:** medium

-   **category:** style
-   **location:** "She held it back and felt the cost of holding it, the small private violation of her own rule..."
-   **issue:** This is on-the-nose telling. It explicitly flags her core contradiction for the reader, which the blueprint states should be shown, not explained.
-   **suggestion:** Cut the direct explanation of her "rule." Show the cost through a physical sensation or a brief, sharp internal image. "She held it back. The lie sat in her throat like something swallowed wrong."
-   **severity:** medium

-   **category:** reveal-safety
-   **location:** "He could also be the kind of man who had built the thing that made men like this man unnecessary, and she knew most of that history..."
-   **issue:** This phrasing is slightly too close to the book's premise-level summary. While Lena knows his Asterion history, stating it as "built the thing that made men... unnecessary" feels like an authorial gloss. It risks flattening the complexity of his role into a simple tagline.
-   **suggestion:** Keep it grounded in what Lena would know specifically. "He could also be the man from Asterion, from the labs that had hollowed out half the city's work, and she knew most of that history."
-   **severity:** medium

-   **location:** "She put it to sleep before it could finish offering to scan her."
-   **issue:** This is a strong line, but the personification might be a shade too much for the grounded style. A scanner "offering" feels slightly whimsical.
-   **suggestion:** Consider: "She put it to sleep before the scan prompt could fully form."
-   **severity:** low

## Overall

This is a strong chapter that successfully executes a complex blueprint. The voice is consistent, the pacing is controlled, and the worldbuilding is woven skillfully into Lena's rounds, delivering on the promise to show withdrawal in human terms. The dialogue, particularly from Mr. Adeyemi and Dembélé, is sharp and moving.

The primary area for revision is a recurring tendency to explain the chapter's own themes and subtext. The prose sometimes summarizes a character's internal state or a thematic contradiction, pulling back from the close-third viewpoint into a more distant, authorial position. Aggressively cutting these explanatory passages and trusting the concrete, physical details to carry the emotional and thematic weight will bring the chapter fully into alignment with the project's restrained, literary style. A second pass to eliminate filtering language will further sharpen the viewpoint and immerse the reader more deeply in Lena's immediate, harrowing night.
