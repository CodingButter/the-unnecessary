## Voice Direction

This is a single-narrator audiobook read in a grounded, austere register. The reader is a close third person seated just behind Eli Rook's eyes. Werner Herzog as a baseline: slow, deliberate, unhurried, more interested in the weight of a thing than in selling it. Nothing here is melodrama. The dread is structural, administrative, quiet. The performance must trust the silence.

**Overall.** Keep the default narrative voice low, even, patient, a craftsman's voice describing a craftsman's morning. Let it move through the ordinary observation and slow only where the prose asks the listener to stop and feel the floor go cold. Resist warmth except where the human texture earns it (the boy at the corner, Marisol, the doorbell that only has to ring). The chapter is a slow dawning, not a series of shocks, so the intensity must accumulate rather than spike.

**Per-register approach.**

- *Default narration (Eli's close third).* Weary and controlled. A man who has had too many of these mornings to be surprised. Dry, level, with a flat patience under it. Use [weary], [flat], [quiet], [measured]. Never lean theatrical; the restraint is the character.
- *The automated corporate notices.* This is the horror engine of the chapter and it must read FLAT and administrative. When the narrator voices the provider's language, drop all human inflection: [flat], [monotone], [cold], even pace, no emphasis, no concern. The calm IS the threat. Do not let warmth leak into "We thank you for your understanding" or "We appreciate your continued cooperation." Read those exactly as a machine that has never met a person would read them. The contrast between the flat notice and the human cost around it is the whole effect.
- *Eli's spoken lines.* Weary, controlled, economical. He spends words like the soft worn bills. [quiet], [flat], [tired]. A working man talking while his hands work. Only the one room-with-too-much-light beat (the old man's question about where he learned it) carries a buried, guarded tension: [guarded], [careful].
- *Lena, over the failing link.* Clipped and tense, bandwidth-broken. Her voice is "narrowed and flattened," dropping syllables. Perform that literally: [clipped], [tense], [strained], with occasional [breaking up] / [glitching] where the prose says the link swallows or drops her. Under it, the quiet she gets when she is angriest, which other people mistake for calm: not raised, lowered. She is the moral floor of the scene, pulling Eli down out of the clean abstraction.

**Pacing philosophy.** Ellipses are the instrument. Short holds inside sentences for the reader to land an image; longer holds at paragraph turns where a realization seats; the longest holds at the four scene breaks (the `---` lines) where time itself jumps. Place every ellipsis AFTER existing punctuation so no comma or period is lost. Let single-line paragraphs ("He read it twice." / "He stood very still." / "It was Lena.") sit in their own air; do not rush off them.

**Intensity arc and the two peaks.** The chapter climbs across roughly twelve hours toward a midnight deadline, and the direction should tighten as it climbs. Scene 1 is domestic, slow, almost still. Scene 2 walks and observes, a touch more momentum, the tally building. Scene 3 is the gathering, where separate things refuse to stay separate. Scene 4 is the call, the tightest and most strained register of the chapter, and it must not relax until the final line.

Give the most deliberate weight to the TWO PEAKS:

1. *The midday "opening of a hand" passage* (Scene 3, "Everything physical was exactly where it had been... a slow opening of a hand that had been holding them up, finger by finger, with a thank-you note for each finger as it let go"). Slow almost to a stop here. This is the thesis landing. Let the image of the hand opening finger by finger be spoken finger by finger.

2. *The final line* (Scene 4, "This was the one he could not fix."). The whole chapter is built to deliver this sentence. Maximum weight, maximum slowness, full stop of air before it. Do not soften it, do not trail it off. Land it flat and final and let it ring.
