# Chapter 4: Priority Tier

He smelled it half a block out, copper cooked past its color, the sweet burn of insulation that had been asked to carry more than it was built for. The smell sat at the back of the throat and would not be swallowed. He had been walking without deciding to walk, the message from before dawn carried somewhere under the ribs with no shape on it yet, and the smell gave the cold morning its first hard edge and gave his hands a question. They were ahead of him before the rest of him agreed to come.

The clinic stood in its converted storefront with the cold already standing in the doorway, and the damage was not inside it. The damage was around the side, at the gray steel box where the clinic's backup tied into the neighborhood line, and he was crouched in front of it before he had told himself to be. He laid the deadened fingertip of his left hand flat to the casing first, always the dead part before the live one, never letting the part of the hand that still felt come near anything that might be live. Warm where it should have been cool. He worked the cover off and the burned smell came up out of it like breath.

The charge controller had taken the hit. The board inside was scorched along one edge, a contactor welded shut, a trace lifted and blackened where the surge had walked up the line in the night and looked for the cheapest thing to die in. He read it slow, naming the failure to himself before he touched it, the discipline a Flint childhood had drilled in early: find the fault yourself, and never once tell a live thing it was dead. The controller. Not the battery. He said it under his breath to fix it where he could find it later. The controller had stood in front of the battery and taken the current so the battery did not, which was the one piece of luck in it, and luck was a thing he had stopped trusting somewhere around midnight the night before.

The readout on the bank below it told the rest. The backup had carried a charge he had budgeted to hold the clinic into the afternoon, and it was already lower than the number in his head, draining toward a floor it should not have reached until dark. Not a clean leak he could find and seal. A grid that surged and sagged under a tier nobody answered for, pushing and pulling at everything tied to it, and a finite stack of batteries spending itself faster than the arithmetic said because the arithmetic had assumed a grid that behaved.

"Eli."

Lena stood in the side door with her coat over her medical clothes and her hands clean and dry and folded against the cold. She did not look relieved to see him. She looked at him the way she looked at a chart she already understood, steady, the practiced neutral she kept so a frightened person could read nothing off her, and behind it the thing neither of them was going to say first. He had her message in his coat pocket. She had been at the bed before dawn. The loss was in the room with them and it had no words that were both true and bearable, so they spent none on it.

"The controller's gone," he said. He kept his voice low and flat. "It ate the surge so the battery wouldn't. You've got backup, but it's going down faster than it should. The grid's not just low this morning. It's bad."

"Bad how."

"It's swinging. Up and down, hard, no schedule. Equipment that was fine yesterday cooks where it sits." He nodded at the box. "That happened to this while everybody slept."

She took it without changing her face. Inside, past her, the clinic ran dim on its spared light, and along the back hall one bed in the end room held its blanket flat and made up over nothing, and he did not look at it for long. A job always had a part past the reach of your hands, and he had trained himself years ago to keep his eyes off it and on the part he could touch.

"Tell me what you can keep," Lena said. "Not what's broken. What you can keep." She had asked him for the truth instead of the comfort once, in this building, and she had not changed the habit overnight.

He wanted to give her a number. He stood and ran the morning out in his head the way he ran a fault, source by source, and the answer would not close. The clinic was not a single machine with a single fault he could isolate. The clinic was the smallest room in a building that was on fire in the walls. He could nurse the backup. He could not make a starved, surging grid behave by standing over one steel box outside one clinic, and behind the box was a whole block of houses and an EV line and a generator pad and a string of panels, every one of them tied to the same swinging line, every one of them his now whether he wanted them or not.

"I can keep you up this morning," he said. "Past that, it's not a clinic problem. It's the whole grid, and I can't hold the whole grid by myself." He heard how it landed and did not soften it. "I need Nolan."

Lena held his eyes a moment, the brief warm directness she gave when she decided to trust a thing, then let it go back to the stern face she wore for the worst of the work. "Then get him," she said, and went back in to the people she still had, and the door let its thread of cold out behind her.

He stood with the burned smell on his hands and the readout falling somewhere behind his eyes, and reached for the one man in the neighborhood who knew the grid better than he did.

---

Nolan came up the block the way a barge comes up a channel, slow and certain and taking the whole width of it, the brace on the right knee clicking once at every other step. He was a big man gone heavy with the years, broad through the shoulders still from a life spent inside the actual machinery, and he lowered himself to the box the way he did everything, deliberately, husbanding the knee, one hand on the casing to take his weight. The gray beard moved when he breathed out at what he saw. He wore the gloves he was never without, stained past their original color, and he put the back of one against the welded contactor before he trusted his eyes, the same instinct Eli had, learned in the same kind of decade.

"Charge controller," Nolan said. His voice was a low gravelled bass that had spent thirty-five years talking over generators and being the last word in a substation, and it did not rise for any of this. "Cooked clean through. Surge came up your feeder and this took it instead of your pack." He turned the dead board to the light, unhurried, reading it. "That's the good news, what there is of it. The bad news is everything else on that feeder is the same age and the same kind of stubborn, and the line's going to keep doing this all day."

"You felt it last night."

"I been feeling it since the tier dropped." He levered himself back up off the knee, slow, and looked down the block, and Eli watched him take in the whole shape of it the way he took in a board, the panels on two roofs, the line of plugged-in cars somebody's grandfather had wired into a common bank, the gray commercial battery shed behind the old pharmacy, the two natural-gas generators on their pad that the block ran when the grid would not. "They cut us under everything that matters and then quit watching the meter. So now it surges when their load drops and it sags when it picks up, and there's nobody on the other end who'd take the call if you made it. We're the ones holding the bottom of it."

"Then we tie it together," Eli said. "Everything local. Solar, the cars, the battery shed, both generators, the clinic backup. One grid, balanced, holding the clinic up no matter what the line does."

Nolan looked at him for a moment with the flat, unimpressed patience of a man who had been told a hundred times by clean-handed people that a thing would be simple. "I know what a microgrid is, son. I built pieces of the real one." He said it without heat. "You want to braid it. Fine. You braid me a generator that'll hand off to a battery bank without a half-second where the voltage goes through the roof, and you do it with gear that was built by six companies that hated each other, and we'll talk."

"I've done handoffs."

"Not these. Come on." And he turned and started down the block on the braced knee, and that was the closest he came to agreeing.

They worked it for the better part of three hours, and it was real, and it was losing. Every source on the block spoke its own dialect of the same three words, charge and discharge and who goes first, and none of them had ever been built to listen to the others. The solar controllers wanted to dump whatever the panels made and shed the rest, blind to anything downstream. The car bank answered to a phone app for a company that no longer ran a server, so an old workaround on it took commands only in a narrow window and ignored everything else. The commercial shed behind the pharmacy held the most stored power on the block and the most jealous controller, a unit that would not discharge into a bus it could not authenticate, and authenticating it meant standing up a stand-in, a local box telling it a small yes in a grammar it would believe.

Some of it bridged. He sat on the cold concrete of the generator pad and tapped the line between a generator and the battery shed and read the handoff with Nolan crouched beside him naming what he saw, and they got the generator to hand its load to the shed without throwing the whole bus into the ceiling, two tries, then a third that held. Some of it would not bridge at all. The solar would not take a priority order the car bank issued, because the two of them had no shared idea of what an order even was, and after twenty minutes of trying to translate between them Eli left a seam there he could not close and routed around it, which cost him a margin he did not have.

"There," Nolan said, low, when the array finally settled into the bus without fighting it. He was down on the knee again over a junction, the big scarred hands working a connection finer than they looked like they could, and the work-stained gloves were off for the close part and back on the second it was done. "Now don't breathe on it."

That was the shape of the whole morning. They got it standing, and it stood only because they stood over it. The two of them could not be at every seam at once, and the grid would not wait for them to be. While Eli held the shed's contactor closed by hand and watched the bus voltage, a generator two hundred feet off began to hunt, its note climbing and falling against the inverter's whine, and Nolan was already moving toward it on the bad knee before Eli called it, because Nolan had heard it first. They traded the failing points back and forth between them, one going slack the moment the other was caught, the braid coming apart at a different seam every time they tied off the last one.

"You feel that," Nolan said once, flat, not a question, when the whole bus shivered under a sag in the line and they both rode it out with their hands on different parts of the same machine. "That's not the gear. That's them, miles off, turning something on. We feel it down here."

"I feel it."

"Thirty-five years, this is what they took. Used to be somebody watched that. Used to be me." He did not say it for sympathy. He said it the way he named a part. He got quieter when a thing was true, not louder, and that one came out almost under the generator's noise.

By midday they had a grid. It powered the clinic. The needle on the backup had stopped falling and started, barely, to climb. It was the most either of them could have done and it was held together by attention and nothing else, the same shape as a machine that ran on a permission no longer issued anywhere, alive only as long as a hand was on it. Eli straightened from the last seam with the ache of the morning's cold worked all the way into his spine and looked at the thing they had made, mismatched gear from one end of the block to the other, lit and balanced and one missed minute from coming apart, and he knew it the way he knew everything his hands had touched in two days. It would hold while they held it. They could not hold it forever, and neither of them said so yet.

---

The shed came first, in the early afternoon, because the arithmetic said it had to. The clinic's draw had crept up with the cold, the backup was climbing too slowly to matter, and the only power left to give it had to come from somewhere already spending itself on something else. He stood at the commercial shed with the load map in his head and started cutting, and the fastest watts for the least cost were a row of houses on the low end of the block that pulled heat all afternoon and held no one the clinic was keeping alive.

"Stop." Talia had come up the block while his hands were busy, the old tablet against her hip, the squared planted stance of a person who had walked into a thousand rooms and made each of them quiet. She was not quiet now. She watched him reach for the shed's panel and the open warmth went out of her face all at once, and she did not bother to hide it. "Say what you're doing. Out loud."

"Shedding load. The clinic needs it and there's nowhere else to take it from."

"Say it the other way." Her voice was a clear carrying alto pitched to the back of a classroom and she did not raise it. She brought it down instead, slower, the calm itself a kind of pressure. "You're going to turn the heat off in somebody's house so the clinic stays lit. That's what shedding load is. Whose house."

He told her. He named the row, because lying to Talia about a number was a thing that had never once worked and he did not have the hour to spend learning it again.

"And who decided that," she said. "You? Because you can reach the switch and they can't?"

"The grid decided it. There isn't enough. Somebody comes off or the clinic goes down."

"The grid didn't pick that row. You did." She came and stood between him and the panel, not touching it, just there, the tablet still at her hip. "I'm not telling you the math is wrong. The math might be exactly right. I'm telling you that math isn't yours to do alone, in your head, standing here, and call it engineering. Those are people's nights. Somebody in that row's got a baby, or a mother who can't make her own heat. You don't get to decide whose cold is acceptable so somebody else stays warm and then hand it to me like it's a wiring diagram."

"Talia, the battery's draining while we have this conversation."

"I know it is. I can read a clock." She did not move. "So decide. Out loud. And write down who paid for it, so when the heat's off in that house tonight it's a thing this neighborhood chose and owned, and not a thing that happened to them in the dark because the man with the screwdriver was in a hurry."

He had spent a working life learning that a clean fix was the one that asked permission of no one, that the best repair was the one nobody had to vote on, and she would not let the clinic's light be a clean fix. The old pull rose in him to reach for a word that would make it a system again, load-shedding, priority allocation, demand management, words that put the choosing somewhere other than his hand on the panel, and he caught her face cooling further as he reached for it, the look of someone who had watched a hundred experts reach for exactly that vocabulary and knew the move before the hand made it.

Nolan put it down between them from where he was working, flat, not looking up. "She's right that somebody pays. I'll tell you the part that matters to me." He set a connector and rose off the knee. "Whatever you cut, I want to be able to turn it back on by hand. Today, tonight, whenever. No box deciding who's warm that I can't walk up to and override. You take the people out of it, you build exactly the thing that already left us." He looked at Eli when he said it, and the look held a second too long.

The line jumped while the three of them stood there.

It came up the feeder the way the night one had, and this time they were standing over it. The bus voltage spiked, the inverters screamed up an octave, somewhere down the block a board let go with a crack and a thread of smoke, and the lit clinic windows flared and dropped and the whole braided thing they had spent the morning on shuddered on the edge of falling over. Nolan was already down the block toward the smoke on the braced knee, fast as Eli had seen him move, and Eli rode the shed's contactor through the swing with his hand on it and his teeth set, holding the one piece he could reach while the surge took another piece he could not. When it passed, the clinic was still lit, barely, and a second junction near the corner was dead and smoking, more gear gone where it stood, and the row of houses Talia had stopped him from cutting was still warm and the clinic was running on a margin that no longer existed.

He shed the row in the end. There was no version where he did not. The batteries would not wait for a meeting and a meeting in the next twenty minutes would not have been a meeting, it would have been him talking faster. He cut the heat to the low row and the clinic held, and Talia stood with him while he did it and made him watch the houses go dark one window at a time, and then she opened the tablet and wrote the row down, every address, in her own hand, the chalk-and-marker callus on her finger riding the screen.

"This is owed," she said. Quiet now, simpler, the way she got when a thing was past arguing. "Not a fix. A debt. I'm going to go knock on those doors myself and tell them it was us, and that we know it, because the alternative is they find out the way everybody's been finding everything out for two years, which is in the cold with nobody to ask." She looked at the dark row and the lit clinic and the smoke off the corner junction. "You and I both lost something just now, and the grid didn't care which of us was right."

He had no answer for her that would hold, so he gave her none. The houses stayed dark. The clinic stayed lit. Between them on the cracked pavement the second dead junction smoked itself out, and the line they had fought over all afternoon had reached down and made the choice for both of them.

---

June found them at the failing grid in the last of the light, coming off the mesh and the salvage with a coil of cable over one shoulder and her scratched lenses pushed up into the unevenly cut hair. She was small and quick and she did not so much arrive as land, perched on the corner of the generator pad half-turned already toward whatever was next, the many-pocketed salvage coat she wore like a workbench clinking when she moved. She took in the dark row and the smoking junction and the look on the two older men in about three seconds, and her nicked technician's hands had already found a tool to turn over.

"Okay, so we're losing," she said. Fast, flat Detroit vowels, no reverence in any of it. "I mean, you both know it. You're being really polite about it, but every hour we lose another box and you can't fix them faster than the grid breaks them. That's just the numbers." She nodded at the corner. "What was that one?"

"Junction," Nolan said. "Surge took it."

"Right, and tomorrow it takes two more, and there's two of you." She said it without cruelty, the way you read a meter. Then she went still, the stillness that came over her when a problem turned serious, and got exact. "You're trying to run a grid by hand. You can't. Nobody can. You need something that watches all of it at once and balances it faster than a person, and we don't have that, and there's exactly one place close that does."

Nolan had gone quiet over the panel. He stayed quiet a moment longer, the big hands flat on the cold metal, and when he spoke it was lower and flatter than it had been all day, which from him was the sound of certainty.

"She's right." He did not look up at first. He had spent the whole afternoon being the man who could hold it, and he laid that down now the way he laid down a tool he was done with. "I been telling myself thirty-five years that hands keep the lights on. Knowledge in a person's head and a person willing to use it. And that's true, and it's been true here, and it's why this clinic's lit right now." He raised his head and looked at Eli, and the gray beard and the lined face held no performance in them at all. "It's not enough. Not this. Not at this size, not at this speed, not with two of us and one of me. I can hold a block for a day. I can't hold this. Hand labor can't keep enough machines alive to matter, and I'd be lying to you and to her if I let you think it could. That's the first time I ever said that out loud and it's the truest thing I've said all day."

It landed in the cold with nothing to catch it. If Nolan said the hands were not enough, the hands were not enough. There was no one in the neighborhood whose word on it weighed more.

"So," June said, into the gap, and she said it plainly because she had not been in the rooms that taught the other two to be afraid of saying it. "Northglass."

The name went through him before he had decided anything, a drop in the body like a stair that was not there.

"It's right there," she went on, fast again, certain, the boldness coming up off her like heat. "The whole Asterion campus, sitting empty, twenty minutes out the old utility cut. Server floors. Real ones. They walked out and left it. Half of it's bricked because it phones home to a company that won't answer, sure, fine, that's every machine I've ever touched. But the iron's there. There's gear out in those buildings that could balance this grid in its sleep and never sweat, orchestration, the actual thing, the thing nobody's hands can do, and we are out here doing it with screwdrivers like it's a hundred years ago because everybody older than me is scared of it." She spread her nicked hands at the dark block. "We go get what works. We bring it back. We stop losing."

"No." It came out of him short and hard, before the reasons, and he heard how hard it was the second it was out.

June blinked at him. "No, like, you want to think about it, or no, no."

"No." He made himself give her the reason, because the reason was real and saying it was the only thing keeping the rest of it down where she could not see it. "Northglass still talks to Asterion. The campus isn't as dead as it looks. There's gear out there that still reports, and they've been watching that ground a while now. You light something up inside that fence and somebody in a clean room three states away gets a pin on a map, and the one thing, the one thing I do, is not be a pin on that map. Not me. Not this neighborhood. We stay small and quiet and beneath the meter, and we stay alive because they're not looking. You go out there, you make us worth looking at."

It was true. Every word of it was true, and he leaned the whole of his weight on it. It still was not enough to carry what was in his voice. He knew it, and worse, he could see they heard it too. Nolan was watching him with the shrewd hooded eyes of a man who had spent a life being told a system was fine and had learned to go look for himself, and what he was looking at now was Eli, and the part of the no that did not add up. June had her head tilted, the impatience gone over into something more careful, because she had named a hard practical problem and gotten back an answer that was the right size for a different, larger thing she could not see.

"It's the same risk we already run," she said, slower. "We run modified gear now. I run a terminal that'd light me up if I leaned on it. You're acting like it's something else."

"It's the risk and it's enough." He shut it down because the alternative was to keep talking, and the more he talked the more the gap showed between the reason he was giving and the thing under it, a place he was not going to go, a door he had kept shut for six years that the name Northglass had put a hand on. The shape of his own refusal did not fit the danger he had described, the way a board too big for its housing does not fit, and there was nothing he could do about the misfit except stop, so he stopped. "We find another way."

"There isn't another way," Nolan said, gently for him. "That's what I just stood here and admitted. You heard me admit it."

"Then we lose slower." It was the worst thing he had said all day and he knew it and he said it anyway, and the standoff settled over the three of them in the dark, the man who had conceded the ground and the girl who had named the door and the man standing in front of it with a dread the others could plainly see the size of and could not begin to read the shape of. June did not push it further. She had put it on the table and the table held it. The clinic burned its borrowed light behind them, and the dark row was dark, and the cold came down, and his no hung in it harder than anything else the day had broken.

---

It was full dark and past the hour when work usually quit, and they did not quit, because the machines did not. The braided grid shed a seam near the pharmacy and Nolan went after it. A surge-cracked board in the car bank gave up and took a third of the storage with it. They lost pieces one at a time the way you lose heat from a house with the door open, not in a crash, in a slow honest bleed, and Eli moved between the failing points knowing he was only choosing the order in which things went, not whether.

Then the runner came up from the clinic, one of Lena's people, with the message that the medication unit had gone down.

He understood it before he reached the back room. There had been a dip when the pharmacy seam let go, a half-second of nothing, and a half-second was all it took. The cabinet had been holding its last authorization with nothing under it, running on a yes the company had stopped meaning at midnight the night before, alive only as long as nothing made it ask again. The dip had made it ask again. It had come up out of the dark looking for a company that was not there, found no one left to grant it permission, and done the thing a careful machine does when no one answers. It had locked the drawers and kept the keys, and behind the gray locked face were doses someone in the ward was owed tonight, and no one alive knew the override, and there was not enough of him left to forge one before morning.

Lena stood at the cabinet with her hand flat on it, the low even voice gone quiet, which was the loudest she got. "It won't open," she said. She did not ask him to fix it. She had stopped asking him to fix the ones that could not be fixed. "I have a person who needs what's in here and I have a box that has decided I'm not allowed to have it. That's twice this week a machine has stood between me and a patient because a company in another state stopped paying its bill."

He looked at the locked face and the falling light, and the day closed its own account, without his help, the sum arrived at while his hands were busy somewhere else. The controller before dawn. The cabinet now. The grid taking a board an hour all night until there were no boards. Two skilled pairs of hands had held a block for one day and the block had won anyway, slowly, correctly, the way the surge had welded the contactor and the cabinet had locked itself, each machine doing exactly what it was built to do on its way to killing what it was built to keep.

The refusal simply ran out of anything to stand on, the way a man stops bracing a door when his arms give. Nolan had said the hands were not enough and the hands had just proven it on a locked drawer. The dread had not moved. It was only that everything else had gotten heavier than it, all at once, and the door he had kept shut for six years was now the only thing on the other side of more nights like this one.

He found June outside, on the corner of the generator pad where she had stayed, turning a dead connector over in the dark because her hands did not stop either. She looked up.

"Cabinet locked," he said. "Won't take a forged key. There's a patient owed a dose and a machine that won't let go of it."

"Yeah." She watched him. She had heard the change in him before he reached her, the way she heard a fault. "So."

He did not give her the real reason. He gave her none of it, not why it had to be him, not what he knew that no one else knew, not the thing waiting at the end of the old utility cut that frightened him more than a dark clinic did. He gave her the survival reason, because it was true and because it was all of the truth he was ever going to say out loud.

"You're right," he said. "There's no other way. We go get what works."

Something lit in her face, vindication, the delight of a machine doing what it should, and then she saw the rest of him and tamped it down, because whatever she had won, he did not look like a man who had agreed to anything good. He looked like a man who had stopped holding a door.

"You and me," he said. "Before sunrise. Don't put it on the mesh. Don't tell anybody where."

She nodded, the boldness gone careful, the question she did not ask plain on her face and left unasked. He turned back toward the clinic, where the last of the light guttered on its stolen power and the dark row held its cold and the cabinet kept its keys, and the dread did not lift with the decision the way relief was supposed to. It sharpened. He had said the word and the time and made the thing he could not take back, and it sat in him larger than the dying block in front of him, the size of a room he was not going to describe, and he carried it down the cold street toward the morning he had set, and said nothing more, to her or to himself, about what was behind the door.
