# Gemini Editorial Critique

## High Severity

- **category:** style
- **location:** "Eli felt the old pull to reach for a word that would make it a system again..." / "Eli felt the name go through him before he had decided anything..." / "He could feel the shape of his own refusal and he could feel that it did not fit the danger..." / "felt the day close its own account..."
- **issue:** Rampant filtering. The narrative distance collapses when you use "felt" to explain Eli's internal state. The Style Guide explicitly prohibits this kind of sensory filtering; it distances the reader from the close third perspective.
- **suggestion:** Remove the filter words and state the experience directly. E.g., "The old pull rose in him to reach for a word..." / "The name went through him before he had decided anything..." / "The shape of his own refusal did not fit the danger he had described..."
- **severity:** high

- **category:** style
- **location:** "He did not decide to change his mind. There was no moment where he weighed Northglass against the lock and found a number that tipped. The refusal simply ran out of anything to stand on..."
- **issue:** This is a direct violation of the project's prohibited patterns list ("It was not X. It was Y."). It tells the reader what *isn't* happening to explain what *is* happening, which reads as generated and rhetorical rather than grounded.
- **suggestion:** Cut the negative framing entirely. Start with the physical reality: "The refusal simply ran out of anything to stand on, the way a man stops bracing a door when his arms give."
- **severity:** high

- **category:** prose
- **location:** "His voice had gone short and literal the way it did when there was too much under it to risk a long sentence."
- **issue:** Telling, not showing, and quoting the character profile directly at the reader. The profile instructs the *author* to make his sentences shorter and more literal under emotion; the prose should not explain this psychological mechanism to the audience.

## Medium Severity

- **category:** prose
- **location:** "He kept his voice low and let it stay flat, because flat was the only register that did not crack."
- **issue:** Over-explaining the emotion. You are telling the reader why his voice is flat instead of letting the tension of the scene imply it.
- **suggestion:** Cut the explanation. "He kept his voice low and flat." The reader knows about the off-page death; they will understand the strain without being told about the crack.
- **severity:** medium

- **category:** prose
- **location:** "It was the trade he hated and she knew he hated it, which was most of why she made him do it standing up."
- **issue:** Mind-reading/telling subtext. The dialogue between Eli and Talia is incredibly strong here; this sentence undercuts it by explaining the dynamic like a textbook.
- **suggestion:** Cut the sentence. Talia's physical positioning ("She came and stood between him and the panel, not touching it, just there") already perfectly communicates that she is forcing him to own the decision.
- **severity:** medium

- **category:** prose
- **location:** "He gave her the survival reason, the one June already had, the one the others would hear and accept and never question, because it was true and because it was all of the truth he was ever going to say out loud."
- **issue:** Over-explaining the secret. The blueprint asks to withhold Eli's real reason, but this sentence hammers the reader over the head with the fact that he is hiding something. It feels heavy-handed.
- **suggestion:** Trim the padding to let the subtext breathe. "He gave her the survival reason, because it was true and because it was all of the truth he was going to say out loud."
- **severity:** medium

- **category:** prose
- **location:** "Lena stood at the cabinet with her hand flat on it, the low even voice gone very quiet, which was the loudest she got."
- **issue:** Clunky phrasing. The paradox of "very quiet, which was the loudest she got" feels like a clever authorial intrusion rather than a grounded observation.
- **suggestion:** Sharpen the description of her anger. "Lena stood at the cabinet with her hand flat on it, her voice dropping to the dead quiet that meant she was furious."
- **severity:** medium

## Low Severity

- **category:** prose
- **location:** "The smell sat at the back of the throat and would not be swallowed."
- **issue:** A bit generic/melodramatic for the opening hook. It lacks the sharp, grounded specificity of the rest of the paragraph.
- **suggestion:** Tie it closer to the physical reality of the grid. "The smell caught at the back of his throat, the sharp, chemical warning of a grid eating itself."
- **severity:** low

- **category:** style
- **location:** "The controller. Not the battery. He said it under his breath to fix it where he could find it later."
- **issue:** Borders on the prohibited "It was not X. It was Y" pattern. It works passably here as Eli's diagnostic thought process, but it's risky given the project's strict rules.
- **suggestion:** Combine them into a single diagnostic thought. "The controller, not the battery. He said it under his breath..."
- **severity:** low

- **category:** prose
- **location:** "The open warmth went out of her face all at once, the legible cooling she did not bother to hide."
- **issue:** Slightly tautological. "Went out of her face" and "legible cooling" are saying the exact same thing twice in one sentence.
- **suggestion:** Pick the stronger image. "The open warmth went out of her face, a sudden, legible cooling."
- **severity:** low

## Overall

This is a highly effective chapter that executes the blueprint's structural requirements with precision. The reveal boundary is held perfectly—Eli's dread is palpable, but Morrow and the buried drive remain completely sealed. The dialogue is the standout achievement here: Talia, Nolan, and June all arrive with incredibly distinct, profile-accurate voices. Talia's load-shedding confrontation is a masterclass in grounded political tension, and Nolan's admission of defeat carries real, heavy gravity. The microgrid engineering feels authentic and lived-in without bogging down the pacing. 

To elevate the draft to the project's standard, you must aggressively hunt down and eliminate the filtering ("felt") and the tendency to over-explain the subtext. You have a habit of writing a brilliant piece of dialogue or physical blocking, and then following it with a sentence that explains to the reader exactly what the characters just did. Trust your dialogue. Trust the blocking. Strip away the filter words, cut the explanatory tags, and let the stark reality of the failing grid carry the weight.
