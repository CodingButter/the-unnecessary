# Opus Critical Read — Chapter 1 "No Signal"

A close prose read held against the Style Guide. The chapter is strong; these are the lines that fall short of it, ranked by severity. (Companion to the Gemini critique; the revision pass synthesizes both.)

### 1. SEVERE — "It was not destruction. Destruction would have left a mark. This left nothing." (Scene 3)
The exact construction the guide bans ("It was not X. It was Y" / "Excessive Binary Contrasts"), run three beats, and the chapter leans on this negation-restatement rhythm elsewhere. The guide: habitual use "makes the prose sound generated."
Rewrite: "Destruction would have left a mark, something to point at and repair. This left the street exactly as it was, working, ready, and quietly no longer owed anything."

### 2. SEVERE — "The street outside looked like a street." (Scene 2 opening)
Tautological, flat scene-opener. Should already do the work the next paragraph does.
Rewrite: "The street outside looked like any other street in Detroit, which was the trick of it." Or: "From the steps the street gave nothing away. That was the first thing wrong with it."

### 3. HIGH — "He read the lights the way you would read a tide chart, the way they told you where the water had come up to and where it had gone back" (Scene 2)
Doubled "the way..." crutch, plus the simile is unpacked across a clause that explains its own metaphor (guide: do not explain symbolism in narration).
Rewrite: "He read the lights the way you read a tide chart, the dead ones marking how far the maintenance had pulled back, block by block, the boundary drawn in cold glass over his head."

### 4. HIGH — "...did not look back at the street, which lay behind him quietly insisting it was ordinary, kept ordinary by hands, by people who had stopped some years ago expecting help to come from anywhere but each other." (Scene 2 end)
Slides from Eli's perception into a sociological sermon (guide: avoid authorial verdicts / thematic summary); also piles personification.
Rewrite: "He stepped over it, put the key in the lock, and did not look back at the street lying quiet behind him, kept ordinary by hands."

### 5. HIGH — "...he did not have to think about being afraid." (Scene 3)
Names the emotion outright (guide: express emotion through attention/avoidance/decisions, do not label it). The chapter has been showing this displacement; here it labels it.
Rewrite: "There was always another stranded machine to bring back to its senses, and the work left no room in him for anything else."

### 6. MEDIUM — Habitual "the way..." anaphora across the chapter
A tic (guide Pass 4: repeated sentence constructions). ~7 instances. Keep the two best (transistor radio; "it had rained Tuesday"); recast the rest.

### 7. MEDIUM — "...and it was not all from the room." (Scene 4)
Telling-by-negation tag that undercuts the strong physical detail (cold through the floor). Cut the tag; the placement after the three names already carries it.

### 8. MEDIUM — "the realest she had asked" / "the lives reduced to a threshold and a clock" (Scene 4)
First is informal-superlative filler; second is narration stating the theme out loud (a verdict). Replace with concrete: let the three names and 23:59 mean it.

### 9. MEDIUM — "...that worked about as well now as it had then." (Scene 1)
Strong image (phone to the window), weak tail that explains the joke. Rewrite: "a thing his father would have done with a transistor radio, and it helped about as much."

### 10. LOW — "gray" overuse (lines 23, 67)
"Gray morning light," "gray as the inside of a switched-off screen," "gray tubs." Keep the screen simile; change the opening "came up gray in the gray morning light" → "came up pale in the morning light."

### 11. LOW — "with a flatness past his years" (Scene 3)
Near the "wise beyond his years" cliché register. Rewrite: "with a flatness Eli knew and let pass."

### 12. LOW — "a person talking from the bottom of a well." (Scene 4)
Worn simile for degraded audio; the preceding sentence already describes it. Cut or freshen.

**Overall:** the few weaknesses cluster in one habit — the narration periodically stops trusting its concrete evidence and states the theme or names the emotion (1, 4, 5, 7, 8), compounded by the "the way..." cadence. Tighten those and the chapter is at full strength.
