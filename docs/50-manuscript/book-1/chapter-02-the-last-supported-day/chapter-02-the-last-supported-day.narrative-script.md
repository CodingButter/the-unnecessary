## Voice Direction

This is not spoken. It is direction for the reader, human or synthetic.

### Overall direction

Grounded and austere. A single narrator carries the whole chapter in Lena Okafor's close register: a forty-six-year-old doctor running an abandoned clinic against a deadline she did not set, holding a banked anger that she will not let become panic because panic stops moving patients. The default sound is weary and controlled, intelligent, exact, with the anger sealed underneath the precision rather than displayed on top of it. The horror of this chapter is administrative and quiet, never loud. Nothing should sound theatrical. Where the prose reaches its hardest truths, the answer is more stillness, not more volume. The voice tightens slowly across the chapter and arrives, in the fourth scene, at an anger that has changed shape and finally has no face to aim itself at. Bookend the chapter with deliberate weight: the opening image of the card taped over the dead light, and the closing image of the cycle pushing and releasing in the dark, should be the two most measured passages in the reading.

### Per-register approach for this chapter

- **Lena's narration (default, weary banked-anger control).** Most of the chapter. Calm, precise, clinical when she is counting; the feeling is held a half-step below the words. Tag this `[weary]` and `[controlled]` and let it ride; do not re-tag every paragraph. Only mark where the register genuinely shifts, into tenderness at a bedside, into colder precision when she does triage arithmetic, into the slow anger-turn of scene four. The intelligence is always working; she is never sentimental about it.

- **The corporate notice (flat, administrative).** When the discontinuation sentence is read, it must read `[flat]`, even, with no human voice inside it, because the prose says exactly that: a sentence built so that no human voice could be heard inside it. The horror is the calm. Do not editorialize it with the voice; let the flatness do the work, then return to Lena's register.

- **Eli's spoken lines (spare, working).** Plain, dry, low. A man reading a room and a machine, choosing words the way he chooses a connector. Tag him `[spare]` or `[plain]` and keep him level; his honesty and his competence are the same flat thing in him. He does not perform reluctance even when he delivers hard news; he simply tells her. No warmth manufactured, no chill manufactured.

- **The patients and staff (their own plain registers).** Each gets one plain human register, not a caricature. Tomas: steady, matter-of-fact, the weather-reporting tone the prose names. The Reyes man: dry, accepting. Mrs. Diallo: tired and clear, rueful, ashamed without self-pity. Dembélé: warm, satisfied, with the loss audible under the satisfaction and not hidden. Mr. Adeyemi: thin around the machine's work, wry, frightened underneath the joke. Tag each at first line and let it hold through their speech.

### Pacing philosophy

Let ordinary punctuation and the tags carry the pace. Ellipses are rare, a handful across the chapter, reserved for a genuine intended beat and always placed after existing punctuation, never sprinkled inside clauses. The long descriptive sentences (the card, the scanner, the egg economy, the final image) are meant to unspool slowly and evenly, not to be chopped. The dialogue is faster and plainer. Do not rush the bedside scenes. Do not slow the corporate sentence into menace; keep it administrative.

### Intensity arc

The chapter tightens. Scene one is survey and dread held flat. Scene two brings Eli and the hard engineering truth, delivered level. Scene three is the human tour, the warmest and most varied register, with the loss banked under the warmth. Scene four is the turn: the anger that had a face in the afternoon now has none, and the reading should narrow and steady and harden through the two anger paragraphs, then settle into the final bedside image, which is the quietest and most deliberate passage in the chapter. The peak is not a raised voice; it is the cold clarity of "There was no one to be angry at for this." Give that, and the last paragraph's breathing machine, the most deliberate weight.

## Performance Script

[measured] Chapter Two. ... The Last Supported Day.

---

[weary] The card was the first thing Lena fixed her eyes on when she came into the room, the way she always did now, before the man in the bed, before the numbers.

It was an index card, white going soft at the corners, taped to the housing of the respiratory controller over the place where a small status light used to burn. The light had meant the machine was certified, current, blessed by the company that built it, and the company was gone, and the light was dead, and so she had written what she needed to know on a card and taped it where the light had been. Cycle good. Last clean read, and a time. Pressures, in her own hand. The card told her what the light no longer could: that it had cycled clean the last time it ran, with no promise of the next.

She put her hand flat on the casing.

[slowly] [controlled] It was cool under her hand, and still, the machine idle on its stand the way it stood through the daylight while the man in the bed did his own breathing. Mr. Adeyemi was awake. Sixty-one years old, narrow under the blanket, propped up, managing his own slow lungs for as long as the afternoon would let him. In the day he came off it for hours at a stretch, sat up, complained about the food, told her about a daughter in a protected zone who could not get out to see him and would not have been let back in if she had. At night it went the other way. His chest could not hold the count alone, and the machine took it up, a small even push and release it would make for him because his own lungs would not make it through the dark, and the daughter went quiet in him along with everything else, and Lena watched the card. That was tonight, still ahead of them both. For now the casing stayed cool and the man stayed talking and the controller only waited, the way she waited, for the dark.

She made herself take her hand off the casing and look at her phone.

[weary] Nine and a half hours. The notice had given a time, the way they always gave a time, and the time was 23:59, which was their polite way of saying midnight without writing the word, the same clean type as the cellular and power notices, a sentence built so that no human voice could be heard inside it. [flat] Remote authentication for the listed devices will be discontinued effective end of day, October the third. [weary] She had read it to Eli earlier over a link that dropped every third word, and she had heard him on the other end go into the place he went, the bench in his head, and she had told him to come down out of it and stay with the body, and he had said he would come. He was coming. That changed the arithmetic, but it did not change it as much as she needed, and she would not let herself pretend it did until she had counted the rest of what she had.

She went to count it.

[controlled] The clinic was three patient rooms, a ward of six beds, an exam room, the front room with its bench and its counter, and a back hall that ran past a supply closet to a door she had not opened in a year. She had taken it over from a dentist who had taken it over from an insurance office, and the bones of all three were still in it if you looked, the reception window, the little frosted partitions, the carpet glue under the new flooring. At this hour the place ran on daylight. She kept the electric lights off to spare the batteries, and the rooms took what the afternoon gave them through the windows, a thin used-up light that thinned further the deeper in you went and did not reach the inner rooms at all. The cold had come into the building the way it came into everything since warmth had become a thing they were allowed only in rations, a cold that settled into the floor and rose through the soles of her shoes.

In the second room she stopped at the diagnostic scanner.

It stood against the wall, white and unmarked and perfect, a clean curved shell with a bed that slid into it, and it was the best doctor in the building and they both knew it. She had watched it find a thing in a woman's pancreas that Lena's own hands and eyes and forty-six years would have missed for another two months, and the two months would have been the difference. It was better than her. It was not better than her at the only thing that mattered tonight, which was running. She woke its panel. It lit and asked her, in its mild way, to wait while it confirmed its license, and the little wheel turned while it reached two thousand miles toward a server to be told it was allowed to know what it knew. The server answered, tonight. The wheel stopped. The machine said it was ready. At midnight the server would stop answering, and the next time someone pressed this panel the wheel would turn and turn and never stop, and the machine would sit here, white and unmarked and perfect, knowing everything and permitted to say none of it.

She put it to sleep before it could finish offering to scan her.

In the same room, the medication-management unit, a gray cabinet the size of a small refrigerator with a screen and a row of locked drawers. It held the doses, decided how much and how often, would not let her hand more morphine than a body should have because it knew the body and she, in a tired hour, might forget. She loved it for that on her good days and resented it on the rest. It had the same heart as the scanner and the controller, the same need to phone a company to be allowed to do its work, and the company had written the same sentence about it. After midnight it might keep the schedules already loaded and refuse to take a new one. It might lock the drawers and keep the keys. She did not know which. No one knew which. The not knowing was the whole of it, the thing under everything tonight, that she was being asked to plan a night around three machines whose behavior after one specific minute no living person could tell her.

A floorboard took weight in the doorway and she did not turn around.

[plain] "Priya wants to know about the Caldwell girl," Tomas said. He was the only other nurse on the floor with her tonight, thirty, steady, one she had not had to teach to be afraid quietly. "Whether we keep her or send her home. And the Okonkwos brought their thing. It's on the counter."

[weary] "What thing."

[plain] "Eggs," Tomas said. "Dozen, looks like. For the father's visit Tuesday. Mrs. Okonkwo said to tell you they're from the Vesely place, the brown ones, she said you'd want the brown ones." He said it without weight, the way you'd report the weather, because it was the weather. "I said thank you. I didn't know what else to do with a fee."

[weary] "Put them somewhere they won't freeze." There was nothing to enter the eggs into. There had been a system once, a screen at the counter that took a card and sent a number to an office that sent a bill to a company that sent the company's money back, and the screen was still there, dark, and the office was gone, and the company had folded into another company that had been discontinued, and so a dozen brown eggs sat on the counter where the screen used to be, and that was the entire economy now, start to finish, the Vesely place to the Okonkwos to her. "The Caldwell girl. What's her fever doing."

[plain] "Down. Hundred even, last hour."

[controlled] "Then she's mine, not the machine's. Keep her. I'm not sending a child into the cold on a night I can't promise her the scanner if she spikes." The words came out and she caught their shape on the way, the scanner she could not promise. "Tell Priya to watch her by hand. Temperature every two hours, the old way, write it down. If she goes up, come get me, don't go to the cabinet, come get me."

[plain] "Because the cabinet might lock," Tomas said. Not a question. He had read the same notice on her face all afternoon.

[controlled] "Because I want to be the one deciding the dose tonight," Lena said. "Not the cabinet, and not whatever the cabinet turns into at midnight."

[weary] He went. She stood a moment in the dim room with the two clean machines reading their perfect numbers at no one, and did the arithmetic she hated, the kind that came out in people. Mr. Adeyemi on the controller, a whole night. The Caldwell child, fever falling, hers by hand. Two on the ward who needed nothing the machines gave. A man coming in the morning who would need the scanner and would have to be told to drive to a clinic two towns over that might or might not still have one running, and might or might not be the kind of place that asked him for papers he did not have. She had equipment she could not trust and judgment she could trust, and the thing she was shortest on was not medicine, and was not hours. It was certainty. Triage ran on certainty. You sorted the ones who could wait from the ones who could not, and to do it you had to know which was which, and tonight the machines that were supposed to tell her had been told to stop telling her at a minute she did not pick.

She caught herself standing too long.

She found herself drifting back to the controller, to the cool idle housing and her flat hand on it and nothing moving under her palm, as if presence were a plan. It was not a plan. A hand on a casing did not authorize anything. She made herself move, because standing still was what you did when there was nothing left to do, and there was a great deal left to do, and she was not at the end of it yet.

She was at the counter, looking at the eggs without seeing them, when she heard the front door, and a cold draft reached her down the hall, and a voice she had only heard for four years through a thinning link said her name in a room instead of out of a speaker, low and dry and whole.

She left the eggs and went to meet him.

---

[weary] Eli stood inside the door with the cold still on him and a canvas bag in one hand, the three o'clock light gone flat and gray in the door glass behind him, and he looked, as he always looked, like a man who had walked through the neighborhood reading it. He was taller than she let herself remember between times, gray come further into the close-cut hair than a year ago. He did not say hello. That was the one thing they had in common in a doorway. He looked past her shoulder down the hall toward the rooms, already counting.

[spare] "Where are they," he said.

[controlled] "Two in that room." She pointed. "Scanner and the cabinet, the medication unit. The controller's down the end, in with the man." She did not say his name. She had not said his name to Eli over the link that afternoon and she did not say it now, and she was aware of keeping it back, holding it the way you hold a thing you are not ready to set down on someone else's table. "Three machines. One of each. That's the whole list."

[spare] "One scanner. One med unit. One controller." He said it back to her the way she had heard him say things back to himself, fixing the count in place. "And the man needs the controller."

[controlled] "He's off it now, in the daylight. He comes off for hours, sits up, breathes on his own. But he can't hold a night. After dark it goes back on him and stays on till morning." She watched him take the shape of it, three machines, one body, and she watched his eyes go a little distant in the way she had learned to read as the thing she both wanted from him and could not stand. He was already building it. "Eli. Before you go up into it. The scanner won't boot at midnight, it checks a license at startup and the license server stops answering, so it just won't come on. I can live without the scanner for a night, I've lived without worse. The cabinet I don't know, it might keep what's loaded and refuse new orders, it might lock. The controller is the one. It runs continuous and it phones home to confirm it's allowed to, and nobody who built it will tell me what it does when the phone stops being answered. So that's the order of it. The man first. Everything else second."

[spare] "Understood." He set the bag down. "Where's your server. You've got an old one. Every place like this has one, a closet, a room, something the last tenant left. I need to be near it and near power."

[weary] "Down the back." She went past him and he followed, and she felt the strangeness of it, his real footsteps behind her in the hall instead of his voice coming and going in a speaker, the link that afternoon dropping his words into holes, [remembered] tell me what stops, physically, [weary] and the holes, and his voice coming back. Now there were no holes. He was here, and that was supposed to be the thing that helped, and she made herself ask the question while she could still ask it walking, before he was bent over something and she had handed him the night.

[controlled] "Can you keep them running. The man, tonight. Yes or the truth, not the comfort."

[weary] He did not answer right away, which was its own answer, and she respected him for not filling it. They had been doing this for four years. She knew the shape of his honesty the way she knew the shape of his competence, and the two were the same thing in him, and tonight she needed one and dreaded the other.

[spare] "I can make the machines stop waiting on the company," he said. "That part I can do. When a server goes quiet, you stand in for it. You put up something local that answers in its place, and the machine stops asking the sky and starts asking the closet, and it runs. I can do that for a machine. The controller, maybe, if the closet cooperates and I have the hours."

[controlled] "And then it's fine."

[spare] "And then it runs." He said it carefully, and she heard him choosing the words the way he chose a connector, the right one, the one that went home. "Lena. The thing the company sent down that machine wasn't only permission to run. It was permission and the whole record behind it, what the doses should be, what the pressures should be, all the checking the machine did against the company's say-so. I can forge the permission. I can't forge what the permission was standing in front of. So the machine will run, and it'll tell you everything's fine, and it'll mean it. It just won't be the company saying so anymore. It'll be me, and a closet."

[weary] She had known this. He had reached for it that afternoon and stopped short, and she had filled in the rest herself in the hours since, in clinical words because the engineering was his and the body was hers. A machine she could not trust. A machine that would say a pressure was right because nothing was left to tell it the pressure was wrong. She had spent her whole career arguing that a visible human deciding was safer than a system deciding, and here was a system she would have to lean on tonight that had stopped being a system anyone could see, repaired by one man into something that would lie to her with total confidence and no malice at all.

[controlled] "How many can you do," she said. "Of the three. By midnight."

[spare] "I don't know yet. One, well. Maybe two. Not all three the way I'd want any of them." His jaw did not tighten, his face did not do any of the things people's faces did. He simply told her. "It's hours a machine, done right. I came to look before I said anything was impossible. So show me the room and let me look."

[weary] She showed him the room.

The door had swelled in its frame and she had to put her shoulder to it, and it gave with a sound, and the air that came out was the stale cold air of a year unopened, dust and old electronics and something faintly sweet, mouse maybe, the smell of a place the world had stopped coming into. The clinic's own server sat on a steel rack under a window painted shut, a squat machine with a single amber light, older than the scanner by a decade, underpowered, kept alive for the records and nothing harder. A work light hung off a hook. There was a stool. On the way down the hall they had passed the food board, and she saw his eyes catch on it for half a second, the cork panel by the back door where Dembélé pinned his columns, what the Vesely place had and what the Reyes place needed and who would carry which to whom, and then Eli's eyes came off it because it was not a machine and went to the rack.

He crouched in front of the old server without being asked, the way he crouched in front of everything, and she watched his hands go to it, fast and certain, fluent in a language she did not speak, [guarded] and she felt the old pull, the relief of competence, and under it the old cold thing, the not-trusting-where-the-competence-went. He could save the man. He could also be the man from Asterion, from the labs that had hollowed out half the city's work, and she knew most of that history, and she had decided years ago not to absolve it and not to reduce him to it, and tonight she did not have the room to do either. Tonight she only needed his hands.

[spare] "This'll hold a light and a board and not much," he said, to the machine, not to her. "I'll have to be careful what I draw. Your power's already cut, you're on the low tier, no emergency restore. If the grid sags tonight and the batteries take it, I lose whatever I've got going and the room goes dark with the rest of you."

[controlled] "I know about the tier." Everyone knew about the tier. The same clean type had dropped the whole neighborhood at noon, the clinic with it, no more emergencies, only standard maintenance windows whenever those came. "If the power goes, the controller's on its own backup for a while. Then it isn't."

[spare] "Then I'll be quick," he said, which was not a promise, and they both knew it was not, and neither of them dressed it as one.

[weary] She stood in the doorway a moment longer. He was already gone into it, the bag open, a coil of cable out, the work light snapped on and throwing his bent shadow long across the painted-shut window. There was nothing for her here. This was his to do and she could not do it and she could not even watch it usefully, and the watching might cost him the concentration that was the only thing in this room worth anything. The decisive work of the night was going to happen three feet from where she stood, and she could not lift a finger of it.

She pulled the door most of the way closed on him, on the dust and the single light and the bowed shoulders, and left it a hand's width open, and turned back down the hall toward the people, toward the part that was hers.

---

[weary] The ward was quiet in the way a ward is quiet, which is not silence. Six beds, four of them filled, the small machine-sounds and body-sounds of people sleeping or pretending to. The afternoon stood in the high windows still, gone thin and yellow, the kind of light that does not last. Lena came into it and let it close around her and started where she always started, the first bed, and moved.

The Reyes man in the second bed had a leg that should have had a particular dressing, a smart foam that read the wound and wicked and reported, and the smart foam came from a supplier that had stopped supplying, and so she had a tin of plain gauze and a salve a woman three streets over cooked from something her grandmother had cooked, and Lena had decided the salve worked, or worked enough, by watching the leg and not by reading a report off a foam that knew more than she did and was not here. She changed it by hand under the work light, the old slow way, and the man watched her do it with the patience of someone who had learned that the slow way was the only way left and was not the worst thing.

[dry] "You'd have had me scanned and stitched and out the door in an hour, time was," he said.

[weary] "Time was," she agreed. The leg was clean. The leg was healing. The scanner could have told her in a minute what her eyes told her in five, and the scanner, tonight and after tonight, would tell her nothing, and her eyes would have to be enough, and they were, this time, on this leg. Accurate diagnosis does not matter if the treatment cannot be obtained. She had read that somewhere once, in some bulletin from when there were still bulletins, and had thought it a cruelty and now understood it as a simple description of where she lived. The machine that could be right and could not run was no better to her tonight than no machine at all, and worse in one way, because it sat there being right and unreachable and made her feel the size of what she had lost.

In the next bed was Mrs. Diallo, who had come in that afternoon with a cough that was not a cough, that was the kind of thing a chest holds for months before it becomes the thing it was always going to be, and who had not come in months ago when it was small because months ago she had still believed, in the old part of her that had not caught up with the world, that coming in cost money she did not have.

[rueful] "I should have come before," she said, before Lena had asked anything, the apology already loaded the way the eggs had been loaded. "I kept thinking. I kept thinking what it would run me. Old foolishness. My husband used to say I'd let a thing kill me before I'd let it bill me." She tried to laugh and the laugh turned into the cough.

[controlled] "There's no bill," Lena said. "There hasn't been a bill here in two years. There's nobody to send it to."

[plain] "I know it. I knew it. It doesn't go out of you, though, the counting. You spend fifty years afraid of the cost of a doctor and then one day the cost is gone and you're still afraid, like a dog that flinches at a hand that's only ever fed it." Mrs. Diallo looked at her with eyes that were tired and clear. "So now I'm here too late instead of broke. That's the trade I made with myself. Cheaper, anyway."

[weary] It was not a thing Lena could fix, the months. The insurer that would once have argued with Mrs. Diallo about whether her chest was worth a scan had pulled out of the whole district two winters ago in a letter that thanked her for her loyalty, and after that there had been a few months of a public plan, and then the public plan had been folded into a state thing, and the state thing had been quietly defunded the way the buses had been defunded, no announcement, just a number that stopped being answered, and somewhere in there the fear of the bill had outlived every institution that had ever sent one. The fear was the last thing left standing of the whole apparatus. A woman had stayed home for months and let a small thing grow large out of a terror of a cost that no longer existed, because the world had taken away the bill and left her the dread of it, which was the worse half to keep.

[controlled] "You're here now," Lena said, which was the honest fraction. She did not say the rest, what the scan she could not run would have found, what the months had probably already decided. The withheld part sat in her throat like something swallowed wrong, and she told herself the truth would do this frightened woman no good at midnight in a cold ward and that morning was time enough, and was not sure she believed herself, and did it anyway. "We'll look at you properly tomorrow. Rest tonight. You're warm here, that's something."

[plain] "It's something," Mrs. Diallo said, and let her keep the rest back, the way patients did, both of them in on it.

[weary] At the ward's end old Dembélé was sitting up in the spare bed where he was not a patient, where he came some evenings because his own house had gone too cold and the clinic had not yet, and he had his board-keeper's notebook open on his knees, working the columns by the work lamp she had carried down to him, because the afternoon had drained out of the windows while she moved bed to bed and the dusk was in the glass now, going blue, going to nothing. Forty years he had run logistics for a freight network, routing container ships and trucks and the long autonomous trains across half a continent, a man who had once moved the contents of the world from where it was made to where it was wanted, and now he sat in a borrowed bed and matched a crate of the Vesely place's brown eggs against the Reyes family's need for them and worked out who walked which way carrying what, the whole supply chain of three streets held in a paper notebook on an old man's knees.

[satisfied] "The Okonkwos paid you in my eggs," he said, not looking up, pleased. "I sent those eggs across. Vesely to Okonkwo Wednesday, against a thing they owed the Veselys from August. Now they've come to you. Three moves, those eggs." He tapped the notebook. "I can still see a chain when it's in front of me. Used to be ten thousand of them at once, on a screen the size of a wall. Now it's eggs. But it's the same shape, you know. It's exactly the same shape. A thing's here, a thing's wanted there, somebody has to know the road between." He said it with a satisfaction that did not hide the other thing under it, the wall-sized screen, the ten thousand chains, the career that had ended in a notebook. Both were in his voice and he did not try to keep either out. "You take the eggs, doctor. They've earned their trip."

[gentle] "I took them." She put her hand on his shoulder a moment, the bone of it close under the cardigan. He was warm. The bed was warm. [weary] The notebook was full of careful columns in a hand that had been trained on a wall-sized screen, and she could not have said, standing there, whether the room was a kindness or an indictment, the man cared for among neighbors or the man reduced to eggs, and she had stopped trying to decide. It was both. It had been both all day, in every bed. The new world that paid her in eggs and salve and a neighbor's grandmother's recipe was warmer than the one that had paid her in a number from an office, and it was a downgrade no one had chosen, a smaller life dressed up in good neighbors. She put one down and the other did not get any lighter.

She left Dembélé to his columns and went the length of the ward and down the hall to the last room, to the man on the controller, because she had been keeping him for last the way you keep the hardest thing for last, and the dark had come down beyond the windows at last, the long afternoon used up bed by bed and the evening settled in behind it, and there was no putting him off any longer.

Mr. Adeyemi was awake.

[thin] "You've been all the way round," he said. His voice came thin around the controller's work, the cycle pushing under his words. "I heard you. You go the same way every night, I know your feet. You saved me for last."

[weary] "I save the talkers for last," she said, and sat down on the edge of the chair beside the bed, which she did not usually do, and he noticed that she did it.

[plain] "So it's true then. The machines." He had heard. Everyone had heard. There were no secrets in a ward at night. "Tomas wouldn't say straight. He's a good boy, he can't lie worth anything, his face does it for him."

[weary] She could move him. She had turned it over all day, in the corner of her mind that never stopped. There was a clinic two towns over that might still be authorized, that might still be running its own controllers come morning, and she could put him in a neighbor's electric car on its borrowed charge and send him across the dark to a place that might take him and might ask him for papers and might already be as far gone as her own. Or she could keep him here, on a machine that might run all night and might lie to her at one minute past midnight and might simply stop, with Eli three feet down the hall trying to make a closet stand in for a company, and herself in this chair, awake, watching the card and not the dead light. There was no safe one. There was no right one to find, only one to choose and stand inside and carry, whatever the night decided to hand her.

[controlled] "I'm keeping you here," she said. "On the machine. With me in this chair. I want to tell you it's the safe choice and it isn't. There's a man down the hall working on it. He's good. He may make it hold. I'm not going to promise you it holds, because I don't know what happens to it at midnight and neither does he and neither does anybody, and you deserve better than a promise I'd be making up. But you can't hold the night without it, and the road to anywhere else is colder and longer and no surer. So you're here. And I'm here. That's the whole of what I've got, and I'm not dressing it up."
