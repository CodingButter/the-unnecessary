## Voice Direction

This is not spoken. It is direction for the reader, human or synthetic.

### Overall direction

Grounded and austere. A single narrator carries the whole chapter in Lena Okafor's close register: a forty-six-year-old doctor running an abandoned clinic against a deadline she did not set, holding a banked anger that she will not let become panic because panic stops moving patients. The default sound is weary and controlled, intelligent, exact, with the anger sealed underneath the precision rather than displayed on top of it. The horror of this chapter is administrative and quiet, never loud. Nothing should sound theatrical. Where the prose reaches its hardest truths, the answer is more stillness, not more volume. The voice tightens slowly across the chapter and arrives, in the fourth scene, at an anger that has changed shape and finally has no face to aim itself at. Bookend the chapter with deliberate weight: the opening image of the card taped over the dead light, and the closing image of the cycle pushing and releasing in the dark, should be the two most measured passages in the reading.

### Per-register approach for this chapter

- **Lena's narration (default, weary banked-anger control).** Most of the chapter. Calm, precise, clinical when she is counting; the feeling is held a half-step below the words. Tag this `[weary]` and `[controlled]` and let it ride; do not re-tag every paragraph. Only mark where the register genuinely shifts, into tenderness at a bedside, into colder precision when she does triage arithmetic, into the slow anger-turn of scene four. The intelligence is always working; she is never sentimental about it.

- **The corporate notice (flat, administrative).** When the discontinuation sentence is read, it must read `[flat]`, even, with no human voice inside it, because the prose says exactly that: a sentence built so that no human voice could be heard inside it. The horror is the calm. Do not editorialize it with the voice; let the flatness do the work, then return to Lena's register.

- **Eli's spoken lines (spare, working).** Plain, dry, low. A man reading a room and a machine, choosing words the way he chooses a connector. Tag him `[spare]` or `[plain]` and keep him level; his honesty and his competence are the same flat thing in him. He does not perform reluctance even when he delivers hard news; he simply tells her. No warmth manufactured, no chill manufactured.

- **The patients and staff (their own plain registers).** Each gets one plain human register, not a caricature. Tomas: steady, matter-of-fact, the weather-reporting tone the prose names. The Reyes man: dry, accepting. Mrs. Diallo: tired and clear, rueful, ashamed without self-pity. Dembélé: warm, satisfied, with the loss audible under the satisfaction and not hidden. Mr. Adeyemi: thin around the machine's work, wry, frightened underneath the joke. Tag each at first line and let it hold through their speech.

### Pacing philosophy

Let ordinary punctuation and the tags carry the pace. Ellipses are rare, a handful across the chapter, reserved for a genuine intended beat and always placed after existing punctuation, never sprinkled inside clauses. The long descriptive sentences (the card, the scanner, the egg economy, the final image) are meant to unspool slowly and evenly, not to be chopped. The dialogue is faster and plainer. Do not rush the bedside scenes. Do not slow the corporate sentence into menace; keep it administrative.

### Intensity arc

The chapter tightens. Scene one is survey and dread held flat. Scene two brings Eli and the hard engineering truth, delivered level. Scene three is the human tour, the warmest and most varied register, with the loss banked under the warmth. Scene four is the turn: the anger that had a face in the afternoon now has none, and the reading should narrow and steady and harden through the two anger paragraphs, then settle into the final bedside image, which is the quietest and most deliberate passage in the chapter. The peak is not a raised voice; it is the cold clarity of "There was no one to be angry at for this." Give that, and the last paragraph's breathing machine, the most deliberate weight.

## Performance Script

[measured] Chapter Two. ... The Last Supported Day.

---

[weary] The card was what Lena looked for first when she came into the room now, before the man in the bed, before the numbers.

It was an index card, white going soft at the corners, taped to the housing of the respiratory controller over the place where a small status light used to burn. The light had meant the machine was certified, current, blessed by the company that built it. The company was gone. The light was dead. So she had written what she needed to know on a card and taped it where the light had been. Cycle good. Last clean read, and a time. Pressures, in her own hand. The card told her what the light no longer could: the machine had cycled clean the last time it ran, with no promise of the next.

She put her hand flat on the casing.

[slowly] [controlled] The casing was cool under her hand, and still, the machine idle on its stand through the daylight while the man in the bed did his own breathing. Mr. Adeyemi was awake. Sixty-one years old, narrow under the blanket, propped up, managing his own slow lungs for as long as the afternoon would let him. In the day he came off it for hours at a stretch, sat up, complained about the food, told her about a daughter in a protected zone who could not get out to see him and would not have been let back in if she had. At night it went the other way. His chest could not hold the count alone, and the machine took it up, a small even push and release for lungs that would not make it through the dark. The daughter went quiet in him then, along with everything else, and Lena watched the card. That was tonight, still ahead of them both. For now the casing stayed cool, the man stayed talking, and the controller waited with her for the dark.

She made herself take her hand off the casing and look at her phone.

[weary] Nine and a half hours. The notice had given a time, as they always gave a time, and the time was 23:59, their polite way of saying midnight without writing the word, set in the same clean type as the cellular and power notices, a sentence built so that no human voice could be heard inside it. [flat] Remote authentication for the listed devices will be discontinued effective end of day, October the third. [weary] She had read it to Eli that afternoon over a link that dropped every third word. She had heard him on the other end go into the place he went, the bench in his head, and told him to come down out of it and stay with the body. He had said he would come. He was coming. That changed the arithmetic, but not as much as she needed, and she would not let herself pretend otherwise until she had counted the rest of what she had.

She went to count it.

[controlled] The clinic was three patient rooms, a ward of six beds, an exam room, the front room with its bench and its counter, and a back hall that ran past a supply closet to a door she had not opened in a year. She had taken it over from a dentist who had taken it over from an insurance office, and the bones of all three were still in it if you looked, the reception window, the little frosted partitions, the carpet glue under the new flooring. At this hour the place ran on daylight. She kept the electric lights off to spare the batteries, and the rooms took what the afternoon gave them through the windows, a thin used-up light that thinned further the deeper in you went and did not reach the inner rooms at all. The cold had come into the building as it came into everything now that warmth was rationed out to them, settling into the floor and rising through the soles of her shoes.

In the second room she stopped at the diagnostic scanner.

It stood against the wall, white and unmarked and perfect, a clean curved shell with a bed that slid into it, and it was the best doctor in the building and they both knew it. She had watched it find a tumor in a woman's pancreas that Lena's own hands and eyes and forty-six years would have missed for another two months, and those two months would have been the difference. It was better than her at everything but the one task that mattered tonight, which was running. She woke its panel. It lit and asked her, in its mild way, to wait while it confirmed its license, and the little wheel turned while it reached two thousand miles toward a server to be told it was allowed to know what it knew. Tonight the server answered. The wheel stopped. The machine said it was ready. At midnight the server would stop answering. The next time someone pressed this panel the wheel would turn and turn and never stop, and the machine would sit here, white and unmarked and perfect, knowing everything and permitted to say none of it.

She put it to sleep before it could finish offering to scan her.

In the same room, the medication-management unit, a gray cabinet the size of a small refrigerator with a screen and a row of locked drawers. It held the doses, decided how much and how often, would not let her hand more morphine than a body should have because it knew the body and she, in a tired hour, might forget. She loved it for that on her good days and resented it on the rest. It had the same heart as the scanner and the controller, the same need to phone a company to be allowed to do its work, and the company had written the same sentence about it. After midnight it might keep the schedules already loaded and refuse to take a new one. It might lock the drawers and keep the keys. She did not know which. No one knew which. The not-knowing was the whole of it: she was being asked to plan a night around three machines whose behavior after one specific minute no living person could tell her.

A floorboard took weight in the doorway and she did not turn around.

[plain] "Priya wants to know about the Caldwell girl," Tomas said. He was the only other nurse she fully trusted tonight, thirty, steady, one she had not had to teach to be afraid quietly. "Whether we keep her or send her home. And the Okonkwos brought their thing. It's on the counter."

[weary] "What thing."

[plain] "Eggs," Tomas said. "Dozen, looks like. For the father's visit Tuesday. Mrs. Okonkwo said to tell you they're from the Vesely place, the brown ones, she said you'd want the brown ones." He said it without weight, the way you'd report the weather, because it was the weather. "I said thank you. I didn't know what else to do with a fee."

[weary] There was nothing to enter the eggs into. There had been a system once, a screen at the counter that took a card and sent a number to an office that sent a bill to a company that sent the company's money back. The screen was still there, dark. The office was gone. The company had folded into another company that had itself been discontinued, and so a dozen brown eggs sat on the counter where the screen used to be, the entire economy now, start to finish, the Vesely place to the Okonkwos to her. "The Caldwell girl. What's her fever doing."

[plain] "Down. Hundred even, last hour."

[controlled] "Then she's mine, not the machine's. Keep her. I'm not sending a child into the cold on a night I can't promise her the scanner if she spikes." The words came out and she caught their shape on the way, the scanner she could not promise. "Tell Priya to watch her by hand. Temperature every two hours, the old way, write it down. If she goes up, come get me, don't go to the cabinet, come get me."

[plain] "Because the cabinet might lock," Tomas said. Not a question. He had read the same notice on her face all afternoon.

[controlled] "Because I want to be the one deciding the dose tonight," Lena said. "Not the cabinet, and not whatever the cabinet turns into at midnight."

[weary] He went. She stood a moment in the dim room with the two clean machines reading their perfect numbers at no one, and did the arithmetic she hated, the kind that came out in people. Mr. Adeyemi on the controller, a whole night. The Caldwell child, fever falling, hers by hand. Two on the ward who needed nothing the machines gave. A man coming in the morning who would need the scanner and would have to be told to drive to a clinic two towns over that might or might not still have one running, that might or might not ask him for papers he did not have. Equipment she could not trust, judgment she could. To sort the ones who could wait from the ones who could not, she had to know which was which, and the machines that were supposed to tell her had been told to stop at a minute she had not picked.

She caught herself standing too long.

She drifted back to the controller without deciding to, to the cool idle housing and her flat hand on it and nothing moving under her palm, as if presence were a plan. It was not. A hand on a casing authorized nothing. She made herself move. Standing still was what you did when there was nothing left to do, and there was a great deal left to do, and she was not at the end of it yet.

She was at the counter, looking at the eggs without seeing them, when the front door went and a cold draft reached her down the hall. A voice she had only ever heard through a thinning link said her name in a room instead of out of a speaker, low and dry and whole.

She left the eggs and went to meet him.

---

[weary] Eli stood inside the door with the cold still on him and a canvas bag in one hand, the three o'clock light gone flat and gray in the door glass behind him, and he looked, as he always did, like a man who had walked through the neighborhood reading it. He was taller than she let herself remember between times, gray come further into the close-cut hair than a year ago. He did not say hello. It was the one habit they shared in a doorway. He looked past her shoulder down the hall toward the rooms, already counting.

[spare] "Where are they," he said.

[controlled] "Two in that room." She pointed. "Scanner and the cabinet, the medication unit. The controller's down the end, in with the man." She did not say his name. She had not said it to Eli over the link that afternoon, and she did not say it now. She kept it back, a name she was not ready to set down on someone else's table. "Three machines. One of each. That's the whole list."

[spare] "One scanner. One med unit. One controller." He said it back to her and then to himself, fixing the count in place. "And the man needs the controller."

[controlled] "He's off it now, in the daylight. He comes off for hours, sits up, breathes on his own. But he can't hold a night. After dark it goes back on him and stays on till morning." She watched him take the shape of it, three machines, one body, his eyes going a little distant in what she had learned to read as the part of him she both wanted and could not stand. He was already building it. "Eli. Before you go up into it. The scanner won't boot at midnight, it checks a license at startup and the license server stops answering, so it just won't come on. I can live without the scanner for a night, I've lived without worse. The cabinet I don't know, it might keep what's loaded and refuse new orders, it might lock. The controller is the one. It runs continuous and it phones home to confirm it's allowed to, and nobody who built it will tell me what it does when the phone stops being answered. So that's the order of it. The man first. Everything else second."

[spare] "Understood." He set the bag down. "Where's your server. You've got an old one. Every place like this has one, a closet, a room, something the last tenant left. I need to be near it and near power."

[weary] "Down the back." She went past him and he followed, and she felt the strangeness of it, his real footsteps behind her in the hall instead of his voice coming and going in a speaker, the link that afternoon dropping his words into holes, [remembered] tell me what stops, physically, [weary] and the holes, and his voice coming back. Now there were no holes. He was here, which was supposed to help. She made herself ask the question while she could still ask it walking, before he was bent over the rack and she had handed him the night.

[controlled] "Can you keep them running. The man, tonight. Yes or the truth, not the comfort."

[weary] He did not answer right away, which was its own answer, and she respected him for not filling it. They had been doing this for four years. His honesty and his competence had the same shape in him, the same root, and tonight she needed the one and dreaded the other.

[spare] "I can make the machines stop waiting on the company," he said. "That part I can do. When a server goes quiet, you stand in for it. You put up something local that answers in its place, and the machine stops asking the sky and starts asking the closet, and it runs. I can do that for a machine. The controller, maybe, if the closet cooperates and I have the hours."

[controlled] "And then it's fine."

[spare] "And then it runs." He said it carefully, and she heard him choosing the words as carefully as he chose a connector, the right one, the one that went home. "Lena. The thing the company sent down that machine wasn't only permission to run. It was permission and the whole record behind it, what the doses should be, what the pressures should be, all the checking the machine did against the company's say-so. I can forge the permission. I can't forge what the permission was standing in front of. So the machine will run, and it'll tell you everything's fine, and it'll mean it. It just won't be the company saying so anymore. It'll be me, and a closet."

[weary] She had known this. He had reached for it that afternoon and stopped short, and she had filled in the rest herself in the hours since, in clinical words because the engineering was his and the body was hers. A machine she could not trust. A machine that would say a pressure was right because nothing was left to tell it the pressure was wrong. She had spent her whole career arguing that a visible human deciding was safer than an automated one. Now she would have to lean on a system no one could see anymore, repaired by one man into something that would lie to her with total confidence and no malice at all.

[controlled] "How many can you do," she said. "Of the three. By midnight."

[spare] "I don't know yet. One, well. Maybe two. Not all three the way I'd want any of them." His jaw did not tighten. His face did none of what other people's faces did. He simply told her. "It's hours a machine, done right. I came to look before I said anything was impossible. So show me the room and let me look."

[weary] She showed him the room.

The door had swelled in its frame and she had to put her shoulder to it. It gave with a sound, and the air that came out was the stale cold of a year unopened, dust and old electronics and something faintly sweet, mouse maybe, the smell of a place the world had stopped coming into. The clinic's own server sat on a steel rack under a window painted shut, a squat machine with a single amber light, older than the scanner by a decade, underpowered, kept alive for the records and nothing harder. A work light hung off a hook. There was a stool. Down the hall they had passed the food board, and his eyes had caught on it for half a second, the cork panel by the back door where Dembélé pinned his columns, what the Vesely place had and what the Reyes place needed and who would carry which to whom. Then his eyes came off it, because it was not a machine, and went to the rack.

He crouched in front of the old server without being asked, as he crouched in front of everything, and his hands went to it, fast and certain, fluent in a language she did not speak. [guarded] She felt the old pull, the relief of competence, and under it the old cold mistrust of where the competence went. He could save the man. He could also be the man from Asterion, from the labs that had hollowed out half the city's work. She knew most of that history. She had decided years ago not to absolve it and not to reduce him to it, and tonight she had room for neither. Tonight she only needed his hands.

[spare] "This'll hold a light and a board and not much," he said, to the machine, not to her. "I'll have to be careful what I draw. Your power's already cut, you're on the low tier, no emergency restore. If the grid sags tonight and the batteries take it, I lose whatever I've got going and the room goes dark with the rest of you."

[controlled] "I know about the tier." Everyone knew about the tier. The same clean type had dropped the whole neighborhood at noon, the clinic with it, no more emergencies, only standard maintenance windows whenever those came. "If the power goes, the controller's on its own backup for a while. Then it isn't."

[spare] "Then I'll be quick," he said, which was not a promise. They both knew it, and neither of them dressed it as one.

[weary] She stood in the doorway a moment longer. He had already gone into it, the bag open, a coil of cable out, the work light snapped on and throwing his bent shadow long across the painted-shut window. There was nothing for her here. The work was his, and she could not do it, could not even watch it usefully; her watching might cost him the concentration that was all this room had worth anything. The decisive work of the night would happen three feet from where she stood, and she could not lift a finger of it.

She pulled the door most of the way closed on him, on the dust and the single light and the bowed shoulders, left it a hand's width open, and turned back down the hall toward the people, toward the part that was hers.

---

[weary] The ward was quiet, which in a ward never means silence. Six beds, four of them filled, the small machine-sounds and body-sounds of people sleeping or pretending to. The afternoon stood in the high windows still, gone thin and yellow, the kind of light that does not last. Lena came into it, let it close around her, and started where she always started, at the first bed.

The Reyes man in the second bed had a leg that should have had a smart foam, the kind that read the wound and wicked and reported. The foam came from a supplier that had stopped supplying. So she had a tin of plain gauze and a salve a woman three streets over cooked from something her grandmother had cooked, and she had decided the salve worked, or worked well enough, by watching the leg rather than reading a report off a dressing that knew more than she did and was not here. She changed it by hand under the work light, the old slow way, and the man watched her with the patience of someone who had learned the slow way was the only way left, and not the worst of them.

[dry] "You'd have had me scanned and stitched and out the door in an hour, time was," he said.

[weary] "Time was," she agreed. The leg was clean. The leg was healing. The scanner could have told her in a minute what her eyes told her in five; tonight and after tonight it would tell her nothing, and her eyes would have to be enough. They were, this time, on this leg. Accurate diagnosis does not matter if the treatment cannot be obtained. She had read that somewhere once, in some bulletin from when there were still bulletins, and thought it a cruelty, and understood it now as a plain description of where she lived. A machine that could be right and could not run was no better tonight than no machine at all, and worse in one way: it sat there being right and out of reach and made her feel the size of what she had lost.

In the next bed was Mrs. Diallo, who had come in that afternoon with a cough that was not really a cough, the kind a chest holds for months before it becomes what it was always going to be. She had not come months ago, when it was small, because months ago she had still believed, in the old part of her that had not caught up with the world, that coming in cost money she did not have.

[rueful] "I should have come before," she said, before Lena had asked anything, the apology already loaded, like the eggs. "I kept thinking. I kept thinking what it would run me. Old foolishness. My husband used to say I'd let a thing kill me before I'd let it bill me." She tried to laugh and the laugh turned into the cough.

[controlled] "There's no bill," Lena said. "There hasn't been a bill here in two years. There's nobody to send it to."

[plain] "I know it. I knew it. It doesn't go out of you, though, the counting. You spend fifty years afraid of the cost of a doctor and then one day the cost is gone and you're still afraid, like a dog that flinches at a hand that's only ever fed it." Mrs. Diallo looked at her with eyes that were tired and clear. "So now I'm here too late instead of broke. That's the trade I made with myself. Cheaper, anyway."

[weary] The months were not something Lena could fix. The insurer that would once have argued with Mrs. Diallo about whether her chest was worth a scan had pulled out of the whole district two winters ago in a letter that thanked her for her loyalty. Everything after it had folded just as quietly, until there was no one left to send a bill to and only the old fear of one still standing.

[controlled] "You're here now," Lena said, which was the honest fraction. She did not say the rest, what the scan she could not run would have found, what the months had probably already decided. The withheld part sat in her throat like something swallowed wrong. She told herself the truth would do this frightened woman no good at midnight in a cold ward, that morning was time enough. She was not sure she believed it, and did it anyway. "We'll look at you properly tomorrow. Rest tonight. You're warm here, that's something."

[plain] "It's something," Mrs. Diallo said, and let her keep the rest back, as patients did, both of them in on it.

[weary] At the ward's end old Dembélé sat up in the spare bed where he was not a patient. He came some evenings because his own house had gone too cold and the clinic had not yet, and he had his board-keeper's notebook open on his knees, working the columns by the work lamp she had carried down to him. The afternoon had drained out of the windows while she moved bed to bed; the dusk was in the glass now, going blue, going to nothing. Forty years he had run logistics for a freight network, routing container ships and trucks and the long autonomous trains across half a continent, a man who had once moved the contents of the world from where it was made to where it was wanted. Now he sat in a borrowed bed and matched a crate of the Vesely place's brown eggs against the Reyes family's need for them, working out who carried what to whom, the whole supply chain of three streets held in a paper notebook on an old man's knees.

[satisfied] "The Okonkwos paid you in my eggs," he said, not looking up, pleased. "I sent those eggs across. Vesely to Okonkwo Wednesday, against a thing they owed the Veselys from August. Now they've come to you. Three moves, those eggs." He tapped the notebook. "I can still see a chain when it's in front of me. Used to be ten thousand of them at once, on a screen the size of a wall. Now it's eggs. But it's the same shape, you know. It's exactly the same shape. A thing's here, a thing's wanted there, somebody has to know the road between." He said it with a satisfaction that did not hide what lay under it, the wall-sized screen, the ten thousand chains, the career that had ended in a notebook. Both were in his voice, and he did not try to keep either out. "You take the eggs, doctor. They've earned their trip."

[gentle] "I took them." She put her hand on his shoulder a moment, the bone close under the cardigan. [weary] The notebook lay open on his knees, careful columns in a hand that had been trained on a wall-sized screen. A kindness and an indictment in the same bed, and she had stopped trying to tell them apart. He was warm. The bed was warm. She left her hand where it was.

She left Dembélé to his columns and went the length of the ward to the last room, to the man on the controller. She had kept him for last as she kept the hardest call for last. The dark had come down beyond the windows now, the long afternoon used up bed by bed, the evening settled in behind it, and there was no putting him off any longer.

Mr. Adeyemi was awake.

[thin] "You've been all the way round," he said. His voice came thin around the controller's work, the cycle pushing under his words. "I heard you. You go the same way every night, I know your feet. You saved me for last."

[weary] "I save the talkers for last," she said, and sat down on the edge of the chair beside the bed, which she did not usually do, and he noticed that she did it.

[plain] "So it's true then. The machines." He had heard. Everyone had heard. There were no secrets in a ward at night. "Tomas wouldn't say straight. He's a good boy, he can't lie worth anything, his face does it for him."

[weary] She could move him. She had turned it over all day, in the corner of her mind that never stopped. There was a clinic two towns over that might still be authorized, that might still be running its own controllers come morning, and she could put him in a neighbor's electric car on its borrowed charge and send him across the dark to a place that might take him and might ask him for papers and might already be as far gone as her own. Or she could keep him here, on a machine that might run all night and might lie to her at one minute past midnight and might simply stop, with Eli three feet down the hall trying to make a closet stand in for a company, and herself in this chair, awake, watching the card and not the dead light. There was no safe one. There was no right one to find, only one to choose and stand inside and carry, whatever the night decided to hand her.

[controlled] "I'm keeping you here," she said. "On the machine. With me in this chair. I want to tell you it's the safe choice and it isn't. There's a man down the hall working on it. He's good. He may make it hold. I'm not going to promise you it holds, because I don't know what happens to it at midnight and neither does he and neither does anybody, and you deserve better than a promise I'd be making up. But you can't hold the night without it, and the road to anywhere else is colder and longer and no surer. So you're here. And I'm here. That's the whole of what I've got, and I'm not dressing it up."

[weary] He was quiet a moment, the cycle going under him.

[plain] "My mother had a doctor used to tell her everything was going to be fine," he said. "Every time. Right up until the time it wasn't, and then he still said it." A small breath, the machine's and his together. "You're not restful, you know that? You're a terrible comfort." But his hand had come out from under the blanket while he said it and found the edge of her sleeve, held it the way a man holds a railing on a stair he is not sure of, and she let him and did not move her arm.

[controlled] "I know," she said. "Sleep if you can. I'll be right here. If anything changes I'll know before you do."

[plain] "That's not restful either."

[weary] "No," she agreed. "It isn't."

She put her hand on the warm casing of the controller, the card above her fingers, the cycle steady under her palm. She watched the man's eyes close and listened to the machine breathe for him in the dark. Everything her hands and her judgment could do was done, and the rest of the night was no longer hers.

---

[weary] She did one more pass because she could not not do it.

There was no medicine left in any move she had. The Caldwell girl's fever held at a hundred, watched by hand, written down. Mrs. Diallo slept, or lay still in the dark with her dread; either way there was nothing tonight. The Reyes leg was dressed. Dembélé had finally put down the notebook and gone under the blanket. Tomas sat at the front with the lights low, awake, dependable, watching the door no one would come through. She checked the cabinet's screen one more time.
