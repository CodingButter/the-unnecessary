## Medium Severity

| Category | Location | Issue | Suggestion | Severity |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Tone | `[tender] One of the boys said morning, Mr. Rook, ...` | The tag `[tender]` feels too warm and emotionally open for Eli's character as established in the direction ("austere, weary, and dry"). It breaks the carefully maintained restraint. | Replace `[tender]` with a more subdued tag like `[softened]` or simply `[quiet]` to show a momentary shift without compromising his core persona. | Medium |
| Density | `[weary] He had forgotten the man was there. [measured] Older, ... sixty maybe, ... on the stool by the window with a tablet Eli had already looked at and set aside, ... an account locked behind an identity check that ran through a bank that had stopped recognizing the neighborhood as a place that banked. [quiet] He had been watching Eli work with the attention people gave to someone doing something they could not do themselves.` | This is a long, complex paragraph of interiority and observation with only three broad tags. The description of the tablet's problem is purely procedural and could be read more neutrally than the `[measured]` tag covering it. | Add a `[flat]` tag before "an account locked behind an identity check..." to separate the neutral technical description from Eli's more personal observation of the man watching him. | Medium |

## Low Severity

| Category | Location | Issue | Suggestion | Severity |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Register | `[flat] "Everybody's house thinks something now," Marisol said as he turned, ... almost smiling, ...` | The `[flat]` tag doesn't capture the nuance of the prose "almost smiling." Marisol's line has a dark, wry humor that a purely flat reading would miss, making her sound defeated rather than resilient. | Change `[flat]` to `[dryly]` or `[wryly]` to better reflect the gallows humor indicated by the prose. | Low |
| Tag-craft | `[tense] [clipped] "I'm going to read you something."` | While v3 can handle multiple tags, `clipped` inherently contains the quality of tension. Using both is slightly redundant and can diffuse the focus of the direction. | Simplify to just `[clipped]`. It's a stronger, more specific direction that implies the tension without needing to state it separately. | Low |
| Pacing | `[flat] He fixed it. ... He took no money for it. ... The boy left.` | These are three short, declarative, and consequential actions. The single `[flat]` tag and even pacing make them feel equivalent, but the final action ("The boy left") has a finality that could be emphasized. | Add a `[slowly]` or `[quiet]` tag before "The boy left" to give the moment a slightly heavier, more conclusive weight. | Low |
| Tone | `[unsettled] But the rhyme would not leave him alone.` | `[unsettled]` is a good tag, but it appears multiple times in the back half of the chapter. The *first* appearance of this dawning, unwelcome realization is a key turning point for Eli's internal state. | To mark this moment more distinctly, consider a more specific tag for its first use, like `[dawning]` or `[troubled]`, before reverting to `[unsettled]` for its later recurrences. | Low |

## Overall

This is an exceptionally strong and performance-ready script. Its word-for-word fidelity to the manuscript is perfect, which is the most critical requirement. The direction is dense, thoughtful, and shows a clear understanding of the chapter's emotional arc and thematic weight. The three primary registers are well-defined and distinct, and the two major emotional peaks are given the deliberate, careful pacing they require. The tag craft is professional and well-suited to the ElevenLabs v3 engine. The suggestions above are minor refinements aimed at sharpening an already excellent piece of direction, primarily by fine-tuning a few tag choices to more precisely match the austere tone of the prose. This script is in excellent shape.
